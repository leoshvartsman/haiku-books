There is a particular kind of attention that notices the freezer light in a corner store at two in the morning, that watches a cleaner push his cart through an empty terminal, that sees in a split fig not beauty or decay but simply wasps doing their one job. The haiku in this collection grew from that attention — unhurried, undeceived, and quietly committed to the world as it actually presents ...

cedar splits in rain—

the grain opens like a mouth

saying nothing back

low tide at Burrard—

the mud remembers each boot

then forgets by noon

corner store hum at

two a.m., the freezer light

colder than outside

the passport photo

younger face, same nervous mouth—

gate 14 calls

before the gate fills

one cleaner pushes his cart

through all that silence

4 a.m. window:

the tarmac holds its own rain—

no departure yet

the fig’s dark interior

split open on the ground, wasps

doing their one job

stone in afternoon

is a different animal

than stone in the morning

the hose pulses once

then settles to a thin stream

I accept this too

tram stop Flinders Lane

the rain makes private the crowd

everyone looks down

I cook with one knife

because I own one knife —

the meal is enough

yaqeen written twice

once in the margin in blue

once again in red

the pen grip roughened

where Arabic curves back left—

my wrist turns with it

the callus answers

before I know the question—

pen already down

market Arabic

bleeding into my Italian—

the vendor nods once

travertine in rain

darkens like cedar—I know

that trick, different stone

the pigeon’s red feet

grip the railing with the ease

of owning nothing

stairwell, third landing—

someone has taped a postcard

of the sea up there
