What the Body Carries

Casey Kim
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
Casey Kim has lived in four countries across nine decades, and this book is what that living left behind — not a memoir, not a reckoning, but a residue. The haiku collected here were written between the ages of eighty-eight and ninety-two, drawn from notebooks kept across a lifetime of crossings: the Pasig River at low tide, a rented room in Nashville, the red dust rising off a Nairobi road, the particular silence of an Auckland winter morning when the body moves slowly and the light does not rush it. Kim did not set out to write a life. Kim set out to pay attention, which turned out to be the same thing. The book's title comes from a conviction that runs quietly beneath every poem: that the body is not a vessel passing through landscapes but a landscape itself, one that accumulates rather than empties. Manila is not the past. Tennessee is not a detour. Nairobi is not elsewhere. They are all present simultaneously, layered in the hands that hold the cup, the shoulders that remember a borrowed coat, the ear that hears a tūī and finds no prior word for what it does. Displacement, in these poems, is not a wound to be healed but a form of wealth — unwieldy, sometimes heavy, but real. Readers may notice that these haiku do not always obey the traditional seasonal word, the fixed syllable count, the resolved image. Kim has spent a lifetime working between languages — Filipino, Swahili, English, and the silences between them — and the poems carry that history in their bones. What they do observe, faithfully and without exception, is the haiku's essential discipline: to be present in a single moment so completely that the moment opens outward into everything else. That is the only rule this book keeps, and it keeps it without fail.
