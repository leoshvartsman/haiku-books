What the Body Carries

Kaori Kato

Kaori Kato has lived in at least three languages, and this book is the record of what each one cost her. What the Body Carries moves from the terraced rice fields of rural Japan through the cold intellectual light of New England, into the heat of American faith and American power, and finally up into the thin air of Bogotá — where the city arrives before the lungs are ready for it. These haiku are not travel poems. They are something closer to testimony: small, precise acts of witness made by a woman who has crossed enough borders to understand that every crossing leaves a mark on the body before it reaches the mind.

autumn terraces—

each paddy holds a different

shade of going gold

workshop: they praise

the *restraint* in my poem—

I had meant to shout

drought-cracked soil outside

and inside, the carpet holds

the shape of old knees

the empty chair waits

the way a wound waits—

open, already knowing

Kato: I speak it

into the Bogotá air—

the altitude holds

Transmilenio stop:

swaying with the bus
