The Body Keeps the River

Yuki Kato
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
Yuki Kato has spent a lifetime learning to look closely: at the body on the examination table, at the river running brown through a city still rebuilding itself, at the olive harvest going thin for reasons no one wants to name. The haiku gathered here span more than six decades and three countries, moving from postwar Osaka through the corridors of European medicine to the warm plaster walls of a Rome apartment where a gecko demonstrates, daily, the art of stillness. They are a physician's poems — precise, patient, trained on what is actually there — and they carry the particular grief of someone who has watched both bodies and ecosystems move through crisis with the same fragile, astonishing persistence. Haiku has always been a form for attending to the present moment, but these poems are also quietly preoccupied with what the present moment contains of the past: a mother who turns the fish only once because she has always turned it only once, a harvest ledger whose thinning column the ink refuses to soften, a seed in a jar whose lid has not been opened since a particular pair of hands last touched it. Kato does not moralize. The form does not permit it, and neither does the temperament. What the poems offer instead is the discipline of witness — the belief that to see a thing clearly, and to set it down without flinching, is itself a form of care. 'The Body Keeps the River' is arranged not strictly by chronology but by mood and method, moving from luminous early memory through the learned precision of medical practice, through the absorbed wonder of a foreigner in Italy, and finally into the spare, unresolved tenderness of old age. Readers will find their own paths through. The silences between poems are part of the text. So is whatever the reader brings to the standing in the rain.

the small blue inhaler and

a half-eaten cracker

rain pocks the Tiber—

a heron stands in it

deciding nothing

at eighty, the words
