Borders of Monsoon

Sage Sato
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
Sage Sato has lived in the latitudes of the monsoon and the latitudes of the microscope, and this book refuses to separate them. Born into the seasonal drama of Dhaka's Buriganga basin, trained as a palaeoclimatologist across three continents, Sato has spent decades reading the Earth's memory — in peat cores, in ice, in the ring-width of trees that survived centuries no living person witnessed. Borders of Monsoon is the record of a life organized not by passport stamps or institutional affiliations but by weather: the particular weight of air before the rains, the colour of light through a laboratory slide, the way fog moves differently over a Scottish loch than over a Korean harbour. These haiku do not explain that life. They inhabit it, moment by pressured moment, asking the reader to stand inside the sensation before reaching for its meaning. The collection moves roughly chronologically — childhood Bangladesh, graduate study in Michigan, fieldwork in Orkney, a late-career residency in Busan — but chronology is not its real architecture. Its real architecture is accumulation: of homelands, of losses, of the specific knowledge that comes from watching carefully and speaking slowly. Sato writes as a scientist who has learned that precision is a form of tenderness, and as a poet who has learned that grief, if observed long enough, acquires the patience of data. The political poems do not soften this. The elegies do not sentimentalize it. What holds the book together is a quality of attention that has been worn, over seventy-eight years, into something close to grace. Haiku has always been a form about belonging to a moment — the season word, the kigo, anchoring the poem to a specific turn of the natural world. Sato extends this logic across hemispheres and decades, asking what happens when the body carries multiple seasons simultaneously, when the smell of Busan rain unlocks a memory of Dhaka, when a Roman summer is held in a core of peat inside a pair of latex gloves. The answer, this book suggests, is not confusion. It is a richer, more demanding form of home.
