Epoxy Floors

Lenny Black
361 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
There is a room most people walk past without thinking. It smells like rubber and cold concrete and something faintly petrochemical, and its floor is harder than any other floor in the house, and its light is more honest. We call it a garage, and we treat it as a leftover — the place where the car sleeps, where the broken things wait, where the stuff we cannot name goes to be forgotten. Lenny Black does not see it that way. For Black, the garage is the one room in a life where a person can still be fully accountable to themselves: to what they have built, what they have lifted, what they have fixed, and what they have simply sat with in the early hours before the rest of the world catches up. 'Epoxy Floors' follows a single recurring figure through the full arc of a day spent in such a space — from the suspended moment before the overhead door is pulled to the last chain-pull that kills the light. The collection does not argue that this life is heroic or exceptional. It argues something quieter and more durable: that attention paid to ordinary things is its own form of devotion, and that a well-kept floor, a chalk-dusted barbell, a coil of silver wire, or a car backed in by feel can hold as much human meaning as any grander subject poetry has ever claimed. These haiku do not reach for transcendence. They stay low, close to the slab, and trust that to be enough. The seven sections move through the day with the logic of ritual rather than narrative — each one a different quality of light, a different register of the body and mind. Readers who have ever found themselves lingering in a garage longer than they needed to will recognize something here. So will readers who have never set foot in one but know the feeling of a space that asks nothing of you except that you show up and do the work.
