What the Hands Remember

Morgan Durand
20 sonnets
Download PDF Download EPUB
Morgan Durand was ninety-three years old when these sonnets were completed, and the form itself seems to know it. The sonnet is a vessel built for argument — for the turn, the pivot, the moment when a mind changes direction — and these poems are full of such turns: from nostalgia into clarity, from witness into complicity, from the desire to belong into something harder and more honest than belonging. Durand has lived in four cities across four countries, worked with ledgers and with dockworkers, watched jacarandas drop their petals on Johannesburg pavement and dogwood open white against Virginia clay, and the sonnets do not try to reconcile these experiences so much as hold them in the same pair of hands. The hands matter here. They are the collection's recurring figure — hands that signed freight manifests and held children and hovered, finally, over the white of a page with their swollen knuckles and their long memory. What the Hands Remember is not a memoir in verse, though it carries a life's full weight. It is a reckoning: with labor and its costs, with the particular loneliness of the intellectual exile, with the moral vertigo of standing in two worlds at once and knowing it. Durand does not ask for absolution. The poems ask only that you stay in the room long enough to feel what is at stake.

Sample Sonnets

The dogwood opened white against the clay,

a shock of cold bloom on that April road,

and I, who knew the Seine's grey-green display,

stood dumb before this American episode.

The red earth bled beneath the blossoms' weight,

a wound the petals neither dressed nor healed,

and I had crossed an ocean, come too late

or far too early, nothing yet revealed.

My dictionary lay cracked on the desk,

spine broken at the word for *longing*—*désir*—

the English cousins circled, arabesque,

and none of them arrived at what I meant to hear.

The dogwood does not ask what country made you.

It opens anyway. It will not aid you.

The bay holds dark at five, before the tankers

come grinding through the mouth of Guanabara,

before the diesel smell, before the anchors

drop their iron weight in the still water.

I stand where the consultant stands at dawn

beside the man who mends his net by feel,

his fingers finding every brokenawn

of rope the way a surgeon finds what's real.

My briefcase latches cold against my shin.

His net smells of the bottom, salt and kelp.

We do not speak. The light begins to thin

the darkness out, and neither one can help

the other with the work that must be done—

yet here we are, two men beneath one sun.

October's jacarandas do not know

they are dying beautifully. This is

the thing I keep returning to—the slow

surrender that looks, from where I sit, like bliss.

At thirty I would have said: resist.

At sixty I would have said: resist, but see.

Now at ninety-three I would insist

on nothing. The petal falls. The street

receives it. The tree is already making

the next thing in the dark of its own wood,

some future blossom in the long unbroken

chain of yes. I have misunderstood

so much. The jacaranda has not.

It drops. It carpets. It is not forgot.
