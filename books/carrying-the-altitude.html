Carrying the Altitude

Jordan Müller
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
There is a kind of altitude that stays in the body long after you have descended. Jordan Müller grew up breathing the thin, luminous air of Bogotá — a city perched at 2,600 metres, where the sky arrives closer and the lungs learn early to negotiate with scarcity. That early education in breath, in threshold, in the gap between what the body knows and what the mind can name, runs underneath every poem in this collection like a subterranean river. Carrying the Altitude is not a book about homesickness, though it knows homesickness intimately. It is a book about what accumulates: the layered sediment of three cities, two continents, multiple languages, and the slow, unannounced work of becoming a person in more than one place at once. Moving from the prelinguistic warmth of a Colombian childhood through the grey, unsparing harbours of Edinburgh and into the cobbled winters of Prague, these haiku do not narrate a journey so much as take its temperature — moment by moment, sensation by sensation, the way a body registers a new climate before the mind has found words for it. Müller works in the classical haiku tradition's devotion to the immediate and the concrete, but bends it toward an interior geography: the weight of a passport, the particular silence between two languages, the shape a coat holds when the person inside it has not quite arrived. The form itself — compressed, imagistic, alive to the unsaid — turns out to be the ideal vessel for a life lived at the borders of belonging. The book is arranged in seven sections that move roughly chronologically but are not strictly memoir. They are, rather, seven different ways of listening to the same life. Readers who have ever felt the coordinates of home shift beneath them — through migration, through grief, through the simple passage of time — will find in these pages not a map of loss but something more useful: a record of what the body carries, and what, quietly, it learns to call enough.
