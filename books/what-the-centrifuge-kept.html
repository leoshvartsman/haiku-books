What the Centrifuge Kept

Seo Lim

Seo Lim has spent a career learning to look. As a laboratory scientist, she has held the world at one remove — pressed beneath glass, suspended in solution, resolved into data — and found it no less astonishing for that. This collection is the record of that looking: a life traced through specimens and seasons, through the Seoul of childhood memory and the wide southerly skies of rural New Zealand, through a pandemic that asked everyone to become, suddenly, a kind of scientist of their own small world. What the Centrifuge Kept is not a memoir in the ordinary sense. It is something more patient than that — an accumulation of precise moments that trust the reader to feel the weight of what is not said. Haiku has always been a form built for the observer, and Lim wears that inheritance lightly. Her poems carry the double attention of someone trained to find meaning in the marginal and the minute: the sound a centrifuge makes in the dark, the shape a lab coat holds long after the body has left it, the way flax bends its whole body in a southerly wind rather than just its tips. She has lived across languages — Korean, English, fragments of French — and the gaps between them become, in her hands, a kind of subject. What cannot be named is not lost. It is simply held differently. The collection moves roughly chronologically, from early childhood in Seoul to her seventy-seventh year, but time here is less a line than a series of returns. The same quality of attention that a child brings to a grandmother's brazier reappears, decades later, at a laboratory bench, and again at a kitchen window watching steam rise from a cooling cup of tea. Lim does not argue that a life of close observation leads to wisdom. She suggests something quieter and more durable: that wonder, properly tended, does not diminish. It sharpens.
