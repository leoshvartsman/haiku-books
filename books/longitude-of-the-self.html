Longitude of the Self

Jamie Garcia

A meridian is an imaginary line — a way of locating yourself on a spinning earth by agreeing, collectively, to pretend the line is real. Jamie Garcia's debut collection, Longitude of the Self, takes that fiction seriously. Across seven sections and more than two hundred haiku, Garcia traces a life measured not in decades but in degrees of longitude: the sun-hammered freeways of Los Angeles, the gray November rivers of Lyon, the vertiginous silence above Banff's treeline, the three-a.m. downpours of Singapore. Each coordinate is precise. Each self found there is slightly different, and slightly the same. Haiku, the form Garcia has chosen, is itself a technology of location — seventeen syllables that say: I was here, it was this moment, something passed through me. In Garcia's hands the form becomes a kind of GPS for the interior, triangulating position not by satellite but by the quality of light on water, the smell of asphalt after rain, the particular loneliness of a hotel soap still in its wrapper. The poems do not argue for a philosophy of home. They enact one, image by image, until the reader begins to feel that home is not a destination the speaker left or is traveling toward, but a recurring frequency — something the self emits, and occasionally receives back. Longitude of the Self is arranged as a journey, but it is not a linear one. Readers will find that the freeway of Section One and the notebook of Section Seven are the same road, and that the person who could not read the eucalyptus's dropped letter at the beginning is, by the end, still learning the alphabet — only now with more patience, and less time, and something that looks very much like joy.
