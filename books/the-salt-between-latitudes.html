The Salt Between Latitudes

Ishaan Chopra
25 sonnets
Download PDF Download EPUB
These sonnets do not move through a life in order. They move through it the way the body does — by sensation, by the sudden return of a smell or a sound that makes one city briefly occupy another. Ishaan Chopra is seventy-five, and the places that have made him are not behind him. Mumbai is in his hands. Toronto is in the way he hears silence. Spain is in his voice and his evening light. The poems gathered here are his attempt to account for all of it at once, not as loss, not as triumph, but as the simple and bewildering fact of what a person accumulates when they have lived across latitudes and languages for a very long time. The sonnet form is not incidental to this project. It is the argument. A form that wants to close, that reaches toward resolution with every couplet, turns out to be the ideal vessel for a man who has spent his life arriving and not quite arriving — who knows that the right word exists, somewhere, in one of his four languages, and that the search for it is itself the meaning. Read these poems as a map drawn from memory, where the distances between places are measured not in kilometers but in the body's own stubborn, faithful keeping.

Sample Sonnets

My mother's hands are what I cannot write —

they keep escaping every line I build,

they move through the green silk and the morning light

and will not hold the shape that form has willed.

The sonnet wants to close, wants the couplet's click,

the satisfying lock of meaning made,

but her hands keep moving, keep the sari's thick

and heavy silk from lying down, afraid

of nothing, folding, folding without end,

the pleat an act of faith in repetition,

in the idea that what you tend

will hold its shape, will honor the decision.

I write in four languages. None arrive

at what her hands knew. All of them survive.

What strikes me now, from Madrid, is how

the cold was never only cold—it held

a quality of attention, a vow

the temperature extracted, something felled

inside the chest when air costs something real,

when breath becomes a thing you have to earn.

The Retiro's mild fog cannot conceal

what bodies learn and do not unlearn:

I stepped off a plane in October, thin coat,

wrong shoes, a suitcase full of cotton shirts,

standing outside arrivals with a note

of someone's address, learning how cold hurts.

That lesson stays. Madrid gives afternoon light.

Toronto gave me cold, and made it right.

The orange groves of late November smell

of something almost gone, the last fruit bright

as warnings, and I drive through them and tell

myself this counts as witness, counts as light.

In Mumbai there were mango trees, the green

weight of the fruit before it turned to gold,

and I would stand beneath them at thirteen

convinced the world was something to be sold.

Now I am selling no one, doing ninety,

the groves receding in the rearview glass,

the afternoon a bureaucratic plenty

of road I've made my habit and my mass.

The fruit hangs on. The fruit will drop by noon.

I know this like a salesman knows a room.
