Clockout in Three Languages

Riley Johnson
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
Riley Johnson has spent a working life learning the grammar of departure — the way a shift ends, a city releases you, a name stops fitting. Clockout in Three Languages maps that life not through argument or elegy but through the senses: diesel and frost, a payphone quarter never spent, the particular weight of a park bench still warm from a stranger. These two hundred and fifty haiku move the way shift-worker memory moves — sideways, loyal to the concrete, circling back to the same cold bus stop from a different decade. They do not explain themselves, and they do not apologise. The collection crosses four cities — a harbour town, a Canadian winter, Shanghai's Pudong district, and the Sydney that persists inside a body even when that body is standing somewhere else entirely — and it crosses five decades of a life spent watching, waiting, and occasionally being surprised by a parking lot maple. Johnson writes from the outside of institutions: outside the academy, outside steady employment, outside the name he was born with. That outsider position is not romanticised here. It is simply where he stood, and what he saw from there. The haiku form suits this project with an almost suspicious precision. Its compression honours the working-class suspicion of grandeur; its attention to the physical world honours the knowledge that accumulates in hands and knees rather than notebooks. What Johnson calls the 'sensory grammar' of his places is also the grammar of survival — the small, exact, unremarkable things that turn out, in the end, to be everything.
