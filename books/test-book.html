Rain is patient. It does not arrive all at once — it gathers first in the hesitation before a storm, in the way a person pauses at a window without quite knowing why. This collection follows that gathering: the slow accumulation of water and silence, the way both find their level in the lowest, quietest places. The haiku here are not explanations. They are the sounds a house makes when no one is speaking. An unnamed presence moves through these pages the way a person moves through a difficult season — not heroically, not with resolution, but with the ordinary persistence of someone who keeps noticing things. A crack in the plaster. A coat hook. The color of sky between two clouds. Attention, in these poems, is not a cure. It is simply what remains when everything else has been stripped of ceremony. The arc bends from anticipation through immersion, inwardness, stillness, grief, and something that cannot quite be called recovery — only clearing. What is left at the end is not transformed. It is simply lighter, the way a gutter runs clear not because it has been cleaned but because the rain has finally passed through. The reader is invited to move slowly, to let the silences between poems count as poems too.

the drain holds its breath—

one ring of water widens

then the whole street waits

the intersection floods

to ankle height at the corner—

a man rolls his cuffs

the hook holds the coat

the coat holds the shape of shoulders

no one is wearing

the clock stopped at dusk

so morning keeps arriving

at the wrong address

one corner bent back

from when I tried to open it—

I folded it down

the puddle holds still

a telephone line, two birds—

then one bird leaves it
