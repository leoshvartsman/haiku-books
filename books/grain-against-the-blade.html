Grain Against the Blade

Jing Li
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
Jing Li has spent forty years learning what wood knows. Across four cities and four languages, across the grain of mesquite and oak and beech and pine, he has accumulated the particular intelligence that lives in the hands — the knowledge that cannot be spoken but can, it turns out, be written. These haiku are the record of that education. They are small, the way a mortise is small, the way a latch-click at dawn is small, but they have been fitted with the same attention a carpenter brings to a threshold: nothing left loose, nothing left proud of the surface, every joint made to hold weight it may never be asked to bear. The collection moves through a life in four landscapes — Shanghai, Edinburgh, Guadalajara, Copenhagen — but it does not move as a memoir moves, forward through time with a destination in mind. It moves the way a hand plane moves, back and forth across the same surface, each pass revealing a little more of the figure in the grain. The early haiku are soaked in river-light and wet-market smell, in the pre-language wonder of a child who does not yet know he is storing everything up. The middle haiku smell of rain on old stone, of sawdust and bougainvillea, of the specific loneliness of a man who thinks in one language and dreams in another and works in a third. The late haiku arrive somewhere quieter — a deer park in winter, a bowl of plums, a table that holds the hands that built it. The title names the central tension of the book and, perhaps, of any life lived between making and being made. Grain against the blade: the resistance that is also collaboration, the friction that produces both heat and smoothness, the way a material pushes back just enough to tell you what it is. Li does not resolve this tension. He planes it down until it glows.
