Sitting on The Couch and Reading

Coach Potato
370 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
There is a particular kind of evening that asks nothing of you. The errands are done, the door is locked, and the couch — your couch, shaped now to the specific geography of your rest — is waiting. You have chosen a book of poetry, which is itself a choice worth noting: not a novel with its forward pull, not a screen with its restless brightness, but poems, which ask you to slow down and then slow down again. This collection follows one such evening from its careful beginning to its quiet end, tracing the small ceremonies by which a person converts a living room into a world. What happens inside that world is harder to name than it looks. There is the body settling, the mind releasing its grip on the day, and then — if the poems are good, and sometimes even if they aren't — a kind of dissolution. You stop sitting on the couch and begin to exist somewhere else entirely, somewhere made of language and the white space around language. The tea goes cold. The cat makes her demands. The phone glows and fades. You do not move. This is not laziness. This is one of the few remaining forms of radical stillness available to us, and it is worth paying attention to. These haiku are an attempt to pay that attention. They are small observations about a small event — one person, one couch, one book, one lamp — but they mean to suggest that chosen stillness has its own interior weather, its own arrivals and departures. By the last page, something has happened. It is difficult to say exactly what. You set the book down slowly, find the floor with your feet, and return to your life slightly altered, the way you always do after a long time somewhere else.
