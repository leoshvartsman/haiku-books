The Light Moves Differently Here

Casey Ali
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
Casey Ali has lived at four latitudes, and this book is the record of all of them. From the red dust of Accra to the salt air of Sydney, from the cedar-grey winters of Vancouver to the jacaranda-heavy summers of Johannesburg, these haiku were written by someone who has had to learn, repeatedly, how to be somewhere. Not to arrive — arrival is easy — but to be present: to let a place enter the body slowly, through the soles of the feet, through the particular quality of its morning light, through the way strangers look away on public transit or a market vendor reads rain before the sky does. Attention, Ali suggests, is not a passive act. It is the work of belonging when belonging cannot be assumed. The haiku form suits this life precisely. Each poem is small, patient, and exact — a document rather than a declaration. Ali is not interested in grand statements about diaspora or displacement; those words appear nowhere in these pages. What appears instead is a grandmother's voice before the rains, a thermostat left at a stranger's setting, purple jacaranda so thick it makes the street forget itself. The ordinary is treated here as sacred not because it is dressed up, but because someone was paying close enough attention to catch it in the act of being ordinary. That act of catching is, in itself, a form of love. The seven sections of this collection move through time and geography without strict chronology, following instead the rhythms of a life: deep memory, disoriented arrival, the long smooth loneliness of professional transit, the fatigue of perpetual translation, and finally — slowly, without fanfare — something like stillness. By the final section, the light finds the same inch of wall it always finds at this hour. The avocado tree planted on a hard day now shades the whole stoep. Nothing is resolved, exactly. But something has been witnessed, and that witnessing has been enough.

red dust between toes—

grandmother calls me inside

before the rains come

the cockatoo screams

what I have not said aloud

since leaving Accra

SkyTrain doors open—

the platform crowd negotiates

without a word said

the mirror fogs slow

I write nothing in the steam

already enough

the storm passes east

dragging its grey curtain low

over the township roofs

gecko at the drain —

not hunting, just considering

the direction of things
