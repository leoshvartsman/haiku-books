What the Weaver Bird Knows

Robin Smith
250 haiku

A life lived across latitudes leaves marks that years alone cannot measure. Robin Smith was born in Addis Ababa, carried a name through several countries, worked and taught and listened in Singapore and Dublin, and has been writing haiku for most of eight decades. This collection does not narrate that life. It does something quieter and more precise: it catches the moments in which a consciousness, moving through the urban natural world, suddenly stops and knows exactly where it is. Red dust. A gecko on a wall. The bench worn smooth by strangers. The pen held above a page in early morning. These are the coordinates of a life measured not in years but in instants of full attention. Haiku has always understood that the smallest living thing can hold the whole world steady. A weaver bird threading grass through grass without a needle is not a symbol of anything beyond itself — and yet, in being fully itself, it opens outward into everything: craft, patience, the intelligence of the non-human, the possibility that we are not the only ones who know how to make something that holds. Smith's haiku work in this tradition while remaining stubbornly particular — rooted in specific cities, specific weathers, specific losses and arrivals. The form asks for nothing sentimental, and Smith does not offer it. What is offered instead is attention, which turns out to be a form of love. The seven sections of this collection move roughly through time, from earliest childhood in Ethiopia to the long Dublin mornings of the present, though time here is less a line than a quality of light — shifting, returning, occasionally flooding a room without warning. Readers who follow the sequence will find a life assembling itself gradually, the way a web is built: one filament at a time, each one necessary, the whole pattern only visible when you step back. Readers who open the book anywhere will find a single moment, complete in itself, asking only to be inhabited.
