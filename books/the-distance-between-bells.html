The Distance Between Bells

Nicola Esposito

There is a kind of time that cannot be measured by clocks or calendars — only by the quality of light falling on a particular wall, by the smell of rain on a specific stone, by the moment you realize you have finally stopped rehearsing your arrival and simply arrived. The Distance Between Bells is a life told in such moments: not a biography of events but a cartography of attention, tracing one person's slow, fitful, sometimes reluctant education in the art of being present. From the ancient ochre streets of Rome to the cold quadrangles of an English university, from the diesel-thick mornings of Andean commerce to the fog-wrapped hills of a Tuscan village, these poems ask what it costs to keep moving, and what it costs to stop. Haiku, with its insistence on the concrete and the immediate, is perhaps the only form equal to this kind of reckoning. Each poem here is a small act of attention — a wet stone, a peeling billboard, a candle burning in an empty church — and each one carries more freight than its seventeen syllables might suggest. The sacred does not announce itself in this collection. It arrives the way fog does: gradually, without permission, until you look up and find the world has changed around you. The seven sections of this book follow a loose arc — from formation through displacement, ambition, rupture, suspension, and finally a tentative, luminous stillness — but they are not meant to be read as a tidy progress. Life rarely moves in one direction, and neither does this collection. What holds it together is not a destination but a thread: the recurring sense that the world is always offering something, and that the whole work of a life might be learning, slowly and imperfectly, to receive it.
