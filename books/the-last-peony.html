The Last Peony

Mira Osei

A peony does not open gradually. It waits, and waits, and then it tears itself apart in a single night, offering everything at once with no apparent concern for how long it can sustain that offering. Mira Osei found in this lifecycle a shape that felt true — not just to grief, which is the obvious companion, but to inheritance, to Black womanhood, to the specific experience of loving someone whose fullness you are still discovering after they are gone. The haiku in this collection follow a single bloom from the sealed, almost stubborn anticipation of the bud to the bare stem standing in water that has not yet been poured out. In between, there is a great deal of living. To write about the peony is to write about attention — the particular discipline of looking at something beautiful while knowing, in the same instant, that it is already leaving. These poems do not sentimentalize that knowledge, nor do they flinch from it. They ask instead what it means to be present to what cannot last: a grandmother's knuckles pressing bulbs into cold ground, a recipe that says only 'handful,' a petal on the floor that you step around for three days before you finally sweep it up. The haiku form, with its insistence on the concrete and its refusal of explanation, turns out to be the right vessel for this kind of looking. What cannot be said directly can sometimes be held in the gap between the image and what it carries. 'The Last Peony' moves in seven sections, each corresponding to a stage in the bloom's life, each widening slightly to include more of what the peony has always been a way of talking about. By the final section, the flower is gone, and what remains is a room, a fork already removed from the table, a name spoken aloud into silence. This is not a book about loss so much as it is a book about what attention can do in the presence of loss — which is not to fix it, or resolve it, but to keep it company with open eyes.
