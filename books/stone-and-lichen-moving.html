Stone and Lichen, Moving

Cruz Hernández
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
Cruz Hernández has spent a working life looking at small things carefully — slides under light, cultures in suspension, the precise behavior of matter at the edge of change. That training shows in these poems. The haiku here do not reach for the grand or the consoling. They reach for the moss in the pavement joint, the hum descending through a centrifuge into a shoe sole, the pigeon making its small aerodynamic decisions against a crosswind. They find that this is enough. It is, in fact, quite a lot. The collection moves through four cities across four decades — a Mediterranean coast, a northern city of grey milk and wet wool, an American sprawl of split brick and self-planting trees, a Swiss lakeside where coots test ice with one lifted foot. These are not postcards and they are not elegies. They are field notes taken by someone who learned early that nature does not wait for wilderness, that it insists itself into every corner where a human has briefly looked away. The bougainvillea bleeds through whitewashed stone whether or not anyone is watching. The ailanthus holds the cold wall. The lichen reads the rock in four scripts. The world is always doing something, and these poems are the record of a person who kept noticing. What emerges across two hundred and fifty haiku is not a travelogue or a memoir, though it contains the materials of both. It is something quieter and more durable: a habit of attention, practiced until it becomes a way of being. The stillness at the end of this collection is not peace exactly — Hernández is too honest for that — but it is something earned, the way a place becomes home not through arrival but through the slow accumulation of ordinary mornings observed with care.

Sample Haikus

bougainvillea bleeds

through the crack in whitewashed stone—

no one fills it now

haar swallows the Firth,

the bridges disappear first,

then the idea of shore

past midnight the hum

of the centrifuge travels

room to room to room

the dumpster shifts once,

the groundhog does not move — it

has outlasted worse

thin cloud, then no cloud,

then the Alps appearing once —

the window fogs shut

after the tram spark—

a woman looks up from her phone

at the dark. looks back.
