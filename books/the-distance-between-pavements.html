The Distance Between Pavements

Andrea Hernández

A haiku is a small room with no furniture. You step inside and find that the emptiness is the point — that what is not said holds the weight of what cannot be. Andrea Hernández has spent a life crossing thresholds: between languages, between hemispheres, between the woman she was expected to become and the one she quietly, stubbornly did. The Distance Between Pavements is the record of that crossing, laid down in the form that suits it best: brief, exacting, and full of what it withholds. The cities in this book are not backdrops. Guadalajara, Manchester, Toronto, and the rural hill country of New Zealand's South Island are each a kind of body — with their own temperatures, their own smells, their own way of pressing themselves into a person who walks their streets long enough. Hernández is interested in what that pressing leaves behind: the sediment of place that accumulates in the self without permission, that resurfaces years later in the smell of a fruit, the click of a radiator, the particular quality of light on an unfamiliar tree. These haiku do not argue that we are shaped by where we live. They simply show the shaping, one small exact moment at a time. The book moves, broadly, in the order that life moved: childhood warmth, the cold intellectual north, the glass ambitions of a corporate city, the suspended breath of bureaucratic identity, and finally the radical quietness of a landscape that asks nothing familiar of the person who arrives in it. But Hernández is not writing a memoir. She is writing something more patient than that — a reckoning with how a self is assembled from the places it has passed through, and how, if you are lucky and attentive, you might one day look down and recognise all of them in you at once.
