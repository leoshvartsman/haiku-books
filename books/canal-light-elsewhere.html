Canal Light, Elsewhere

Sage Andersson
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
There is a particular kind of attention that belongs to people who have left. Not the dramatic attention of crisis, but the low, chronic alertness of someone who has learned that any place can become a memory without warning — that the smell of rain on stone, the weight of a coin, the pitch of a church bell can all become evidence of somewhere you no longer are. Sage Andersson has moved too many times to be innocent about this. A musician before a poet, she carried her noticing first through sound, then found that haiku — small, time-stamped, ruthlessly specific — was the form that fit the life. These poems are her field notes. Canal Light, Elsewhere moves through four cities and one long question: what does a body do with everywhere it has been? The seven sections trace a loose arc from Amsterdam to Los Angeles by way of Hiroshima, Boston, and the unnamed in-between spaces of tour vans and airport terminals. But this is not a travel book, and these are not postcards. The haiku here are acts of reckoning — with beauty, with guilt, with the strange discipline of starting over, with the way a self accumulates like sediment, slowly, without asking permission. Andersson writes in the tradition of haiku as witness: the poem as a single clear moment, held up to the light. But her moments are haunted. The canal is also a wound. The blooming oleander insists on blooming. The hand that forgets a chord finds a pen. Read these poems as you would read a musician's practice log — not for narrative, but for the quality of attention, the evidence of someone who kept showing up to notice, city after city, until the noticing itself became home.

Sample Haiku

February cracks—

the canal opens one seam

under my old bike

the suitcase opens

and Amsterdam smell lifts out—

old wool, canal damp

the glass case is fogged

in the corner near the floor—

child height, I notice

the hallway at two,

cases like sleeping bodies—

I step around them

ice on the railing,

one sparrow testing the edge —

it holds, barely holds

Silver Lake water

catching the last of the light—

a jogger, earbuds
