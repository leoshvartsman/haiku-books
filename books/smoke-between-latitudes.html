Smoke Between Latitudes

Harper Martin

A haiku is a threshold — seventeen syllables wide, permeable in both directions. Harper Martin's 'Smoke Between Latitudes' stands in that threshold and refuses to move. Assembled from four geographies that are also four states of being — Saigon's saturated heat, Tokyo's exacting quiet, the peat-dark hills of rural Scotland, the grey-green patience of Portland — this collection does not offer the reader a tidy arc of exile and homecoming. It offers something more honest: a body in ongoing negotiation with the places that have pressed themselves into it, and the silences that remain where translation fails. These are not postcards. They are pressure marks. Martin works in the haiku tradition with full awareness of what that tradition demands and what it cannot hold. The form's insistence on the present moment becomes, across two hundred and fifty poems, a kind of ethical commitment — to witness without sentimentalizing, to name without claiming, to let a grandmother's wet footprint on tile carry the weight that grief cannot always speak aloud. The collection's title comes from no single poem but from the condition the whole book inhabits: smoke is what remains after burning, and it travels between fixed points without belonging to either. These haiku are that smoke. Readers moving through the seven sections will find the book structured as a series of crossings rather than a linear journey. Each section is a threshold with its own weather, its own quality of light and loss. What accumulates is not resolution but a different kind of knowing — the knowledge that a person can be fluent in four landscapes and still find the most important things untranslatable, and that this, too, is a place one can learn to stand.
