Dust on Three Tongues

Aditya Reddy
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
Aditya Reddy has lived in three cities across nine decades, and this book is his argument that none of them ever left him. Delhi, London, Cairo — each pressed its particular light into him, its particular noise and silence, and what you hold here is the residue of that pressure: ninety-four years distilled into two hundred and fifty haiku, brief as breath, heavy as stone. The title comes from something Reddy said in conversation: that he has always carried dust from each city on his tongue, that he speaks in all three registers at once even when only one language comes out. Dust on Three Tongues is an attempt to make that simultaneity visible on the page. The haiku form suits this project with an almost uncanny precision. A haiku does not explain or argue; it holds a moment open long enough for the reader to step inside. Reddy's moments are not exotic or monumental — a grandmother lifting brass to her lips, chalk dust on a sleeve, a boot print in Thames mud — but they accumulate into something vast. Memory, this book quietly insists, is not a filing system. It is a landscape: crumbling in places, luminous in others, and entirely alive. The past does not recede. It crowds forward, pressing against the present until the two become indistinguishable. The seven sections move roughly chronologically through a life while refusing to behave like a memoir. There is no tidy arc of departure and return, no lesson neatly learned. Instead there is a man at a window, in his ninety-fourth year, finding that every street corner he has ever known still breathes inside him — and being, against all reasonable expectation, astonished by this. Read slowly. These poems reward the pause between them as much as the words themselves.

before the roosters —

into its new shape

rain on the notebook,

the ink runs—in Cairo once

minaret shadow

touches the taxi's windshield—

the driver does not look

the heron is still

so still the water forgets

to move around it

light pools on the sill

the same pale gold that I watched
