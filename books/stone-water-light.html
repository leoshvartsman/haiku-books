Stone Water Light

Sage Lee

There is a kind of knowledge the body keeps that the mind never quite translates — the particular weight of cold air on a specific latitude, the way a certain quality of light lands differently on water depending on which river it is, the sound a city makes just before it wakes. Stone Water Light is a book built from that knowledge. It follows one life across four cities and several decades, not as autobiography but as sensory archaeology: what remains in the hands, the knees, the inner ear, after the addresses have changed and the languages have shifted and the self has had to learn, again and again, how to belong somewhere new. The haiku gathered here are organized by place but haunted by all the others. Oslo shadows fall across Kolkata afternoons. A Lyon fog recalls a Baltimore estuary. The book's central question is not where home is but what urban nature — the crow, the weed in the pavement crack, the rain on whatever roof is currently overhead — demands of a person who keeps moving through it. Nature in cities is rarely pristine; it is stubborn, adaptive, indifferent to human restlessness in ways that turn out to be a kind of comfort. These poems try to pay honest attention to that stubbornness. Sage Lee has arranged the collection to move roughly through time and geography, but also through registers of feeling: from the austere quiet of Scandinavian winters toward the overwhelming generosity of a Kolkata monsoon, with a final section that refuses to let any of it go. Readers may find their own cities somewhere in these pages, or may find instead the sensation of recognizing a stranger's window and understanding, briefly, what it costs to leave it behind.
