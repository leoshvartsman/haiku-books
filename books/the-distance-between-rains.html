The Distance Between Rains

Saoirse Kelly
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
There is a kind of distance that has nothing to do with miles. It lives in the body — in the way a particular quality of light can make the chest contract, or the smell of rain on warm concrete can split a person briefly in two, belonging to two cities at once. Saoirse Kelly's debut collection takes its title from this interior geography: the gap between one rain and the next, between one self and the next, between the person who left and the person who arrived. Across seven sections and four countries, these haiku accumulate the way weather accumulates — slowly, then all at once. Kelly writes from a life lived at multiple latitudes: Dublin first, then the flat horizons of East Anglia, then Montevideo and the wide uncertain light of the River Plate, then Mumbai, enormous and instructive. The haiku form suits this life exactly. Small enough to hold a single moment of attention, it is also spacious enough to let what is absent speak. A chimney's smoke carried home in a coat. A heron standing in fog as though fog were solid ground. A fan slowing like an interrupted thought. These are not travel poems, and this is not a memoir of displacement. It is something quieter and more demanding: a record of how a person becomes plural, how the self learns to hold contradictory weathers inside a single body without resolving them. By the final section, the speaker dreams in Spanish and wakes to a bird that belongs to neither Spanish nor Irish, and answers it anyway. This is the book's quiet argument — that home is not a coordinate but a quality of attention, and that the self capable of that attention has been assembled, slowly, from everything the rain has taught.
