What the Light Remembers

Blake Andersson
25 sonnets
Download PDF Download EPUB
Blake Andersson is ninety-nine years old, and he is not interested in telling his life in order. What the Light Remembers moves the way memory actually moves — not forward, but sideways, by association, by the particular quality of an afternoon, by the way a smell can collapse sixty years in an instant. The landscapes here are real: the Highveld outside Johannesburg, a Japanese rock garden at dawn, a Vermont farmhouse in mud season, a Vienna apartment where a gull returns, or doesn't, to the same railing. But the book is less concerned with place as geography than with place as the thing a body carries long after it has left. These are sonnets — a form that has always understood the relationship between constraint and feeling, between the fourteen lines that hold and the emotion that presses against them. Andersson wears the form lightly, letting it do its structural work without announcing it. What accumulates across the ninety-nine pages is something harder to name than a memoir and more personal than a meditation: a single consciousness gathering itself, in its final season, into something like wholeness. The book's central question — what survives translation across languages, hemispheres, and names — is never answered directly. It doesn't need to be. The asking is the answer.

Sample Sonnets

Before the word for red, there was the road,

the street still steaming where the rain had passed,

a child's foot finding something warm and broad,

the dust turned dark, turned clay, turned holding fast.

I did not know the name for what I smelled,

that iron tang the highveld sends up wet,

only that something in the body swelled

and pressed against a boundary not set yet.

The mine headgear stood above the roofline still,

its angular devotion to the sky

a shape I filed beneath the word for hill,

not knowing yet what hills were made to pry.

The body learns its landscape bone by bone

before the tongue can claim it as its own.

The calligraphy class met on Thursdays.

We entered, bowed, removed our shoes outside.

I was the only student whose gaze

carried another continent inside —

the red roads of the Highveld, the mine dumps

pale against the summer sky, the way

my grandmother's hands moved past the lumps

of memory I'd carried to that day.

The master set the ink stone before me.

I ground the ink until the water turned

to something dense and purposeful and free

of the grey dilution. What I learned

was this: the preparation is the poem.

The grinding is the only way toward home.

The passport photograph shows a younger jaw,

a surname spelled in ink I didn't choose,

the border guard who studied it with awe

or boredom — hard to say — before the queue

moved on. I've crossed enough of those low desks

to know the face they want: composed, awake,

not haunted. Singh in capitals. The text

of what I was, for someone else to take

and stamp and hand back, slightly warm. I've held

that booklet in bad light beside a glass

in this Viennese café, the window swelled

with condensation, watching strangers pass,

and thought: the man they photographed is gone.

The man who holds the photograph lives on.
