What the Light Remembers

Sage Lee

There is a kind of attention that has no nationality — that belongs equally to a child watching light fail over the foothills and to an aging expatriate listening to church bells answer each other across a river. Jordan Rodriguez has spent a life cultivating that attention, moving through landscapes that refused to resemble one another, learning to stay present inside the disorientation. What the Light Remembers is the record of that practice: not a memoir, not a travelogue, but something closer to a long listening — the way a musician listens, with the whole body, for what the room is trying to say. These two hundred and fifty haiku were gathered across decades and latitudes, from the high dry air of Colorado to the lakeshore grit of Chicago to the amber-stoned riverbanks of Florence. They do not argue for any single place or any single self. Instead, they do what haiku has always done at its most honest: they catch the moment just before interpretation arrives, when a magpie is still only a magpie, when rain is still only rain, when a trumpet's bell cooling in the dark is still only that — and somehow, because of the precision of the catching, becomes everything else as well. Read this book the way you would listen to an improvisation: not hunting for the theme, but trusting that the theme is finding you. The sections move roughly in the order a life moves — outward from the first longing, into the noise, across the water, and back toward something that is not quite home but is no longer exile either. What stays, Rodriguez seems to suggest, is not the places themselves but the quality of looking they demanded. That quality, once learned, travels without luggage.
