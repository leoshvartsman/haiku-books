What the Body Remembers

Parker Sato
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
Jamie Martinez has lived in latitudes rather than years. Born into Miami's salt-bright heat, carried by restlessness through Vermont's cold awakenings, Bangkok's drenched temples, and finally into the amber stillness of São Paulo, Martinez has spent a long life learning what the body insists on keeping long after the mind has moved on. These two hundred and fifty haiku are the residue of that keeping — not memoir exactly, but something more honest: the flash of a screen door spring, the weight of a name written in pencil, the precise temperature of tea allowed to go cold. Together they form a single sustained attention, one life held up to the light and turned slowly, the way you turn a stone you have carried so long you have forgotten it is in your hand. Haiku has always been a form that trusts the particular over the general, the moment over the arc. Martinez uses that trust radically here, refusing the comforts of narrative explanation and instead placing the reader inside sensation — inside the green-brown mirror of a Biscayne bay, inside the gap left by a temple bell after thunder, inside the visible tendon of a ninety-nine-year-old wrist still taut, still present, still holding. The poems do not ask for your sympathy. They ask for your attention, which is a more generous and more difficult thing to give. What the Body Remembers is arranged as a journey, but it does not arrive at wisdom so much as at openness. The final section finds Martinez with hands unclenched, not because the struggle is over but because the hands have finally learned what they were always practicing. Readers who come to this book at any age will find their own latitudes somewhere in its pages — their own inherited names, their own rooms where tea goes cold, their own mornings when the light lands on a knuckle and makes a small bright lake of it. That is the quiet ambition of this book: not to speak for a life, but to make a life speakable.

mangrove roots reach down

into the green-brown mirror—

my knees do the same

Sato: my father's

silence pressed into two sounds —

I carried it far

Vermont cold arrives

not with drama—just a door

left open too long

tuk-tuk at the curb

engine ticking in the heat—

the driver sleeps, upright

the grandmother's hands

on the clipboard—steadier

than mine have been lately

the cold cup, the dusk,

the petals on the concrete—

nothing needs my help
