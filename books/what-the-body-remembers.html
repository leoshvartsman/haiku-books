What the Body Remembers

Parker Sato
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
Aging, Riley Müller suggests, does not arrive on a particular birthday. It arrives in the sound a piece of birch bark makes underfoot, in the grey hair you almost don't recognize in a site office mirror, in the way your thumb knows without thinking to press against rust. Müller came to poetry after years as a structural engineer, and that training is everywhere in this book — not as metaphor, exactly, but as a way of paying attention. An engineer learns to read what a material is telling you long before it fails. These haiku do the same thing with a life. What the Body Remembers moves across four cities and two continents — Oslo, then the fog-softened edges of a Canadian arrival, then years of construction work, then Istanbul — and it asks, quietly and without self-pity, what accumulates in a person the way sediment accumulates, the way a railing rusts, the way the worn edges of a backgammon piece record seventy years of a hand without anyone meaning to keep that record. This is not a book about old age. It is a book about the strange discovery, made somewhere in the middle of things, that the process has been underway all along. Haiku has always been a form preoccupied with transience, with the single moment that contains the whole of something. Müller uses it to do something slightly different: to show how moments accumulate into a self, how a childhood window and an Istanbul morning can exist in the same body simultaneously, how clarity and grief and wonder are not stages but weather. These 250 poems are brief by design. What they leave in the silence between them is the point.

birch bark curls away

like letters I never sent

white on white on white

pencil shavings curl

beside my coffee—someone

reads my margin notes

fog takes the far shore

first, then the middle distance —

I am still here though

I signed the card first —

by the time it came back around

the ink had dried dark

the strait holds its lane:

cargo from Odessa north,

fuel south, always south

he loses the game

and sets the pieces back himself—

this is also skill
