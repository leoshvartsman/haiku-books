What the Body Remembers

Sky Singh
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
There is a kind of knowledge the mind cannot hold alone. It lives instead in the hinge of a knee descending wet stairs, in the way the chest tightens before the brain has named the season, in the involuntary curl of fingers around a shape that no longer exists. Sky Singh came to understand this at eighty — not as philosophy but as plain fact, the way you understand gravity or thirst. Memory, he found, had never really lived in the mind at all. It had been distributed all along: parceled out to cartilage and lung tissue, to the pads of the fingers and the small bones of the inner ear, kept safe in the body's own dark archive while the mind busied itself with other business. What the Body Remembers moves through eight sections, each one a different chamber of that archive. The journey is not chronological and not quite geographical, though it passes through Prague, Baltimore, Portland, and Singapore — through the Cold War and its particular hungers, through immigrant winters and equatorial noons, through decades of professional precision and the slow, clarifying loosening of old age. The haiku gathered here do not explain these places or these years. They do something quieter and stranger: they let the body speak in its own syntax, which is the syntax of sensation — sudden, non-linear, startlingly specific, and almost always arriving a moment before understanding does. Readers may find that their own bodies begin to answer back. That is the nature of this kind of knowing. It is not stored in one person. It is the oldest form of language we have — older than words, older than maps — and it recognizes itself across distances that the mind would call unbridgeable. Enter slowly. The body will know where it has been.
