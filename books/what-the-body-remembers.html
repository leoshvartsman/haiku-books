What the Body Remembers

Sam Chen
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
A haiku is a small container for a large fact. It does not explain; it holds. In this collection, Sam Chen has arranged 250 such containers along the arc of a life that has refused to stay in one place — a life conducted in multiple languages, across cities that each demanded a different version of the self, through work that required sitting very still while other people's grief moved through the room. What the Body Remembers is not a memoir, but it carries the weight of one. The body, it turns out, is an excellent archivist: it keeps the temperature of a Brussels tram window, the particular anxiety of a misspelled nameplate, the exact angle of a tissue box that has not been touched in a week. These seven sections move roughly chronologically, though time in haiku is never quite linear — it folds, doubles back, rhymes with itself across decades. A reader will find Brussels in Rome, and Rome in Hong Kong, and Hong Kong in a fellowship hall in the American Midwest where the coffee has been warming since seven. What connects these places is not geography but consciousness: one person learning, again and again, what it costs to arrive somewhere and what it costs to leave. The sections are not chapters so much as weather systems, each with its own pressure and light. Chen has spent decades as a therapist, a traveler, a student of languages, and a keeper of other people's silences. That particular combination — the clinical attention, the immigrant's hypervigilance, the polyglot's awareness that every word is a choice among choices — gives these haiku their distinctive texture: precise, tender, occasionally wry, and quietly undeceived. To read this book from beginning to end is to accompany a consciousness through displacement and into something that is not quite home but is, perhaps, better than home: a hard-won ease with the world's refusal to resolve.
