What the Body Remembers

Parker Sato
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
There is a kind of knowledge the body keeps that the mind cannot fully articulate — the weight of a particular cold, the smell of a room that no longer exists, the way a new street feels foreign underfoot until, one ordinary morning, it doesn't. What the Body Remembers traces one life across continents and decades, from the row houses of Philadelphia's Kensington neighborhood to the jacaranda-lined streets of Brisbane, Australia, asking what accumulates in us as we move through the world and what, if anything, we are permitted to leave behind. Haiku, with its insistence on the present moment and its appetite for the small and overlooked, turns out to be a precise instrument for this kind of reckoning. These poems do not argue for a self; they observe one into being — through the frost on a transom window, a form asking for a father's name, a purple flower swept twice from a street not yet learned. The collection moves through grief, labor, displacement, and quiet dailiness, and if it has a thesis, it is this: identity, like language, is always being translated, and belonging is not a place you arrive at so much as a practice you return to, imperfectly, every day. The seven sections of this book follow a rough chronology — childhood, searching, apprenticeship, work, transit, arrival, and something like peace — but they are not a memoir in miniature. They are a series of attention acts, each one a small wager that looking closely at the particular is the only way to say anything true about the universal. Readers who have ever felt themselves to be between worlds, between names, between one version of themselves and another, will find familiar ground here, even if the ground itself is new.

Peyton Sato, number two

what I came to learn

rush hour, the Red Line—

a child draws on the window

what she'll never file

high tide on the river—

the water holds the city

upside down, at rest

the river bends

and I am on the outside curve—

still learning to lean
