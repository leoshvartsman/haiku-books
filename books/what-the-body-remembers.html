What the Body Remembers

Haruki Tanaka
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
A haiku is a small door left ajar. You do not walk through it so much as you feel the draft it makes — a sudden change in the temperature of your attention. Haruki Tanaka has spent a lifetime collecting these drafts: in the cedar-and-mud countryside of his childhood, in the fluorescent corridors of New York law firms, in the salt-bright sprawl of Miami, and finally along the wide brown patience of the Río de la Plata. What the Body Remembers is the archive of that collection — not a memoir, exactly, but something more honest than one. Two hundred and fifty haiku that do not explain a life so much as they press a thumb into it, leaving a mark. The title is a kind of argument. Tanaka is interested in what survives the forgetting — not the grand events that announce themselves in memory, but the sensory residue that lodges somewhere below language: the smell of pine resin and charcoal, the particular squeal of packing tape, the weight of a borrowed gourd in unfamiliar hands. The body keeps its own ledger, and these poems are its entries. Arranged in seven sections that move roughly through time and geography, they resist the tidiness of a journey with a destination. There is no arrival here that does not also contain a departure. There is no loss that does not leave something behind in its place. Readers who come to haiku expecting serenity will find something more complicated and, perhaps, more companionable. Tanaka's poems are capable of stillness, but they are equally at home in fluorescent-lit exhaustion, in dark courtroom humor, in the strange comedy of aging, in grief that has been worn so smooth it can almost pass for peace. What holds all of it together is a quality of attention — unhurried, unsentimental, and stubbornly in love with the fact of being alive in a world that keeps changing its address.
