Meridians of Grass

Alex Simon

A life lived across four cities on four continents does not accumulate neatly. It sediments. Paris becomes a particular quality of light remembered at the wrong moment; Tokyo becomes a posture of attention carried into streets where it doesn't quite fit; Santiago becomes a crack in the voice when certain subjects arise; Johannesburg becomes, finally, the ground underfoot. Alex Simon's 'Meridians of Grass' does not attempt to reconcile these selves or arrange them into a coherent arc of growth. It does something quieter and more honest: it walks with them. Haiku, with its devotion to the single perceived moment, turns out to be the ideal form for a consciousness shaped by displacement. Each poem is a small act of arrival — at a pigeon's iridescent neck, at rain rings on a canal, at the particular silence of an acacia at noon. The form does not ask where you came from or where you are going. It asks only what you noticed, and whether you were present enough to let it matter. Simon was not always present. This collection admits that freely. But the nature walk, that oldest and most patient of human rituals, offered a discipline: to move slowly, to look down as often as up, to let the body lead the mind back into the world it had been too busy or too grieved to fully inhabit. Read in sequence, these two hundred and fifty poems trace something like a longitude of the interior — not a journey from innocence to wisdom, but a gradual, nonlinear learning to carry all of one's latitudes at once. Read in fragments, opened at random on a difficult afternoon, they offer what the best haiku has always offered: the small, reliable shock of recognition, the world suddenly present, the self briefly, mercifully, beside the point.

a pigeon's oil-green

neck—I am small enough still

to see this clearly

ginkgo leaf, yellow,

pressed against the third step's edge—

no wind. it stays there.

mural eye still whole

though the wall has split beneath it —

the child keeps watching

duty-free counter:

a perfume called Midnight Rose —

no rose, some midnight

the storm's first cold drop

lands on my bare forearm, slow—

the whole sky shifts weight

protea keeps shut

all that colour practicing

patience in the cold
