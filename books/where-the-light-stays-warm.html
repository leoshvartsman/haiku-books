Where the Light Stays Warm

Riley García
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
There is a kind of knowledge that lives below language — in the body's startled recognition of a particular quality of afternoon light, in the way a smell can return you to a kitchen you haven't stood in for twenty years, in the weight of a stone that fits your palm as though it was waiting. Riley García has spent a life accumulating this knowledge across hemispheres, and Where the Light Stays Warm is its record. These haiku do not document places so much as they document what places do to a person: how they enter through the senses and quietly rearrange the furniture of the self. Moving from the brown-water shores of Río de la Plata through the red clay hills of Virginia, the dust and bougainvillea of Delhi, and finally to the sun-warmed tiles of Guadalajara, García charts a life that refuses to shed its previous geographies. Each new place does not replace the last but layers over it, the way light layers over water. By the final section, the speaker carries all latitudes simultaneously — not as burden but as a hard-won form of wholeness. This is a book about belonging, but not the simple kind. It is about learning to belong to the accumulation itself. Haiku, with its insistence on the single breath, the one true moment, is the ideal form for this project. Each poem here is a flashpoint where distance collapses — where a wall of bougainvillea in Guadalajara becomes, for an instant, a wall in Delhi, and the self standing before it is somehow both people at once. Readers who have ever been remade by a place, or who carry more than one home inside them, will find their own gaps and pins and threshold light somewhere in these pages.

the brown water holds

last light like a bruise going

yellow at the edge

red clay on my boots,

a color I have no word

for in Spanish yet

the call to prayer lifts

what the heat pressed down all night —

I lie still and rise

shadow of a door

crosses the floor by inches —

time, made measurable

the web sags slightly

toward the heavier water —

fog has weight after all

these bougainvillea—

I have touched their paper blooms

in two countries now
