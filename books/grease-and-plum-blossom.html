Grease and Plum Blossom

Ariel Santos

Ariel Santos has spent a life accumulating latitudes. Born in São Paulo to a family whose name arrived in Brazil by way of Germany, he carried that doubled inheritance through workshops in New York, garage bays in Tokyo, and finally to the coast of South Korea, where this book was completed. Grease and Plum Blossom is the record of that passage — not a memoir, not a travelogue, but something closer to a seismograph: small instruments registering the precise tremors of a body moving through the world and being changed by it. The haiku form suits this project with an almost suspicious neatness. Like a man who has worked with his hands, Santos distrusts ornamentation. What he offers instead is attention — to the quality of light in a São Paulo afternoon, to the exact sound of a torque wrench coming to rest, to the particular silence that follows a workshop's polite confusion. Each poem is a moment of contact between a life and a place, and the collection's argument, if it has one, is that such moments of contact are what a life actually consists of. Not the years, not the languages, not even the cities — but the mango splitting, the steam rising from the grate, the coin landing in the cupped hand. The seven sections move roughly chronologically, but time in this book behaves less like a river than like a body of water with currents running in several directions at once. São Paulo keeps surfacing in Tokyo. A German surname echoes through a Korean port. The hands that tighten bolts are the same hands that once pressed against a wooden wall to feel a drum. Readers who follow Santos from section to section will find that the book's true subject is not any single place but the strange, stubborn continuity of a self that persists across all of them — accumulating scars, languages, and a hard-won capacity for stillness.

mango splits open—

red clay takes the sweetness in

before I can reach

his accordion

smells of the man who is gone—

I do not open it

the manuscript page

buckles in the cold, slightly —

Tokyo August—

even the shadows are warm

inside the garage

I crouch to see it:

a whole street reflected, warped—

oil makes better art

the cliff edge, the bloom,

the ship passing far below—

three things, then just one
