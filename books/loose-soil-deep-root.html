Loose Soil, Deep Root

Harper Patel

There is a kind of knowledge that lives below language — in the heel that remembers a particular cobblestone, in the throat that holds the residue of two different grills, in the dark that tells you, by its angle, exactly where someone you love is eating dinner without you. Harper Patel has lived on four continents before the age of twenty-two, and this collection is not about that fact so much as it is about what that fact does to a body: the slow, cellular accumulation of places that never quite leave and a self that never quite arrives. These are not travel poems. They are survival poems, written from the interior. "Loose Soil, Deep Root" moves not through geography but through the seasons of a self still in formation — disorientation, hunger, irony, dread, longing, language, and finally a hard-won, rain-soaked reconciliation. The haiku form is chosen with intention: seventeen syllables is approximately the space available when you have not yet decided which language to feel in. Within that constraint, Patel finds room for dry wit, geological patience, and a tenderness that never announces itself. The poems do not ask for sympathy. They ask for attention. What accumulates in the body when the mind keeps moving? Not nostalgia — nostalgia requires a single origin. What accumulates is something stranger and more durable: a literacy of thresholds, a fluency in departure, the discovery that grief and joy hold the same posture in every latitude. This collection is the record of that discovery, made slowly, made honestly, made in the only form that could hold it without spilling.
