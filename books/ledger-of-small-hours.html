Ledger of Small Hours

Robin Novak

A ledger is a record of what has been exchanged — what came in, what went out, what remains. Robin Novak has spent a career reading ledgers, and this collection is the one she kept for herself: a life measured not in columns but in the small, irreducible moments that columns cannot hold. Across Quito's altitude and Kolkata's low tide, through government offices and pre-dawn job sites and the particular loneliness of a Miami bus, these haiku ask what it costs to move through the world — geographically, professionally, and in the quieter, less mappable sense of becoming someone new while carrying the person you were. 'Ledger of Small Hours' moves through time the way a life actually does: not cleanly, not in straight lines, but with the recurring sensation that something important is being counted and something equally important is being lost, and that these may be the same thing. Novak brings a financial analyst's precision to the work — an eye trained on what is visible, what is recorded, what the numbers refuse to say — and turns that precision inward, toward childhood abundance and the arithmetic of exile, toward bodies worn by labor and a grief that enters the wrong field in a spreadsheet, toward a late stillness that is not peace exactly, but something more honest than peace. The haiku form suits her: spare, exacting, capacious in its silences. This is a collection about currencies that have no symbol — the cost of a name, the weight of altitude in the blood, the slow accumulation of years in a place that was never quite yours. Novak does not resolve these accounts. She leaves the last page ruled and empty, which is its own kind of answer.
