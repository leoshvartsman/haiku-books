Mud on the Threshold

Riley García

There is a particular kind of attention that travel forces on a person — not the wide-eyed attention of the tourist, but something more provisional, more animal. You learn to read a sky you have no history with. You learn which silences are empty and which are full. Riley García's debut collection, *Mud on the Threshold*, is built from that kind of looking: patient, undefended, and earned across four countries and the years it takes to stop performing arrival and simply arrive. These haiku do not romanticize displacement. They hold it steady, the way you might hold a compass in a city where none of the streets run the direction you expect. The collection moves through Prague, New Zealand, Jakarta, and the unnamed spaces between them — workshops, thresholds, laundry lines, the underside of a ceiling where a gecko waits. But geography here is less a setting than a pressure. Each latitude teaches the body something different: how cold a wire can be before a sparrow releases it, how a durian cart can become a kind of company, how fog arrives in Czech before it arrives in English. What García discovers, slowly and without fanfare, is that belonging is not a place you find but a practice you repeat — the daily act of noticing, of saying *I was here and I looked carefully*. The mud on the threshold is not something to clean away. It is evidence of the crossing.
