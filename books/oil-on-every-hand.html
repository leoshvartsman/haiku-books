Oil on Every Hand

Blake Lee
25 sonnets
Download PDF Download EPUB
There is a particular knowledge that lives in the hands — not wisdom exactly, but evidence. Blake Lee's debut collection begins there, in the residue that work leaves behind: grease under the thumbnail, rust read in red and blue across the knuckles, the black that soap and water argue with but cannot win. Lee is a young Arab-American mechanic and poet, and this book moves through four landscapes — Dubai, Vermont, Cairo, Philadelphia — not as a travelogue but as a reckoning with what a body accumulates when it is always, in some sense, arriving. Empire is here, and displacement, and labor, and the strange education of being between things without being lost in them. The sonnet is Lee's chosen form, and the choice is not accidental. The sonnet is a room with rules, a structure that holds pressure the way a gasket holds pressure — because it is pressed between two surfaces that want to part. Lee uses that tension honestly, letting the volta do its work, letting the form ask the questions the speaker is still learning to ask. These are not poems that arrive at easy resolution. They are poems that stay marked by what they've touched, and trust the reader to understand why that marking matters.

Sample Sonnets

The workers came from Bangladesh, Kerala,

from Sindh, from Oromia, from the slow

interior of lives I had no parallel to,

their hands the actual fact of the vertigo

of towers. I am twenty now and trained

to make the line do work the way a wrench

finds torque—in Cairo I was also stained

with grease, I also felt the specific clench

of a bolt that won't give. We ate together

sometimes, lunch on the garage floor, the bread

torn even. I won't make more of it than whether

it was true. It was. They worked. They bled.

The towers stand. The sonnet is a room

I'm building too, and wondering for whom.

She writes in Arabic along the edge

of her syllabus, small haiku no one reads,

the girl across the table at the ledge

of every workshop, trailing off. She bleeds

the margins dry. I recognize the script,

the leftward lean of longing, and I want

to ask her which city she's from, what gripped

her into flight and dropped her in Vermont

among the maples having their loud fits.

But workshops don't allow for this. We talk

about the poem's failure, how it sits

too heavy in the sestet. After, we walk

out separate ways into the orange air.

The marginalia stays. We leave it there.

The Cairo heat is not Dubai's hot glass,

it rises from the ground up through your feet,

through asphalt, through the engine block, the mass

of decades concentrated into heat.

Dubai was cool inside and hot outside,

a managed system, everything controlled,

but Cairo had no thermostat, no guide—

it just was hot the way that blood is old.

I'd stand above the gearbox in the noon

and sweat into the casing, sweat into

the work, become a part of what the room

was doing, which was simply pushing through.

Your body is the first machine you learn.

It also rusts. It also has to turn.
