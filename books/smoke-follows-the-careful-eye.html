Smoke Follows the Careful Eye

Quinn Davies

There is a kind of life that looks, from the outside, like wandering — mill town to metropolis, old world to new, one rented room to another — but that is, from the inside, a sustained act of witness. Quinn Davies has spent decades paying the particular attention that most of us mean to pay and don't. These poems are the record of that attention: rain on Welsh slate, the Tagus going slack at low tide, a hardware store bell ringing into a Canadian January. Each image is small. Each image is exact. Together they add up to something that resembles a life, which is to say something that resists addition and insists on being felt instead. Haiku, as a form, is famously humble — seventeen syllables, one breath, one moment caught before it closes. Davies uses that humility as a kind of discipline, a way of keeping the ego out of the frame so that the world can step in. There are no grand declarations here, no monuments to suffering or joy. There is soot on a windowsill. There is a cormorant holding its wings open to August. There is a bottle cap turning in a current. The ecological grief is real; so is the tenderness for a clerk who knows your name. Davies does not resolve the tension between these things. The tension is the book. 'Smoke Follows the Careful Eye' moves roughly chronologically — from the industrial north of England outward through displacement and hunger, through sun and estuary, through grief and granite, into a small Canadian town where something like peace becomes possible without ever becoming simple. Readers who follow that arc will find it honest and unhurried. But these poems also hold up individually, each one a complete act of looking. Open to any page. Something will be waiting there, neither dirty nor clean yet — the word, as Davies writes, for between.
