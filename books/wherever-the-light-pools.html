Wherever the Light Pools

Parker Wilson
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
A haiku is, among other things, a way of paying attention — a small act of witness that says: this moment happened, and I was in it. Parker Wilson's 'Wherever the Light Pools' collects 250 such acts of witness across four decades and four time zones, tracing a life not as a narrative arc but as a series of precise instants: the color of ice at dusk, the sound of a first monsoon on tin roofing, the particular silence of a studio at four in the afternoon. Geography here is not backdrop. It is the medium through which the self is shaped and reshaped — the way granite coastline teaches a different kind of patience than tropical rain, the way a city's light at midnight asks different questions than a frozen lake at dawn. The collection moves roughly chronologically, from the wide-open winters of a Maine childhood through the identity-making years of young adulthood in Britain, into the vertigo and aliveness of Bangalore, and finally toward the bittersweet strangeness of Los Angeles — a city that calls itself arrival. But the deeper journey is interior: a reckoning with names chosen and names inherited, with the distances between landscapes and the distances between selves. Wilson is interested in what persists across those distances — what the light does when it finds you, wherever you have traveled to become yourself. Readers familiar with the haiku tradition will recognize its classical commitments here: the cut, the season word held lightly, the image that opens rather than explains. But Wilson works in a distinctly contemporary register, one that trusts the form to hold autobiography without collapsing into confession, to hold displacement without collapsing into elegy. These poems do not resolve the contradictions of a life lived across cultures and identities. They hold them — the way ice holds a crack, the way a canvas holds wet paint — with patience, and with care.

frozen lake at dusk—

the color no paint can name

settles into ice

my Hindi name-form,

Parker, spoken in Bangalore—

the same mouth, new air

pub table, sticky,

the napkin where I draft it—

five, seven, and five

first rains and the earth

gives up a smell it has held

since October's end

four o'clock arrives

the walls change their mind about

what color they are

midnight freeway—

a truck full of roses going

somewhere before dawn
