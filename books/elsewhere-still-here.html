Elsewhere Still Here

Robin Patel

Every place we have ever lived leaves a residue. Not just memory — something more physical than that: a particular quality of light behind the eyelids, the temperature at which cold becomes cold, the precise pitch of a city's silence at 4am. Robin Patel has lived inside many such residues, carrying Prague winters and Tokyo neon and Athens dust and New Zealand mud in the same body, the same throat, the same pair of hands. Elsewhere Still Here is the record of that accumulation — not a travelogue, not a memoir, but something closer to a cartography of the self as it is shaped, and reshaped, by the places that claim it. Haiku, with its insistence on the immediate and the sensory, turns out to be the perfect form for this kind of reckoning. These poems do not explain or argue. They hold a vending machine button, a grandmother's fogged window, a boot releasing from mud, and trust that the body of the reader will know what the body of the poet knew. The seventeen syllables become a kind of common ground — neutral territory where a Czech word and a Greek ache and an English sheep-count can briefly coexist without resolution. The pandemic arrives in the middle of this collection as it arrived in the middle of everything: abruptly, reordering the terms. For a life assembled from departures, the sudden impossibility of leaving proved a strange and clarifying rupture. What remained when movement stopped was not emptiness but accumulation — all the elsewheres pressing in at once, insisting on being counted. This book is that counting. It does not end in arrival so much as in a kind of companionable uncertainty, the discovery that belonging nowhere and everywhere are, in the end, nearly the same thing.
