What the Canals Remember

Morgan Lewis

Morgan Lewis spent half a century learning to argue precisely — to build a case from evidence, to make language do exactly what it was told. What the Canals Remember is what happened when that discipline finally broke open. These two hundred and fifty haiku move through a life in the way a life actually moves: not in chapters with clean transitions, but in flashes of heat and paperwork and water, in the sudden recognition that a lizard on a limestone shelf and a canal in November are, somehow, the same lesson. The book is organized as a journey — Texas to New York to Miami to Amsterdam — but it is less a travelogue than a sediment core, each layer pressing down on the one beneath it, the whole column telling a story no single layer could. Haiku arrived late, as the best teachers often do. There is something almost perverse about a litigator choosing the form: a career built on accumulation, on the weight of precedent and the architecture of argument, surrendering to seventeen syllables and the discipline of leaving almost everything out. But perhaps it is not perverse at all. Perhaps the legal mind, trained to find the one fact that changes everything, was always suited to this — to the single image that cracks a moment open and lets the light through. Lewis has said that writing haiku felt less like learning a new language than like finally being allowed to speak in the one that was always underneath. What holds this collection together is not theme but texture: the specific gravity of places that have been truly inhabited, the way a climate gets into the body and stays there, the long patience of someone who has learned to wait for what is true to surface on its own. There is grief here, and humor, and the particular tenderness of a person who has loved several cities without ever quite belonging to any of them. There is also, in the final pages, something rarer — a gratitude so carefully stripped of sentiment that it arrives as fact, the way good evidence does.
