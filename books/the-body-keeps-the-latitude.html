The Body Keeps the Latitude

Harper Martin

The body is a better archivist than the mind. It does not organize by date or sentiment or the story you have decided to tell about yourself — it files by sensation, by reflex, by the angle of a wrist over a kettle, by the way the shoulders move before the brain has named what it smells. Harper Martin's debut collection begins from this premise and does not let go: that the places we have lived, the people we have lost, the languages we have loved imperfectly, do not disappear when we leave them. They go somewhere quieter. They go into the body, and they wait. 'The Body Keeps the Latitude' is not a collection about immigration or grief or belonging in any simple sense, though it moves through all of these. It is a collection about the intelligence of the physical — the knowledge stored in appetite and muscle, in the tongue's hesitation over a vowel, in the hands that shape dough according to a recipe that was never written down because it was never meant to outlive the hands that held it. These haiku do not argue for any particular relationship to place or origin. They observe, with the precision that the form demands, what the body does without being asked. What it knows. What it refuses to forget. The collection moves in seven sections, tracing an arc that is not quite a journey and not quite a homecoming — more like the slow, patient work of learning to live in a body that carries more than one world. Readers who know this feeling will find themselves recognized here in ways that may be difficult to name. Readers who do not will find a door left open. Martin's haiku are brief the way wounds are brief: the event is over quickly; the rest takes longer.
