Where the Compass Hesitates

Sage García
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
A compass hesitates not from failure but from sensitivity — it trembles at the threshold between certainties, registering forces the steady hand cannot feel. This collection is built from such trembling. Sage García grew up between the continents that the Bosphorus holds apart, learned language from the layered noise of Istanbul's waterfront, and then spent a decade aboard vessels where the horizon was the only fixed thing and the body learned to read weather before the mind could name it. These haiku are the residue of that education: brief, salt-stripped, saturated with the specific gravity of places that refuse to stay in the past. Haiku has always been a form for the threshold moment — the instant before the frog leaps, after the bell stops ringing, when the season turns on a single image. García brings to that tradition a life lived across genuine thresholds: between cultures, between hemispheres, between motion and stillness. The compression of the form does not simplify these crossings. It intensifies them, the way a narrow strait intensifies a current. Each poem holds more water than it appears to contain. The collection moves in rough biographical arc — childhood, formation, sea years, landfall, settlement — but time here is not linear so much as tidal. Istanbul surfaces in a New Zealand downpour. A ship's compass rose appears under a cracked bulb in a Wellington harbour shed. The body that learned to brace against Southern Ocean swells finds itself, years later, still bracing in a flat farmhouse at three in the morning. What García traces is not a journey from there to here but the slow, imperfect, ongoing work of becoming someone who can hold all of it at once — who can stand, at last, without needing the ground to stop moving.

blinks green through the harbour mouth—

timber, aggregate, steel—

the water reads none

the fence runs uphill

until I can't see where it

ends or who it serves

she used to read mine,
