The Slow Work of Light

Harper Smith

There is a kind of change that does not announce itself. It happens not at borders or thresholds, not in the bright punctuation of arrivals and departures, but in the long unpunctuated middle — in the ten-thousandth ordinary morning, the gesture repeated until the body no longer needs to think, the light falling at the same angle it fell thirty years ago on a different wall. Harper Smith's The Slow Work of Light is a book about that kind of change: the kind that can only be seen in retrospect, the way you might notice a tree has grown by standing under it and finding more shade than you remembered. These haiku were written across decades and across continents — markets in Delhi and Austin, doorways in Oslo and unnamed cities, rooftops at dusk, lamp-lit streets in November rain — but they are not, finally, a book about places. They are a book about attention: what it costs, what it yields, and what becomes possible when a person stops moving fast enough to actually see. At sixty-eight, Smith writes with the particular quality of someone who has learned, slowly and not without difficulty, to read the present tense. The poems do not reach for transcendence. They stay close to the ground — to limestone and gecko shadow, to the worn thumb of a chai-wallah, to the small ceremony of straightening a shoe before sleep — and trust that the ground is enough. The collection moves in eight sections, each a different angle of light on the same abiding question: how does a life accumulate, and what does it leave behind? Readers will find no dramatic arc, no crisis resolved. What they will find is something rarer — a sustained quality of noticing, offered without agenda, in the form that has always been most honest about the limits of what language can hold.
