Dust on the Lotus

Jun Ito

Jun Ito retired from banking at sixty-eight, and for eleven years did nothing so dramatic as disappear. Then, at seventy-nine, he walked onto a red dirt road in rural Rajasthan with a notebook and no particular plan, and began writing the poems collected here. 'Dust on the Lotus' is the record of that walk — not only the physical one, taken in the long heat of an Indian afternoon, but the longer walk backward and forward through a life shaped by four places: the moss-garden quietness of Kyoto where he was formed, the harbour brightness of Melbourne where he remade himself, the particular silence of Hiroshima that has no adequate name, and the red earth of a country he arrived in late and received with open hands. Haiku has always understood that the smallest moment contains the largest truth — that a bead on an abacus, a rusted stapler, a gecko's pulsing throat can hold what years of careful language cannot. Ito works in this tradition without nostalgia for it, finding in the seventeen-syllable form not a constraint but a kind of mercy: the permission to stop before explanation, to leave the weight of a thing exactly where it fell. These poems do not argue for any conclusion about age, grief, memory, or arrival. They simply look, with the particular steadiness of a man who has learned that looking is enough. The book moves in seven sections, loosely chronological in feeling if not in fact — from the quiet release of a working life set down, through the layered territories of memory, into the alert, unhurried attention of a body slowing into its final landscape. Readers who expect resolution will find instead something richer: the gradual recognition that a life accumulated is not weight to be shed but soil, dark and patient, in which something continues, without announcement, to grow.

Sample Haiku

dust on the abacus —

the last bead has not moved

since my daughter's birth

in the bamboo grove

I counted stalks like columns

in a ledger, once

tram stop, no one else—

the drizzle makes its small sound

on my foreign coat

I leave early now

to pass the desk before light —

easier that way

chai smoke, neem smoke, dung smoke

the morning layers itself—

I breathe all three in

wild marigold bends

under a bee's small landing —

the field does not care
