Light Coming Through

Taylor Smith
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
Taylor Smith has spent nine decades looking. Not accumulating, not summarizing — looking. This collection is the residue of that practice: 250 haiku gathered from a life that moved between hemispheres, between languages, between the cold clarity of a Canadian winter and the dry, insistent light of a Santiago afternoon. There is no continuous narrative here, no arc of triumph or loss. What there is, instead, is a quality of attention that has been refined over a very long time into something very small and very exact. Light Coming Through is organized loosely by chapter of life, but it resists the pull of memoir. The poems do not explain or interpret; they observe. A pair of wool mittens stiffened on a line. Cadmium yellow loaded onto a knife. A fishmonger's thumb pressing a gill. These are not symbols. They are the things themselves, held still for a moment before the light shifts. That the same consciousness moves through all of them — from childhood to the edge of ninety — is felt rather than argued, the way you feel a continuous thread when you run your hand along it in the dark. Readers looking for a summing-up will need to look elsewhere. What Smith offers is something rarer: the record of a self that never stopped being curious about the world it found itself in. The haiku form, with its demand for compression and its tolerance for incompleteness, turns out to be the perfect container for this kind of wisdom — not because it resolves anything, but because it refuses to. Each poem is a window. The light coming through is always a little different. The looking is always the same.

Sample Haikus

wool mittens stiffened

on the line overnight—hands

I cannot get back

a seminar ends,

chairs scraping back from the table—

the silence after

the fire escape holds

its usual parliament—

I don't disturb them

canvas to the wall:

its back is plain as Tuesday—

I prefer it now

the plaza at dusk:

dogs, pigeons, one old man, me —

all of us staying

the hill's shadow falls

across a child's chalk drawing —

the chalk stays vivid
