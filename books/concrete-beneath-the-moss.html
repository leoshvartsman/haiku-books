Concrete Beneath the Moss

Casey White
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
There is a crack in almost every sidewalk, and something is growing in it. Casey White's debut collection begins with that simple, stubborn fact — that nature does not hold itself apart from cities, waiting for us to come looking. It is already here, in the hailstone cooling on asphalt, in the gecko paused mid-wall, in the sulfur rising through a Reykjavik street grate like a reminder from below. Concrete Beneath the Moss traces one poet's path across three cities — Denver, Hanoi, Reykjavik — not as a traveler collecting impressions, but as a body learning, slowly, to pay attention to the world it keeps landing in. The collection moves in the shape of a year of waking up. It begins restless and half-formed, full of the particular ache of a young person who senses that something is happening just at the edge of their perception. It travels through disorientation and overwhelm, through the slow education of the senses that comes from living somewhere strange, and arrives — without fanfare, without resolution — at a kind of earned quiet. The haiku form suits this arc precisely. Each poem is a moment of contact: between eye and object, between self and city, between the human need to name things and the world's patient refusal to be fully named. What White discovers across these three cities is not that nature is everywhere in the same way, but that it is everywhere in its own way — and that the self, too, is a kind of wild thing, taking root in whatever crack it finds. The moss does not wait for permission. Neither, this book quietly argues, should we.
