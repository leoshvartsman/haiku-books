What Remains After the Thaw

Casey Müller
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
There is a particular kind of exposure that comes not from catastrophe but from its slow ending. When the cold finally breaks, what it releases is not clean — it is everything that was preserved in place, still holding the shape of the moment it froze. Casey Müller has spent a working life in the presence of harm: documenting it, measuring it, writing it into the official record. These poems are not that record. They are what accumulated in the margins. Müller writes from a body that has lived in four countries and learned, in each of them, a different grammar of belonging — Baltic granite, Icelandic steam, Parisian stone, the unnamed interiors of bureaucratic fluorescence. The haiku form suits this kind of knowledge. It does not explain. It arrives, registers, and holds still long enough to be felt. What emerges across these eight sections is less a memoir than an inventory: of what the professional eye trained itself not to see, of what the cellular self refused to forget, of what a middle-aged body carries without having been asked. This is a collection about survival, but not in the heroic sense. It is about the quieter survival of small recognitions — a fromagerie gesture, a gate-latch scar, a stamp kept clean and unstuck in a drawer. Müller does not ask where they have been. They ask, with patience and without self-pity, what remains. The answer, it turns out, is considerable.
