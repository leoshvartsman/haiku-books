# Introduction Welcome to this collection of 250 haiku that celebrate the turning wheel of seasons and the fleeting moments that define our days. Whether you're a longtime lover of this ancient Japanese form or discovering haiku for the first time, these verses invite you to pause, breathe, and notice the world with fresh eyes. Haiku, in its traditional form, captures a single moment in nature through three lines of seventeen syllables (5-7-5). Born in Japan centuries ago, this deceptively simple poetry seeks to illuminate the profound within the ordinary—a dewdrop catching morning light, the sound of wind through autumn leaves, the silence after snowfall. The best haiku create what Japanese poets call "aha moments," sudden flashes of recognition or wonder that connect us more deeply to the natural world and our place within it. This collection blends traditional approaches with contemporary sensibilities, honoring haiku's roots while allowing the form to breathe and evolve. Some poems here follow classical patterns precisely; others bend the rules in service of capturing truth. All were generated and carefully curated through an AI-assisted process, representing a unique collaboration between human sensitivity and artificial intelligence—a modern twist on an ancient art. The haiku are organized seasonally, beginning with spring's awakening and moving through summer's abundance, autumn's transformation, and winter's quiet reflection. This structure mirrors the traditional Japanese saijiki, or seasonal almanac, which has guided haiku composition for generations. Each season brings its own palette of images, emotions, and natural phenomena, creating a year-long journey through the cycles that shape our lives. You might approach this collection in several ways. Read it straight through as a meditation on time's passage, or let it fall open randomly and see what moment speaks to you today. Keep it nearby for quick dips into mindfulness between busy hours, or use it as inspiration for writing your own haiku. Some readers prefer to match their reading to the actual season, while others enjoy the contrast of reading winter poems on summer days. However you choose to engage with these verses, remember that haiku rewards slow reading. Let each poem settle before moving to the next. Notice which images linger in your mind, which lines make you pause and look out the window, which moments remind you of your own experiences with the natural world. In our fast-paced digital age, haiku offers something precious: permission to slow down, to notice, to find meaning in the smallest observations. May these 250 moments of captured time bring you closer to the quiet wonders that surround us always, waiting to be seen. *Riley Kowalski* Spring arrives not as a gentle whisper but as a symphony of contrasts—the delicate and the industrial, the wild and the cultivated, the innocent and the complex. In urban landscapes, the season's awakening takes on a unique character, where jasmine vines thread through chain-link fences and children's sidewalk art blooms alongside actual flowers. These haiku capture spring's particular magic in the city, where nature's persistence creates unexpected poetry. Here, the sweetness of jasmine doesn't exist in pastoral isolation but mingles boldly with bus exhaust, creating a distinctly modern perfume. Children's chalk birds—those "impossible" creatures of imagination—share space with climbing vines, reminding us that creativity and growth are kindred spirits. This is spring as most of us actually encounter it: not in pristine gardens but in the margins and crevices of daily life, where beauty asserts itself with quiet determination. These moments ask us to pause and notice how the season transforms even the most ordinary urban corners into places of wonder.

Bus exhaust mingles

with jasmine from hidden yards

in the narrow lanes

The subway musician

plays Chopin with missing keys—

commuters slow down

Antique shop displays

music boxes, broken clocks

time for sale, slightly used

Street musician's case

holds coins from seven countries

melody needs none

Shoe repair shop holds

decades of walking stories—

leather memory bank

carries birthday cake through rain

sweetness under plastic
