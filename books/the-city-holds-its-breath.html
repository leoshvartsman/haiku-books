The City Holds Its Breath

Robin Sato
25 sonnets
Download PDF Download EPUB
These sonnets were written across four decades and four cities, though not always in the cities they describe. Memory has its own geography — its own water table, its own fault lines — and Robin Sato has spent a career measuring the ground beneath places while the places themselves kept changing. The poems gathered here follow that double work: the outward work of an environmental surveyor moving through Hanoi, Kyoto, Bangalore, and Dubai, and the inward work of a person trying to understand what a city leaves in you long after you have left it. The sonnet felt like the right instrument for this. It is a form that holds pressure, that asks a question and then lives inside the question for fourteen lines before arriving somewhere that is not quite an answer. The cities in these poems are not symbols or arguments. They are places with specific smells and specific soils, specific ways of flooding and specific ways of enduring. What Sato asks — quietly, persistently — is what survives the act of carrying a place away from itself. What gets lost in translation between the ground and the person who once stood on it. The book moves roughly in the order of a life, though it does not tell that life as a story. Readers will find their footing quickly enough. The main thing to know is that these poems trust the particular: the jar sweating on a kitchen shelf, the crow on a flooded parking structure, the name hung on a wooden board in a foreign script. The large questions are here, but they arrive through the small ones, the way water finds its level through whatever cracks are available.

Sample Sonnets

I am not the person who was born in Hanoi,

not entirely. I have taken other names,

lived in the grammar of three other cities, employed

my hands in different work, slept under different flames

of stars. And yet the jar sweats in my memory

with a fidelity no other image holds —

the clay, the cold water, the shadowed inventory

of that kitchen, my grandmother's hands, the folds

of her ao dai against the tile floor.

The self is a water table: what you draw

from it depends on what the season, what the more-

than-conscious need is. I draw now. I draw

from fifty-eight years of silt and solitude.

The water comes up red. It tastes of childhood. It is good.

November and the Kamo runs its grey

between the stones as if it has no rush,

the herons posted where the shallows fray

to silver thread beneath the evening's hush.

I'd come from Hanoi's Red River, wide and loud,

from flood-season urgency and silt and noise,

and here the water moved beneath a cloud

with something close to what I'd call restraint, or poise.

A woman fed her dog beside the bridge.

Two students passed with bundled winter coats.

The city held its breath along the ridge

of dusk, and I was one of the unmarked notes

in someone else's evening, someone else's town,

watching the river carry nothing down.

What I trust in this city is the salt—

not the towers, not the eight-lane roads,

not the glass that takes no part in fault,

but the salt that underlies the loads.

The Gulf puts it in everything: the air,

the concrete slowly weeping at its base,

the ghaf root finding it and saying here,

the nurse's scrubs, the prayer's unheld space.

In Hanoi the Red River laid its silt,

in Kyoto the moss held mineral rain,

in Bangalore the red earth carried guilt

and iron both, the two of them the same.

Each city writes itself into the ground.

I've followed the inscription. I have found.
