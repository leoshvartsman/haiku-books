The Tide Keeps Different Time

Casey Smith
25 sonnets
Download PDF Download EPUB
A life is not lived in one place, even when it looks that way from the outside. Casey Smith's debut collection moves through four cities and several decades, tracing what accumulates in a person who has been held, briefly or for years, by Lisbon's tiled facades, New York's workshop rooms, a Scottish peat bog in November, and the long blue winters of Oslo. These are not travel poems. They are poems about what geography does to the self when the self is paying attention — how a place enters the body and stays there, becomes a kind of grammar you didn't know you were learning until you find yourself thinking in it. The sonnets gathered here are formal but not stiff, rooted in a tradition the collection both honors and argues with. Smith brings Portuguese saudade into English lines that were not quite built to hold it, lets the trumpet through the workshop wall, lets the peat bog have the last word. The result is a book that moves from inherited longing through restless formation toward something harder to name — a stillness that has been earned, that knows exactly what it cost, and that is not quite the same thing as peace, but is close enough to live in.

Sample Sonnets

The fado singer holds the note until

the note becomes the room, and then the room

becomes the river, and the river fills

with everything that left and didn't come

back whole—the caravels, the emigrants,

the children grown to strangers in a north

their parents named with hope, the consonants

of saudade that have no English worth

the saying. I am one of those who left

and made a north of it, who learned that March

in Oslo drinks the same gold, the same bereft

light from the water. Nothing fills the arch

of what is gone. The fado doesn't say return.

It says: admit the thing you cannot unmourn.

Someone was working through a Miles Davis

transcription on the other side of that

rehearsal wall, the trumpet thin and nervous

against the plaster, and I sat

with my first draft bleeding under rings

of coffee, thinking how the break

between the note and silence, where it sings

because of what it doesn't say, would make

a better sonnet than the one I'd written —

all that careful rhyme, the argument

too tidy at the seam, not split, not bitten

through by something actual, something meant.

The trumpet stopped. The silence had more weight.

I crossed out seven lines. I started late.

November on the bog smells like the end

of something patient that has been at work

since long before the forestry, the bend

of road, the pylons where the buzzards lurk.

I walked the edge of it on lunch one day,

a Tuesday with the van in for repair,

and felt the ground move slightly, give and sway —

not dangerous, just honest: nothing there

is fixed the way a city fixes things

with concrete and intention and a plan.

The bog remembers water. Water brings

itself back, always, outlasting the van,

the volunteer, the grant, the parish hall.

I stood there on its breathing. That was all.
