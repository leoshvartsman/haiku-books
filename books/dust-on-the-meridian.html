Dust on the Meridian

Morgan Taylor
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
A meridian is a line of reckoning — the point at which the sun stands highest, the axis from which longitude is measured, the seam a navigator draws across the world to know where they are. Dust on the Meridian asks what happens when a life crosses several such lines and finds that none of them quite agree. These two hundred and fifty haiku move through four places — London, Norwich, the Australian Outback, Lyon — not as a travelogue but as a body's slow account of what it means to be held by a landscape, and then to leave it, and then to wonder what held you at all. The book's central preoccupation is architectural as much as ecological: how do things stay up? How does a building negotiate with the ground beneath it, how does a reed bed absorb the weight of a hunting bird, how does a person learn to read belonging through the soles of their feet rather than through any act of will? Morgan Taylor, writing from a life that has moved between drawing boards and red dust and rain-grey stone, finds in the haiku form something structurally honest — a load-bearing minimum, a form that holds by refusing to hold too much. Each poem is a footing, a junction, a point where two materials meet and agree, provisionally, to bear weight together. The collection does not resolve into arrival. Its title carries that ambiguity deliberately: dust settles on a meridian line, obscures it, makes the reckoning harder. To read these poems in sequence is to accompany a consciousness that is always, in some sense, still calibrating — measuring the distance between the place the body remembers and the place the body stands, finding that distance not a failure but a kind of precision. What the book offers, finally, is not belonging but something more durable: attention, which is the practice of belonging to wherever you actually are.
