Borrowed Skies

Cruz Ruiz

There is a kind of belonging that never quite arrives — not because it is withheld, but because the person seeking it has already become too many people, has already learned the grammar of too many streets. Cruz Ruiz has lived in four cities, worked under four different skies, and carried a name that clerks read twice. 'Borrowed Skies' is the record of that carrying: not a lament, not a triumph, but something more patient than either — an accounting of what the body retains when it has passed through places that did not keep it, and what the spirit makes from the residue. These haiku do not ask where home is. That question, Ruiz seems to have decided, is the wrong instrument for the measurement. Instead they ask what a person is made of after the cities have finished with them — what weight lives in a name, what ceremony survives relocation, what the hands remember after the scaffolding comes down. Moving from the dreamy unease of earliest memory through the fractures of identity, the dignity of physical labor, the discipline of calligraphy, the hush of ritual, and the long suspension of a life lived between shores, the collection arrives finally at something that is not resolution so much as release: an open hand, a window left unlatched, a name worn smooth enough to be held without flinching. Readers who have belonged nowhere completely will recognize the terrain. But Ruiz writes with enough precision and enough tenderness that the recognition is never merely consolation. These are poems that ask you to look carefully at what you have been circling — and to consider that the circling itself may be the home.
