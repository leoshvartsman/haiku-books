The Distance Between Mangoes

Ishaan Chopra

There is a particular kind of knowledge that lives below language — in the weight of humidity before a monsoon breaks, in the exact pressure required to test a ripe peach, in the way the body orients itself toward a sea it has not seen in decades. Ishaan Chopra's debut collection, The Distance Between Mangoes, is built from this knowledge. Moving across Mumbai, Toronto, Barcelona, and finally Madrid, these haiku do not chart a life so much as take its temperature — again and again, in different climates, in different skins. The haiku form suits this project with an almost suspicious precision. Its compression mirrors the compression of the immigrant self, the traveler's self, the self that has learned to carry what matters in the smallest possible vessel. Chopra works within the tradition while pressing quietly against it: his syllables sometimes strain, his seasons are not always Bashō's seasons, and occasionally a Spanish word or a crossed-out Hindi line breaks the surface like something that refused to stay submerged. These are not failures of form. They are the form, faithfully recording what it actually costs to move through the world in more than one language, more than one body, more than one version of home. What holds the collection together is not nostalgia, though nostalgia is present, nor is it resolution, though something like peace arrives in the final pages. What holds it together is attention — the sustained, almost devotional act of noticing that Chopra practices across seven decades and seven sections. The distance between mangoes, it turns out, is not a distance in miles. It is the gap between what the body knows and what the mind can say. These poems live in that gap. They ask you to live there too, briefly, and to find it — as Chopra himself seems to have found it — less like exile and more like a room you have finally stopped apologizing for.
