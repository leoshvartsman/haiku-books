What the Soil Keeps

Sage Clark
20 sonnets
Download PDF Download EPUB
A gardener learns to tell time differently. Not by the clock or the calendar but by what is happening in the soil, on the branch, in the quality of light at a particular hour of a particular month. Sage Clark has spent a life in that kind of time — working ground in Texas, Sydney, Paris, Tuscany — and these sonnets are the record of it: not a memoir exactly, but something closer to what a gardener does when the season ends and she walks the beds one last time, noting what came through and what did not. The form here is deliberate. The sonnet is a container, and Clark uses it the way a gardener uses a wall: not to imprison but to train, to give shape to what would otherwise reach in every direction and exhaust itself. Each poem carries what the Japanese call a kigo — a seasonal word, a marker that locates you in the turning year — because Clark believes, as the old poets did, that to name the season is to name something true about the self who is living inside it. Jacaranda. Espalier. Persimmon. Firefly. These are not decorations. They are the poem's roots. What the Soil Keeps moves, roughly, as a life moves: from the unformed heat of a Southern childhood through the hunger of early apprenticeship, the long discipline of the middle years, and into the spacious, wondering attention of late life. But seasons do not move in only one direction, and neither do these poems. What you will find here is not a tidy arc but something more like a garden in late October — evidence of everything that grew, and grew back, and was cut, and grew again.

Sample Sonnets

The jacaranda bruised the pavement blue

before I understood what blue could mean—

not Austin sky, not Paris morning dew,

but something in between what's felt and seen.

November heat in Sydney pressed like hands

across my shoulders as I crossed the square,

the petals soft as any reprimand,

the smell of them both rotten sweet and rare.

I'd come to learn the kigo of this place,

to name the season in a stranger's tongue,

to press my thumb into the violet lace

and find a poem rising, green and young.

The bruise stayed on my fingers half the day.

I could not wash what I had touched away.

The pear against the wall holds nothing back—

each branch wired flat against the January stone,

spread wide as something caught, pinned to the black

and freezing dark, disciplined, and alone.

I did this work. I chose the angle, drove

each nail, each eye-hook into Paris grey,

and coaxed the lateral shoots until they wove

their patient argument against the day.

The tree does not resist. It only grows

in the direction it is given, slow

and faithful to the wire's will. One knows,

after thirty years, what hands can show:

that beauty trained to surface, spread this thin,

is still a form of beauty flowering in.

Midsummer midnight and I cannot name

the species of this quiet—not the hush

of cellar cold, not Sydney's harbour-flame

at dusk, not Austin evenings where the brush

of cedar pollen made the air turn gold.

This is a quiet that has traveled through

six centuries of stone and hasn't cooled,

a warmth held in the walls the whole day through

that breathes out slowly now against my arm

as I lean on the sill. The fireflies

complete their indecipherable charm

below. The fig tree rustles. Something sighs

or it's the vine. I have no need to know.

The summer night is full. I let it go.
