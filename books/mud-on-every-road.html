Mud on Every Road

Jordan Lee
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
Jordan Lee has lived in four cities on three continents, and the mud of each one has found its way onto every road. This collection is not a travel book, though it crosses oceans. It is not a memoir, though it spans a life. It is something closer to what the body remembers when the mind has stopped insisting on a story: the weight of a seed pressed into soil, the particular way steam rises through a city grate, the color a dried chili turns when summer has been gone long enough. These haiku were written across decades and landscapes, but they arrive here as a single education — the slow, often comic, occasionally devastating process of learning where you are by learning what your hands already know. Belonging, Lee suggests, is not a coordinate. It is a quality of attention, something that can be practiced anywhere and lost anywhere, something that has more to do with the angle of afternoon light than with the language spoken beneath it. The four cities named in these pages — Kathmandu, New York, Guadalajara, Paris — are less destinations than conditions, each one a different pressure applied to the same material: a body trying to be present, trying to read the world through its own accumulated calluses and confusions. The mud is different everywhere. The road is always the same road. Readers will find no strict chronology here, only a loose arc from early rootedness through displacement and hard-won stillness toward something the final section can only call spaciousness — a word that earns its meaning by the time you reach it. Each section moves at its own pace, the way seasons do, arriving before you are ready and leaving before you are done. Bring whatever landscape you carry. It will be recognized.
