Red Dirt, Neon Rain

Casey Walker
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
A life is not one place. It is the accumulation of soils underfoot — the red clay that cracks in forty-degree heat, the peat that gives a little with every step, the black volcanic loam that smells of something older than memory, and finally the sealed, indifferent concrete of a city that never asked you to arrive. Casey Walker has lived across all of these surfaces, and this collection is the record of that passage: not a memoir, not a confession, but a series of small precise observations gathered over eight decades of paying attention. Haiku, with its insistence on the exact and the immediate, turns out to be the right form for a life lived in transit — each poem a single moment of ground beneath the feet, sky overhead, and a body registering both. The title comes from two kinds of weather: the red dirt of the Australian interior where Walker grew up, and the neon-lit rain of a Tokyo alley encountered decades later. Between those two images lies everything — a name changed, a climate endured, a farm worked, a movement served, a city absorbed. The poems do not arrange these experiences into argument or resolution. They sit beside one another the way strangers sit on a long train journey: separately, and yet going somewhere together. Readers will find in these pages not a tidy arc but something more honest — the texture of an actual life, with its loneliness and solidarity, its losses and its stubborn, recurring wonder. Walker has said that the haiku form taught a discipline that took years to learn: resist the explanation, trust the image, let the silence after the poem do its own work. That discipline is everywhere in this collection. These are not poems that tell you what to feel. They are poems that place something in front of you — a crow, a thermos, a crossed-out name, a teapot set on its corner of bench — and step back. What rises in you as you look is yours. That is the gift, and the invitation.
