Altitude and Ash

Sacha Martín

Download PDF Download EPUB
There is a particular kind of seeing that belongs to altitude — clean, geometric, stripped of the textures that make the earth difficult to love and impossible to leave. Sacha Martín has spent a working life in that suspended position, above weather, above borders, above the slow emergency unfolding on the ground below. Altitude and Ash is the reckoning that position demands: a pilot's attempt to reconcile the long view with the close one, to bring what is witnessed from four hundred feet back down to the level of a knee scraped on salt rock, a petal on hot concrete, seeds pressed against a ribcage at a border crossing. The collection moves in a rough arc — from a childhood coast saturated with sensory life, through the threshold years of first flight and the quiet estrangement of routes over Japan, to the gravity of Hiroshima, the restorative solitude of Vermont woods, and finally the layered, bittersweet ground of Rome. But the arc is not a simple ascent and return. It doubles back on itself, the way grief does, the way responsibility does. The haiku form — spare, imagistic, committed to the present moment — becomes here a kind of instrument panel: each poem a reading, a small calibration, a way of asking what the gauges know that the pilot does not yet. What Martín is ultimately navigating is a question that belongs to this particular century: what does it mean to witness a burning world from above, and to land in it anyway? Altitude and Ash does not offer an answer so much as a practice — the sustained, honest act of looking, from every elevation, with both eyes open.
