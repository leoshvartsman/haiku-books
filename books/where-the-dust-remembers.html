Where the Dust Remembers

Robin Lee
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
There is a kind of knowledge that lives below language — in the body's memory of a particular heat, in the way a certain quality of light makes the chest tighten before the mind has named the reason. Where the Dust Remembers is a life mapped not by dates or distances but by sensation: the grit of a Cairo khamsin still lodged in the teeth years later, the deliberate silence of a Kyoto temple that asks nothing and receives everything, the physical reckoning of Dubai's agricultural fields where exhaustion and grace arrive in the same hour, the fog of San Francisco that softens edges without erasing them. These haiku move through four landscapes as a traveler moves through four seasons — not leaving one behind to enter the next, but carrying each forward into the body like sediment, like vocabulary, like scar. The question this collection refuses to answer is where home is. The question it keeps asking, in image after image, is what the body knows that the passport does not. Robin Lee writes from the tradition of the kigo — the seasonal word that anchors a haiku to a specific moment in the turning world — but expands that tradition across hemispheres and decades, so that a jacaranda bloom and a raked gravel garden and a date palm at harvest and a San Francisco hummingbird can all be understood as seasonal markers of a single, continuous life. To read these poems in order is to make a journey. To read them again is to realize you were always already home, or that home was always already this: a grain of sand on the page, the pen moving around it as if it were planned.

still warm against my boot sole—

I do not step back

exhales last February's

warmth into July

wet eucalyptus—
