Soil Remembers Every Foot

Jordan Lee
25 sonnets
Download PDF Download EPUB
There is a kind of knowledge that lives below language — in the hands, in the lungs, in the particular way a body learns to read weather or altitude or the smell of a kitchen before the rest of the house is awake. Jordan Lee has spent a life accumulating that knowledge across four landscapes, and this book is the record of what it costs and what it keeps. The title is not a metaphor so much as a fact: soil does remember. So does the body that has worked it. These sonnets move through Kathmandu's terraced hills, New York's workshop winters, Guadalajara's long agricultural seasons, and Paris's stone-banked river — not as a travelogue, but as an inquiry. What do we carry when we leave? What do we lose that we didn't know we were carrying? Lee writes with the patience of someone who has learned to read a field before speaking about it, and the form itself — the sonnet's old architecture of turn and return — becomes a kind of home the poet can rebuild in any language, on any ground. Readers who have ever stood in a place that was not the place that made them will find something here that they have not yet had words for. That, perhaps, is the whole point.

Sample Sonnets

The rhododendrons hold the ridge like monks

who will not speak until the fog has cleared,

their red a wound the January sun

has pressed against the bark for forty years.

I walked that path before I knew that walking

was a kind of argument with stone,

before my hands had learned the grammar of

a hoe, the conjugation of a bone.

The altitude was everything back then—

it pressed the breath into a sharper thing,

made every step a word in some slow psalm

the mountain kept on patiently revising.

I carry it, that thinning of the air,

into each city where I learn to bear.

She kept it by the register, that orchid,

pale as something grown without a sun,

the fluorescent tube above it torrid,

buzzing like a thing that can't be done.

And still it pushed one spike of purple out,

improbable beneath the humming light,

surviving every reason left to doubt

the possibility of getting it right.

I bought a coffee, watched it through the glass

of January's long and bitter dark,

and thought of all the drafts I'd let me pass

too quickly, dousing my own smallest spark.

The city teaches waste and teaches will.

That orchid had no business blooming still.

The monarchs come through in October's dry

and middle weeks, when corn stands at its height,

a moving stain of amber cross the sky

that turns the whole field briefly into light.

You do not see them coming. Then they're there—

ten thousand wings between the stalks and sun,

a rustle in the air that isn't air,

a tide that passes through before it's done.

I'd stop the machete mid-swing just to watch,

shoulder still braced against the morning load,

and feel the season hinge—that notch

where summer ends and something else is owed.

Migration does not ask permission. It

moves through you like a year you can't forget.
