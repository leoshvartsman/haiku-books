What the Soil Remembers

Simone Marino
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
A haiku is a small container for an enormous fact. In this collection, the facts are these: that a body carries the landscape it first knew, even across oceans; that ecological grief and personal grief are not separate; that belonging is rarely chosen and rarely clean. The poems in What the Soil Remembers were written across four cities over nearly two decades, and they follow one life as it moves — sometimes willingly, sometimes not — through radically different ecologies, each one asking something different of the person who arrived in it. The river at Buenos Aires. The dune at Provincetown. The cracked riverbed at Florence. The monsoon at Bangalore. Each place left a residue, the way soil retains the memory of every season it has ever absorbed. The haiku form was not chosen for its smallness but for its precision — its insistence on the present moment even when the present moment is almost unbearable to hold. These poems do not argue. They observe. They stand at the edge of a drought-exposed riverbed or press a thumb across a blurred ink column and simply report what the body noticed before the mind could intervene. What emerges, across two hundred and fifty poems, is not a thesis about climate or displacement or language, but something closer to a record: of attention paid, of grief metabolized slowly, of wonder that kept returning even when it had no right to. The seven sections move roughly in chronological order, though the book is less a memoir than a meditation. Readers who have ever felt the specific ache of a landscape they cannot return to, or the disorientation of arriving somewhere that will not immediately receive them, will find their own coordinates somewhere in these pages. The soil remembers everything deposited into it. So, it turns out, do we.
