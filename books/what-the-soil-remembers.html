What the Soil Remembers

Claude Robert
20 sonnets
Download PDF Download EPUB
A garden is not a metaphor — or not only. It is also soil, weather, the specific weight of a trowel, the smell of horse manure in February cold. Claude Robert's sonnets begin there, in the body's knowledge of ground, and ask what it means to tend a life the way one tends a garden: with patience, with seasonal faith, with the understanding that what you bury is not always lost. These poems move through four landscapes — Paris, Norwich, Edinburgh, San Francisco — and carry the sediment of each one forward into the next, the way a gardener carries the habits of one climate into another and slowly, imperfectly, learns to read a new sky. The collection thinks in two languages and belongs fully to neither, and that doubleness — that slight displacement from any single tongue — turns out to be one of its subjects. So does the question of names, of chosen selves, of what survives translation and what is merely transformed. But Robert wears these themes lightly. The sonnets trust the reed bed, the haar, the barrow, the fog to do the philosophical work, and they are right to. What the Soil Remembers is a book about rootedness written by someone who has had to choose their roots — and who has found, in that choosing, something more deliberate and perhaps more durable than the rootedness that simply arrives at birth.

Sample Sonnets

She said: the image earns its place or not.

She said: the adjective must justify

its presence at the table. What you've got

is atmosphere — now find the reason why.

I cycled home along the river path

with her words caught between my handlebars.

The reeds were doing nothing in the math

of evening — neither symbols, neither scars,

just reeds, just cold, just the specific grey

of Wensum water thinning toward the dark.

I stopped and looked. I did not look away.

I tried to see the reed bed, not the mark

I'd planned to make of it. The difference was

the whole of what I had to learn. It was.

The barrow wants to tip. February

has set the path to iron under heel,

and still I push the horse-shit to the borders,

the steam of it the only warmth I feel.

This is the work that doesn't get remembered,

the labour underneath the labour's name,

the thing that makes the August roses possible,

the buried fact that earns the border's fame.

I've learned to love this logic without glory,

the way a life can run on hidden work,

on what is given back to what gave first,

on showing up in cold before the dark.

Nobody photographs the February barrow.

The garden, come July, will not be narrow.

She was already old when I arrived there,

the head gardener, Morag, small and sure,

who walked the beds in rubber boots on Monday

and catalogued the week's work, cure by cure.

She never asked where I had come from, only

whether I could tell a weed from what

was meant to stay, and when I showed her that I could,

she nodded once and that was that.

I think of her the way I think of Edinburgh —

exacting, spare, not given to excess,

a kind of love that operates through standard,

that shows itself in accurate redress.

She taught me that devotion is a grammar.

I conjugate it still, in every border.
