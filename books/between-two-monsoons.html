Between Two Monsoons

Riley García

There is a kind of weather that has nothing to do with climate. It moves through a person the way a front moves through a valley — slowly, then all at once, leaving the air changed in ways that are difficult to name. Riley García was eighteen when these poems were written, and had already lived in enough places to know that belonging is not a destination but a condition, something you carry or don't, something that shifts with the light. Between Two Monsoons is not a book about travel. It is a book about the interval — the long, unsteady pause between one version of yourself and the next, between one language settling in your mouth and another one leaving it, between the place your body is and the place your nervous system still thinks it is. The haiku gathered here move through eight interior seasons, from the frozen restlessness of a Czech winter to the 2 a.m. electricity of Jakarta rain, from the wide silence of a New Zealand fog to the slow grammar of heat and ripening. What connects them is not geography but physics — the particular pressure of being mid-crossing, of standing in the gap between two climates and belonging entirely to neither. García writes from that gap with precision and without self-pity, finding in the smallest observations — a sparrow testing ice, a thumb-bruised mango, a word held in the mouth like a stone — the exact weight of a life lived in plural. Readers will find no resolution here, and that is the point. The book ends not with arrival but with ceremony: small, deliberate acts of attention that do not require a fixed address. If there is an argument running beneath these pages, it is this — that being between things is not a failure of belonging but a particular kind of belonging, available only to those who have learned to stand still in the crossing and look around.
