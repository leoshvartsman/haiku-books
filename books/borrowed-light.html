Borrowed Light

Morgan Gagnon
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
Some lives are built from a single place, deep and continuous as a root system. Others are assembled — gathered from kitchens and border crossings, from languages learned late and cold learned early, from the particular quality of light in a city that is not yet yours but is slowly, incrementally, becoming so. Borrowed Light is a book of that second kind of life. These haiku follow a young person across three countries and two adopted languages, through the physical work of professional kitchens, the sensory bewilderment of arrival, the quiet comedy of being illiterate again at an age when you thought you were done with that. What holds the journey together is not narrative but attention — the daily practice of looking closely at what is directly in front of you, which turns out to be enough. Haiku has always been a form for the present tense, for the thing seen rather than the thing remembered. It asks its writer to stop and notice, and it asks its reader to do the same. In a life organized around movement and displacement, that discipline becomes something close to survival. These poems were written in notebooks on trains, on the backs of prep lists, in apartments where the heat was unreliable and the light was borrowed from the building across the street. They are small. They are meant to be. The world they record is a world apprehended in fragments — a crack in lake ice, a stranger catching a coffee cup, a Czech word finally pronounced correctly — and the fragments, set side by side, make something that holds.
