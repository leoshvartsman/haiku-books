Borrowed Light

A life lived across latitudes leaves a particular kind of residue — not quite memory, not quite loss, but something closer to light refracted through many windows at once. Parker Chen's debut collection, Borrowed Light, moves through Stockholm winters and Vermont maples, Vancouver rain and Oslo dusk, assembling a self from the materials each place offers and each place withholds. These haiku do not mourn displacement so much as they document its texture: the specific weight of a suitcase that has crossed too many time zones, the way cardamom smells identical in two languages, the slow grammar of a neighbor's nod. The haiku form is itself a kind of immigration — a compression, a crossing into a space smaller than the feeling that fills it. Chen works within that compression with precision and patience, trusting the image to hold what argument cannot. What emerges across these 250 poems is not a story of arrival or departure but something more honest and more difficult: the portrait of a consciousness that belongs, partially and permanently, to several places at once, and has learned to call that multiplicity home. Borrowed light, after all, is still light. It still illuminates. It still warms.
