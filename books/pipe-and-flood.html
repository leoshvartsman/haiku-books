Pipe and Flood

Morgan Kim
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
Water does not know where it is. It falls on Kuala Lumpur and Manchester and the Irish midlands with equal indifference, finds the lowest point, and moves. Morgan Kim has spent forty years watching it do this — in monsoon drains choked with bottle caps, in bedsit radiators that click and sigh, in bog-land pipes that smell of centuries, in harbour water that opens for a ferry and then closes as though nothing passed. What this collection discovers, slowly and without announcement, is that the water was always the same water, and so, perhaps, was the person watching it. Pipe and Flood is a migrant's autobiography told in the smallest possible units of attention. It does not explain displacement or argue for belonging. Instead it crouches at drain mouths, reads invoices in hot vans, presses a thumb to a copper fitting in the dark, and lets the accumulation of those moments carry the weight that prose would over-explain. The haiku form suits this project exactly: it is a vessel for what arrives before understanding, for the thing perceived before the story about the thing takes over. The book moves roughly in time and geography — Malaysia to England to Ireland to Australia — but it is not a straight line. Sediment does not travel in straight lines. Voices from earlier sections surface in later ones; a drain in Section 1 rhymes with a gutter in Section 5; a word deleted in Section 6 is still somehow present in Section 7. By the final pages the reader has not witnessed a journey so much as an accumulation, the slow filling of a pipe with everything a life has passed through. What Kim offers is not resolution but something rarer: the particular peace of a person who has finally stopped being surprised by their own plurality.
