Smoke Remembers Everything

Alex Novak

Some bodies learn fire before they learn fear. Alex Novak grew up in Hanoi breathing incense and river mist, then spent decades crossing hemispheres — Melbourne, Brisbane, and finally Delhi — carrying a firefighter's particular knowledge of what the world looks like when it is coming apart at the seams. This collection is the record of that crossing: not a memoir, not quite a confession, but something closer to what smoke does when it moves through a room, tracing the shape of everything it touches without ever settling into one place. Haiku, with its insistence on the physical and the immediate, turns out to be the right form for a life lived in the body's extremes. The seventeen syllables do not explain or resolve; they arrive the way sensation arrives — sudden, precise, and already passing. Novak uses the form to hold what longer lines might sentimentalize: the specific weight of a suitcase that won't close, the color of a ridge line going orange at three in the morning, the way grief lives in a chin strap still adjusted to someone else's jaw. The poems ask nothing of the reader except presence, which is, of course, everything. Seven sections trace an arc from childhood to a late stillness that is not peace exactly but something harder and more honest — a reckoning that refuses the comfort of conclusion. What burns here is not only timber and scrubland. What endures is not only scar. By the final poems, three languages are surfacing in the same breath, three cities competing for the same sleeping body, and the question of where a person belongs has been quietly, permanently retired in favor of a stranger and more livable question: what have I carried, and has it been enough to carry?
