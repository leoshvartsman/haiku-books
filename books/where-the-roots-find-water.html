Where the Roots Find Water

Michi Suzuki
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
A landscape architect learns to read the world in layers — soil over rubble, roots over memory, the living over the erased. These haiku are the record of that reading. They move through Hiroshima and Beirut, through a childhood written in two scripts, through coastal fog and market mornings in a city that has not yet decided whether to call you its own. They ask what it means to tend a place, to draw its contour lines, to press your thumb into its soil — and whether that act of tending is itself a form of belonging, even before belonging arrives. Michi Suzuki came to this work as someone who has spent a professional life studying how the living persists through the catastrophic: how oleander returns to scorched ground, how pine roots answer a blast wall's argument decades after the blast, how water finds the same low hollow it found five hundred years ago. The haiku form — its compression, its insistence on the present moment, its tolerance for what is left unsaid — proved the right vessel for that kind of knowledge. Each poem is a site survey. Each white space on the page is what the ground has not yet given up. This collection is arranged not as autobiography but as a kind of geological cross-section, moving from the deep historical strata of atomic aftermath and war rubble up through the personal sediment of name and language, through professional immersion in damaged landscapes, and finally toward the surface: the tentative, hard-won present tense of a life taking root. Readers who come to these pages looking for resolution will find something more honest — the ongoing negotiation between a person and the ground beneath them, conducted one small observation at a time.

oleander blooms—

the epicenter still warm

under tourist shoes

two scripts on one form—

I choose the box that says other

and feel accurate

one pine root, one crack:

not metaphor—load bearing,

real, and still spreading

reed choked culvert, yes—

or a stormwater channel

finally answered

huarango shadow

moves across the ruin wall —

the ruin stays still

I find the drawing

I made in Beirut, folded:

a park that stayed planned
