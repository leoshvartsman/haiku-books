What the Mouth Remembers

Cameron Martin
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
A haiku is a small container for a large fact. Riya Kapoor has spent five decades collecting facts the body refuses to forget: the specific pitch of a pressure cooker heard through a cracked wall, the comedy of packing a winter scarf in an Australian December, the way a broken sauce announces itself before you have found words for what went wrong. These poems move through Delhi, Australia, Portland, Lyon, and the quieter country of late middle age — not as a travelogue, but as an archaeology of sensation. What the mouth remembers, it remembers precisely. This is not a book about nostalgia. Nostalgia softens; these poems do not. Kapoor's eye is a critic's eye — trained, hungry, and quietly merciless — and she turns it on herself as readily as on a warping menu board or a sauce gone wrong. The woman who moves through these pages is always in translation: between hemispheres, between languages, between the person she was in one city and the person the next city required her to become. The haiku form suits this life exactly. It asks you to be present. It does not ask you to explain. Read in sequence, the seven sections trace something close to a life — saturated and loud at the start, gradually stilling, arriving at the end not at resolution but at a particular quality of wakefulness. Read in fragments, each poem stands alone, a door into a room that smells like somewhere real. Both readings are true. Kapoor has written a book that knows how memory actually works: not in chapters, but in sudden returns — a smell, a sound, the weight of a suitcase clasp under familiar hands.
