What the Water Carries

Yuki Kato
25 sonnets
Download PDF Download EPUB
Yuki Kato has spent a lifetime reading bodies — the body of a patient, the body of a city, the body of a river at flood. These sonnets gather that lifelong attention into a single, sustained act of witness. They move from the Yodo River of childhood Osaka through the hospital wards of Florence and into the stone heat of contemporary Rome, where an eighty-one-year-old woman sits by a window, watching a fig tree crack the pavement and the climate measurements climb, and still finds the world worth recording. The book does not argue for hope so much as practice it, the way a physician practices: with clear eyes, steady hands, and no illusions about what the data shows. The sonnet form is not incidental here. Kato returns to it the way she returns to the taking of a pulse — for what it reliably reveals, for the discipline of its attention, for the small mercy of the turn at line nine that insists on complication before it allows a close. These are poems about what is lost to time and warming and forgetting, and also about what refuses to be lost: a name pressed to a page, the smell of tatami after rain, a gecko holding a wall in the last of the August heat. Readers who come to this book as strangers to medicine or to Japan will find themselves, before long, at home in both. The particulars are precise and the feeling is universal. Something is ending. Someone is watching. The watching matters.

Sample Sonnets

The Yodo ran brown-gold that August, swollen

past the levee stones my father named,

and all the neighborhood stood watching, stolen

from their suppers, watching, almost shamed

to see such power in a thing so common—

water, only water, turned to will.

The persimmons on the wire were women

bending in the wind and bending still.

My mother didn't watch. She sorted fish

by size and salt along the kitchen mat,

her fingers certain as a prayer, each wish

pressed inward, private, wordless, intimate.

The river has forgotten how to flood.

My mother's hands are now my only blood.

To take a name is to commit a theft

from no one and from someone all at once.

Lee was my father's silence, what was left

of a migration I was never once

invited to understand. He carried it

the way you carry weather in your knees —

you know it only by the ache. I quit

that name not out of shame but out of these

accumulations: ledgers, corridors,

the long Italian evenings when my name

sat in a colleague's mouth behind closed doors

and came out altered, smaller, and the same

word felt like borrowed clothing. So I chose.

A name I chose is a name that grows.

November fog erased the Arno's far

bank every morning, left the near bank strange

and close, the plane trees bare, a single car

moving along the lungarno's range

of empty benches, lamplight still in use

at eight a.m., the season slow to lift.

I walked to work through that, found no excuse

to stop—not cold, not beauty, not the drift

of wood smoke from a window somewhere high

above the street. The hospital was warm

and smelled of what hospitals smell of: the dry

erasure of the body's own alarm.

I entered it the same way every day:

the fog behind me, slowly burned away.
