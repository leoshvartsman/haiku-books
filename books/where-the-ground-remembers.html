Where the Ground Remembers

Sol Carvalho
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
Sol Carvalho has spent ninety-nine years learning what ground can hold. Born in Rio de Janeiro and carried by profession and circumstance through American cities, Oslo, and finally Dhaka, she worked for seven decades as a landscape architect — someone paid, in essence, to understand what a place wants to become. The haiku in this collection are not a memoir, though they move through a life. They are something closer to soil samples: small, dense cores drawn from different depths, each one smelling of a particular earth, a particular light, a particular season of the self. The title comes from something Carvalho observed early in her training: that soil is not static matter but accumulated time, built from everything that has lived and fallen and broken down before. She came to understand her own displacement the same way. The homes she left did not disappear. They composted into her. Rio became the sensory baseline against which all other places were measured. The American cities taught her the discipline of longing. Oslo gave her precision. Dhaka gave her surrender. And somewhere in the layering of all four, she found not rootlessness but a kind of richness — the fertility of a life that has taken in more than one world. These 250 haiku are arranged chronologically, following the arc of a long life, but they are not nostalgic in the usual sense. Carvalho does not mourn what she left. She notices what it left in her. Readers who have known more than one home, more than one language, more than one version of themselves, will recognize the particular attention at work here — the attention of someone who has learned, through long practice, to look at a place as if it might be the last place, or the first, or both at once.

Sample Haikus

bougainvillea bleeds

through the whitewash, staining noon

a color with no name

the drafting room smells

of rubber and ambition —

someone else's country

April tulip:

the red arrives before

the green is finished

ink line faded

to the color of the paper —

the garden remains

mustard oil on fish—

the vendor wraps it in news

I cannot yet read

the moss I planted

in Oslo — did it outlast

the drawing office?
