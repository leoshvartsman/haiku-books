The Spice Routes Home

Sky Cohen

There is a kind of hunger that doesn't know its own name until it has already carried you across several oceans. Sky Cohen's debut haiku collection, The Spice Routes Home, is the record of that hunger — and the slow, surprising discovery that stillness was never the opposite of movement, but its destination. Measured not in years but in accumulated weathers, this book moves through a Vancouver childhood soaked in cedar and Pacific fog, a young adulthood awakened by Sydney light and Istanbul smoke, years spent at restaurant tables with a critic's notebook and a pilgrim's appetite, and finally a field in Connacht where the rain teaches patience to anyone willing to stay long enough to learn it. Cohen works in the jazz tradition, trusting the space between notes as much as the notes themselves. The haiku form — spare, exact, quietly explosive — becomes the perfect instrument for a life lived at the intersection of belonging and departure. These poems do not resolve their tensions so much as hold them, the way a sustained chord holds both its harmony and its ache. To read this collection in sequence is to feel the gradual slowing of a restless body, the widening of a restless eye. What Cohen is ultimately tracing is not a journey outward but a journey toward a self that was always there, waiting in the gap between the ferry horn and the silence that swallows it, between the word you reach for and the one that doesn't exist, between the bite and the satisfaction. The spice routes, it turns out, were always routes home — even when home was still being invented.
