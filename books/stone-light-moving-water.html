Stone Light, Moving Water

Taylor Roberts
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
Taylor Roberts has spent a career counting things — spores, specimens, data points, the incremental creep of lichen across stone. This collection is what happened when that same precision of attention turned inward, toward the landscapes that made and remade one life: the moorland of an early childhood, the raked gardens of a formative apprenticeship, the desert light of a long middle chapter, the rain-softened Pacific Northwest of a late arrival. Haiku, it turns out, is a scientist's natural form — seventeen syllables as a measurement, a single moment isolated like a sample under glass, the world reduced to what is actually there. But Stone Light, Moving Water is not a travelogue, and Roberts is not a tourist of place. These poems ask a harder question: what does it mean to belong somewhere when you have, by accident or ambition or love, belonged to many places? The lichen on a Cairo wall and the lichen on a Seattle kerb are the same organism, indifferent to the passport in Roberts's pocket. The crow on a wire speaks the same language in every city. Urban nature, it seems, has no interest in the human drama of migration and longing — and that indifference, rendered here with both scientific exactness and genuine tenderness, becomes its own kind of consolation. The seven sections follow a rough chronology of a life, though time in these poems moves the way water moves in the title — not in a straight line, but finding its level, pooling in unexpected hollows, carrying light from somewhere else. Readers need not know the biography to feel its shape. The grammar of these poems was learned before words, and it is offered back here in the same spirit: not as explanation, but as evidence.

grey rain on grey stone,

a grammar I learned before

I learned any words

the gardener rakes

not the gravel but the hour—

I watch and say nothing

my microscope hums

the muezzin hums a street down—

two kinds of searching

mortar crumbled here

since before I arrived—

lichen did the work

coho in the culvert:

even here, even this late,

the body knows home

braw—then hoopoe—then

a Cairo fig—one notebook,

one confused spring
