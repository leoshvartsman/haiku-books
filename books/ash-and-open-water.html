Ash and Open Water

Cameron Andersson

There is a kind of knowledge the body keeps that the mind cannot file away. It lives in the particular weight of a key in a foreign lock, in the way a smell — petrichor on hot tar, sulfur rising from black earth — can collapse a thousand miles of distance into a single breath. Cameron Andersson's debut collection, Ash and Open Water, is built from that knowledge. Moving through Johannesburg's Highveld, a fog-bound British arrival, the night streets of Vienna, and the geothermal silences of Iceland, these haiku ask not where a person has been, but what each place has left inside them — what residue of light and language and moral weight continues to work on a life long after the passport has been stamped and the season has changed. The collection traces a young life in motion, but it refuses the romance of the wanderer. There is homesickness here that has no clean address. There is the particular loneliness of the night-shift worker, the immigrant's estrangement from their own reflection, the spiritual vertigo of standing on lava rock and feeling, for the first time, genuinely small in a way that is not diminishing but clarifying. Andersson writes in the haiku tradition's commitment to the precise and the present, yet the poems accumulate into something longer — a life being assembled from its own scattered evidence, the way a geologist reads strata, layer by slow layer. Ash and Open Water is finally a book about what carries: what survives translation, what survives departure, what a grandmother's word in Afrikaans is still doing, quietly, in the syntax of an Icelandic winter thought. The haiku form — spare, uninsistent, patient — turns out to be the perfect vessel for this kind of inquiry. It does not demand resolution. It holds the question open, the way a landscape holds a traveler's footprint long after the traveler has gone.
