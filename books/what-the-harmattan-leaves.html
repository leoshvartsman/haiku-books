What the Harmattan Leaves

Parker Chen
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
Parker Chen has spent a life learning to read the world through its weather and its food — the way a dry wind carries memory before it carries dust, the way a knife held long enough begins to hold you back. What the Harmattan Leaves is a book of haiku that moves through continents and decades, from the red roads of Accra to the wet stone plazas of Madrid, with long seasons in professional kitchens in between. It is not, finally, a book about displacement, though displacement is everywhere in it. It is a book about the persistence of the senses: how the body keeps its own archive, how the hands remember a technique or a texture or a grief that the conscious mind has long since filed away or lost. The haiku form suits this project with an almost uncomfortable precision. Its compression mirrors the way sensory memory actually works — not in narrative but in flash, in the sudden return of a smell or a quality of light that arrives before language does and often leaves before language can catch it. Chen works within the tradition while remaining answerable to specific, lived experience: the palm nut paste smuggled through customs, the whetstone worn concave by decades of the same stroke, the mango in a Madrid market that is the right shape but the wrong month. Each poem is a small act of attention, and the accumulation of that attention across two hundred and fifty poems becomes something that feels less like a collection than a life held carefully up to the light. Readers are invited to move through these seven sections in order, following a rough arc from early childhood to late middle age, from pre-linguistic sensation to hard-won ambiguity. But the book also rewards wandering. Chen is a chef, and this is a book built for return visits — for the poem you pass over the first time that stops you cold the third time through, when the season has changed or your hands are tired or you have just eaten something that reminded you of somewhere you cannot quite name.

harmattan comes—

mango leaves hold the white dust

like flour on hands

lip split to bleeding—

I press my tongue to the place,

taste iron, taste here

mise en place: each thing

in its place before the fire—

the mind rests in this

Barcelona fig:

I find the seeds after, small

and dark in my teeth

the block has gone soft

in one spot only—the spot

where I always stand

Sunday at noon: a

woman counts coins twice, puts them

back, buys nothing, smiles
