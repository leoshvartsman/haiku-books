What the Borders Remember

Jordan Brown
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
A haiku is a small room with no furniture. You bring yourself to it, and the room tells you what you are carrying. Jordan Brown has spent six decades carrying a great deal — childhood in Addis Ababa under the long shadow of imperial decline, young adulthood in the American South learning a country through its silences and its heat, years of police work in Japan and then in the corridors of European institutions, and finally the slow, accumulated reckoning of a life that has been, by any measure, several lives at once. What the Borders Remember is the record of all of them, written in the only form capacious enough to hold what prose would over-explain and memoir would sentimentalize. These 250 haiku move through seven stations, each with its own emotional weather and its own quality of attention. But they are not a memoir in disguise. Brown is not interested in narrative causality — in how one thing led to another — so much as in the texture of specific moments: the weight of a coin counted twice, the grammar of a Southern summer, the particular silence of a police box at three in the morning, the way a word in Japanese can escape the mouth mid-argument in French and briefly make a stranger of you to yourself. Power appears here not as abstraction but as sensation — in a warden's mouth, in a blank line on a form, in the angle of a briefcase sliding across a train seat. The intimate and the political are not treated as opposites. They are understood, from the first poem to the last, as the same thing. Readers who come to this collection expecting the pastoral quietude sometimes associated with haiku will find instead something more unsettled and more honest: a form put to work in the service of moral complexity, historical memory, and the strange, persistent task of accounting for oneself. The borders this book remembers are not only geographic. They are the borders between languages, between roles, between the person one was and the person one became, between what the official record says and what actually happened. Brown writes across all of them with the compressed precision of someone who has learned, over a long life, that the shortest distance between two truths is a single, well-chosen image.
