Longitude of Light

Parker Thomas
250 haiku

There is a kind of life that cannot be measured in years or accomplishments, only in the quality of attention paid — to a railing tasted on a winter dare, to the long shiver of a powerline after birds have gone, to the weight of a balcony chair that has learned a body over decades. Longitude of Light is Parker Thomas's reckoning with that attention: a collection of haiku that moves through the landscapes of a long life not as a travelogue but as a kind of cartography of the self, each place leaving its particular grammar on the man who passed through it. New York's concrete and brine, the fluorescent hum of office years, the astonishment of Australia, the grief of vanishing reefs, the bewildering joy of Rio in late life — together they form a single, sustained act of witness. Haiku, with its insistence on the present moment and its distrust of abstraction, turns out to be the ideal instrument for this kind of reckoning. Thomas uses the form not as a container for pretty images but as a precision tool, cutting away everything that isn't essential until what remains is the thing itself: the smell of pastrami under glass, the archaeology of keyboard dust, a beetle swimming three inches of sky in a bromeliad cup. The losses here are real — of neighborhoods, of ecosystems, of the body's former certainties — but so is the wonder, and neither is permitted to crowd the other out. What emerges is a portrait of a consciousness that has stayed genuinely open across eight decades, still capable of being stopped in its tracks by a vendor's call or a bay holding the last light like a mouth not yet ready to close. Readers are invited to move through these seven sections in sequence, following the arc from boyhood to old age, from the Northern Hemisphere to the Southern, from restlessness to a hard-won stillness. But the book also rewards wandering. Each haiku stands alone, a complete world in seventeen syllables. Together they form something larger: the longitude of a single light, tracked across a lifetime of mornings.
