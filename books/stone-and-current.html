Stone and Current

Taylor Anderson

There is a kind of nature that does not wait for wilderness. It arrives in the gap between bricks, in the sparrow that has learned the rhythm of your block, in the weed that shoulders through asphalt because it has no other option. Stone and Current is a book about finding that nature — persistent, unannounced, indifferent to borders — while a body moves through cities it does not yet know how to read. These poems were written in Philadelphia and Reykjavik, in Rio de Janeiro and in the unnamed transit zones between them, wherever the ordinary world offered itself up for a moment and asked to be heard. A haiku is a tuning fork. Strike it against something real and it hums at the frequency of that thing — not explaining it, not improving it, just holding the pitch long enough for you to feel it in your sternum. The haiku gathered here are organized not by geography alone but by the interior weather that each place produced: the grounded restlessness of a city you grew up beside, the disorientation of being a student of something larger than yourself, the humid tangle of beauty and unease, the silence that pools in the spaces between making. They trace a life in motion, but they are most interested in the moments when motion stops — when a sparrow lands, when fog refuses to speak, when a folding chair holds the shape of a sound that has already left the room. You do not need to have been to these places. You only need to have stood somewhere and noticed that the world was continuing without your permission, and felt, in that noticing, something close to relief.
