Ash Knows No Borders

Sky Patel
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
Sky Patel has spent a career running toward fire. As a firefighter working across three continents, he has learned what most of us are spared from knowing: that destruction has a grammar, that what a flame takes and what it leaves behind are both, in their way, a kind of argument. This collection is that argument made into poetry — 250 haiku written across four cities, two hemispheres, and a lifetime of arriving somewhere new and learning, again, what it costs to stay. The title is not a comfort. Ash knows no borders because fire doesn't either, and neither does the grief that follows it, and neither, Patel insists, does the fury. The haiku form suits this project with an almost suspicious precision. Its compression mirrors the compression of displacement — the way an entire world gets folded into a coat pocket, a borrowed word, a lease clause you have to look up. But Patel is not a poet who mistakes restraint for passivity. Trained partly through the British haiku tradition and partly through the irreplaceable education of watching things burn, he uses the form's silences as load-bearing walls. What is not said in these poems holds up everything that is. Readers who come expecting tranquil nature imagery will find it here, transformed: the horse chestnut, the goldfinch, the sparrow on the roof — but each one weighted, each one knowing something it shouldn't have to know. The book moves roughly chronologically, from a luminous and uneasy Vienna childhood through apprenticeship, crisis, and political reckoning, arriving finally at a hard-won, multilingual peace. But Patel is not interested in the clean arc of an immigrant success story. He is interested in the charred door frame that still holds the shape of a door. He is interested in what survives — memory, language, political fury, tenderness — and in the uncomfortable truth that survival is not the same as safety, and home is not the same as belonging, and belonging, when you finally find it, may be the most radical act available to you.

Sample Haikus

February ice

cracks the Danube into seams—

I traced them like maps

her red pen rests on

the one line I kept intact—

I look at the floor

3 a.m. call—

I lace my boots in the dark

the city already orange

the door frame stands where

the door stood — architecture

of what was kept out

press photo printed,

the building behind him burns

in the same edition

the lease is thirty pages—

I signed a country once

with less paperwork
