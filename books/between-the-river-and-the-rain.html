Between the River and the Rain

Jordan Chen
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
Jordan Chen has lived inside too many weathers to believe in a single home. Born in New Orleans, shaped by its levees and live oaks and the particular weight of its summer air, Chen later found themselves navigating Melbourne's upended constellations, the fluorescent corridors of press conferences across two continents, and finally the canal-laced neighborhoods of Osaka — each city leaving its sediment, each departure revising what came before. Between the River and the Rain is the record of that accumulation: 250 haiku written across three decades, in notebooks and on press credentials and on the backs of receipts, in languages Chen was still learning to trust. The haiku form suits this project with an almost suspicious precision. Its economy mirrors the journalist's instinct to compress, to find the load-bearing detail; its insistence on the present moment pushes back against the reporter's habit of always contextualizing, always situating the now inside a larger narrative. These poems are where Chen stopped filing and started looking. They are also, quietly, an argument: that displacement is not the opposite of belonging but one of its possible shapes — that a person can be fluent in humidity, in the angle of afternoon light off a foreign river, in the specific loneliness of finding a constellation and losing it again, and that this fluency is a kind of home. The collection moves roughly chronologically, though memory refuses strict order and several poems circle back across decades without warning. Readers will find childhood and grief and moral urgency and dry comedy and something that arrives, by the final section, close to peace — not resolution, but rest. The river in the title is more than one river. The rain is the same rain, falling in different languages.
