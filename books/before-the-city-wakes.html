Before the City Wakes

Jamie Rodriguez
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
There is a kind of attention that only comes from having left something behind. Guadalupe Rodríguez has spent a life cultivating that attention — across Guadalajara and Mexico City, across a Tennessee hollow and the gray winters of Philadelphia, across decades of early mornings and the particular silence that precedes commerce, memory, and grief. Before the City Wakes is the record of that cultivation: 250 haiku arranged not as autobiography but as something closer to a ledger of the senses, each entry small and precise, the whole accumulating into a portrait of a woman who has never stopped noticing the world before it notices her back. Rodríguez works in the classical spirit of haiku — the compressed image, the seasonal turn, the moment that opens onto something it does not name — but her materials are her own: Talavera tile and invoice stamps, chamoy and January rooftops, the worn eagle on a peso held too long in a waiting hand. The poems do not explain migration or nostalgia or belonging. They do what haiku has always done: they arrive at the threshold of meaning and stop, trusting the reader to feel the draft coming through the door. What accumulates across these seven sections is not a thesis about displacement but something more durable — a sensibility, a set of hands, a wrist that still knows the motion even when the work is done. Read this collection as you would watch a city wake: slowly, without forcing the light. The poems reward return. A word crossed out in the final pages rhymes with a crack in the tile on the first, and the smoke that finds every crack in Guadalajara is still finding its way through, page by page, until something lifts — not the fog, as Rodríguez herself tells us, but something else entirely.

purple drifts settle

on cracked Talavera tile—

yesterday's color

high-rise glass holds cloud

for one second, then releases

what it cannot keep

the notebook open

smells of cedar and new rain

my pen does not move

a window faces

west but I never look up

until the light fails

*frontera* slips out

mid-sentence, and you nod once—

you did not hear it

the pigeon's feathers

against the wind—a small

and serious work
