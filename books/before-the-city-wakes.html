Before the City Wakes

Jamie Rodriguez
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
These haiku were written before the city woke. In the thin hour between dark and day — in a rented room in Busan, at a stone windowsill in Rome, beside a well in a village whose name does not translate — Saanvi Singh sat with a notebook and waited for the light to come. What arrived instead were these poems: small, exact, and quietly insistent on being alive in a body that has belonged to more than one place and therefore, in some permanent way, belongs to none. To move across landscapes is to accumulate weather. The red earth of rural India does not leave the feet simply because the feet have since walked Atlantic sand, Seoul pavement, Roman cobblestone. It travels inward, becomes cellular, becomes the way the hands reach for a pot that isn't there or the tongue searches for a word that exists in no single language. "Before the City Wakes" is a book about that accumulation — not as loss, not quite as gain, but as the particular texture of a life lived in translation. Each of the seven sections marks a geography, but the real territory is the self moving through all of them, changed and unchanged, carried and carrying. Haiku asks its writer to be present to a single moment with such precision that the moment opens into something larger than itself. For a poet who has made a practice of crossing thresholds, the form becomes a kind of portable home — small enough to take anywhere, exacting enough to tell the truth. These two hundred and fifty poems were written at dawn because dawn is the hour that belongs to no one yet, the hour before the world reasserts its familiar demands. In that gap, something honest becomes possible. This book is the record of what was found there.

clay pot at the well—

inside it, last night's coolness

holds its own weather

February light

flat as a hospital sheet—

boats leave anyway

the tteokbokki cart

lit orange in the cold street—

I point, she nods, good

switching keyboards mid-

sentence—the cursor blinks, waits—

I blink back, deciding

cold marble beneath

my palm—the bar has held this

cold since before me

warm floor under feet

and the body says: morning,

says: someone loves you
