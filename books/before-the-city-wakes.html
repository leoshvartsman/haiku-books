Before the City Wakes

Alex Rodríguez

There is an hour before the city wakes when time does not quite belong to the present. The streetlights are still on. The birds have not yet committed to their noise. A man sits in a kitchen in Austin, Texas, both palms around a cup of coffee, and he is ninety-eight years old, and he is also thirty, and also seven, and the cobblestones of Madrid are as close as the limestone outside his window. Alex Rodríguez has lived long enough to know that memory is not the past reaching forward — it is the present, deepened. These haiku were written in that hour. Rodríguez spent decades as a paramedic, arriving at the edges of other people's lives with trained hands and a radio asking where he was. He carried two languages the way some people carry two citizenships: not always comfortably, not always with equal weight, but always. The Spanish of his childhood and the English of his becoming press against each other throughout this collection, not in conflict but in conversation — the way two people who have known each other a long time can sit together without speaking and still be understood. Before the City Wakes moves as a long morning moves: from darkness into gradual light, from stillness into the first sounds of the world resuming. The seven sections trace an arc that is not quite chronological and not quite thematic, but something more like atmospheric — a slow change in the quality of the air. Rodríguez does not offer resolution. He offers presence. He offers the cardinal that turns its whole head to look at you, and the fact that you do not look away. That is enough. At ninety-eight, he has learned that enough is not a small thing.
