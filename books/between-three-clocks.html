Between Three Clocks

Jamie Garcia
25 sonnets
Download PDF Download EPUB
Jamie Garcia has lived inside more than one clock. Born in East Los Angeles, trained across European capitals, schooled late in the Canadian Rockies, and finally settled near the equator, he has spent a lifetime in the productive discomfort of not quite belonging to any single place — and belonging, in his own way, to all of them. These sonnets are the record of that reckoning: not a lament for what was left behind, but a patient inventory of what the body carries when it moves through the world for eighty-five years and refuses to stop paying attention. The form Garcia has chosen is itself a kind of argument. The sonnet is a container with a long memory — European, formal, built for turning. In Garcia's hands it becomes something more porous: it holds Los Angeles heat and Lyon rain with equal pressure, lets Mandarin and French and English press against its walls, and finds its volta not in rhetorical reversal but in the quiet shock of continued living. What you will find across these five sections is not a straight line from loss to acceptance, but something truer to the way a life actually accumulates — in layers, in echoes, in the sudden recognition that the distances you have carried have become, at last, a kind of home.

Sample Sonnets

The stucco wall ran all the way to Soto Street

and someone long before us planted at its base

a bougainvillea that refused the heat,

that found in heat the reason to embrace

the wall more tightly, thorns hooking the paint,

its roots below the sidewalk cracking slow

the city's grid, patient as a saint

who has a century and nowhere else to go.

I think of infrastructure now the way I think

of childhood—what holds us without our knowing,

the pipes below the street, the missing link

between the stable and the always-growing.

The bougainvillea knew the wall would crack.

It bloomed regardless. It did not look back.

At Saturday's market near the Presqu'île,

the stalls arrange their colors in the cold:

mâche in loose bunches, dark and dense, the feel

of something living barely, winter-bold,

and radishes, their skins a lacquered red,

their flesh so white they seem to hold the light.

I buy what I can carry, buy my bread,

and practice being present, being right-

sized in the crowd—a man among the stalls,

not a consultant, not American,

not anything the morning's context calls

for. Just a shopper. Just a citizen

of hunger, of the cold, of what the earth

still offers up in winter for its worth.

The Bow is not the Rhône. The Rhône runs wide

and purposeful between its cropped stone banks,

a river that has learned to organize,

to move through cities, earn its civic thanks.

The Bow does something else. It hesitates.

It thickens near the shore where willows lean

and ice assembles slowly, delegates

a portion of itself to white, to clean

suspension—neither moving nor at rest.

I watch from the footbridge in the four o'clock

blue-dark, and think: perhaps the Bow knows best

what I am doing here—how to unlock

the body from its fluency and try

to mean the thing you say before you die.
