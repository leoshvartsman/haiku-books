The Long Way Around

Sky Cohen

There is a kind of life that resists the straight line — that accumulates meaning not through arrival but through detour, not through mastery but through the slow, humbling education of displacement. Sky Cohen's The Long Way Around is such a life rendered in the form that suits it best: the haiku, which has always known that the largest things fit inside the smallest containers. These two hundred and thirty poems span seven decades and four countries, moving from the snow-muffled silences of a Canadian childhood through university corridors and Cairo ledgers and the neon-threaded streets of Seoul, tracking a single consciousness as it loses and grieves and, against all reasonable expectation, opens further with age rather than closing. Cohen works in the classical haiku tradition — the seasonal image, the cut, the breath held between two things — but bends that tradition toward the autobiographical in ways that feel earned rather than imposed. These are not confessional poems dressed in haiku's clothing. They are genuine acts of attention, moments in which the self becomes briefly transparent and the world shows through: a mother's back, a rusting nail bin, a sail white against diesel smoke, a pair of green shoes on a subway stair. The accumulation of such moments across a lifetime is, Cohen suggests, what a life actually is — not its decisions or its losses or its achievements, but the quality of noticing that persists through all of them. Readers who come to this collection looking for resolution will find something better: honesty. The Long Way Around does not conclude that the detour was worth it, exactly, or that late joy cancels earlier grief. It concludes something quieter and stranger — that the route itself has become familiar, that familiarity is a form of belonging, and that belonging, recognized at last, feels less like coming home than like suddenly understanding a language you have been hearing your whole life.
