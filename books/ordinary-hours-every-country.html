Ordinary Hours, Every Country

Saanvi Singh
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
There is a version of this book that is about migration, about the particular ache of living between places, about identity stretched across time zones and languages and kitchens that are almost, but not quite, yours. That book exists. But it is not this one. This one is about what remains after you stop narrating your own displacement — after the story of crossing loses its grip and something quieter takes its place. A weed finding a crack. The weight of a rolling pin that has not moved in years. A sparrow deciding the scooter can wait. These haiku were gathered across many ordinary hours, in many ordinary places, by a woman who eventually stopped asking whether she belonged to them and began, instead, to simply look. What she found was not belonging, exactly, but something more durable: the stubborn, impersonal fact of the world continuing to be itself regardless of who is watching. The crab rights itself. The light comes on in the fifth-floor window. The mango juice runs down the wrist. These things do not require her, and that, it turns out, is a relief. The book moves through seven ways of paying attention — to what grows, to what hungers, to what rests, to weather, to animals, to strangers, and finally to the hours that seem to belong to no one at all. There is no argument being made. There is only the invitation to stop, briefly, and notice what is already, stubbornly, quietly there.
