What the Tongue Remembers

Riya Kapoor
25 sonnets
Download PDF Download EPUB
These sonnets began, as most hungers do, before language. Before I had words for what I was losing or finding, I had the pressure cooker's cry above the ceiling fans in Delhi, the eucalyptus bark unspooling in Melbourne's dry heat, the oyster's cold weight on a November tongue in Portland, the bouchon window fogging and clearing in Lyon. The body keeps its own records. This book is an attempt to read them. What the Tongue Remembers is not a memoir of displacement, though displacement is everywhere in it. It is closer to an argument — that the self is not the thing that gets left behind when you cross time zones and languages and food cultures, but the thing that accumulates them, that becomes, over time, a kind of living translation. The sonnet felt like the right form for this: a small room with rules, a structure that holds pressure. You can put a great deal of grief and appetite into fourteen lines if you trust the container. The book moves through four cities across roughly three decades, but it does not move cleanly. Memory doesn't. A meal in Lyon calls back a kitchen in Delhi; a notebook in Portland fills up with the wrong words for an oyster and the right ones for longing. Readers who have ever eaten their way through homesickness, or stood in a foreign supermarket holding something familiar and felt the particular vertigo of that — this book is, among other things, for you.

Sample Sonnets

What is the first grammar? Not the word—

the pressure before the word, the thing that waits

inside the body wanting to be heard

before the tongue forms anything. It states

itself in heat, in smell, in the low drone

of a city at its noon, in orange fallen,

in brown water moving, in the stone

warmth of a courtyard nobody called in

because it was already ours. I carry

Delhi the way the body carries bone:

not decoratively, not as quarry

for some poem's need, but as the grown

structure underneath—the thing that holds

the shape of me in Lyon's winter colds.

The rangehood broke in April and we taped

a note above it, three of us, in three

different scripts: Devnagari shaped

like temple doors, then Jung-ho's careful plea

in hangul, neat as folded laundry, then

Maria's Portuguese in looping blue.

The landlord never came. We cooked again,

the smoke collecting in the kitchen's stew

of garlic, sesame, coriander,

the extractor fan replaced by open

windows and argument about the tender

age of onions. Something broken

shared between three people in three tongues

is a country made of smoke and lungs.

Six hours left and I have read the magazine

and watched the map, that pulsing little plane

inching above a blue that has no feature,

no city, no address, no weather name.

The screen says: outside temperature, minus fifty.

The cabin holds its artificial noon.

I think of Lyon, waiting, grey and misty,

the Saône in March, the cold specific stone.

I think of what it takes to call a place

the place you live, to let it change your eye,

to learn its light, its bread, its particular face,

to grieve what's far while standing in the near.

The little plane moves forward. So does grief.

So does the living of it. So does here.
