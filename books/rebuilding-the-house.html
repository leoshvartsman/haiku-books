Rebuilding The House

Carpenter Jones
369 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
Every house holds more than it was built to hold. Beneath the paint and plaster, behind the walls we stop seeing because we see them every day, there is a record of time — of water that found its way in, of weight that settled unevenly, of repairs made in haste or love or both. To open a house up is to read that record. To rebuild it is something else entirely. It is a conversation with what was, conducted in sawdust and chalk lines, in the smell of new pine and the ache of a shoulder pressed against a door that will not give. This book began in that conversation and did not end until the door opened clean. The haiku in this collection move through the full arc of a renovation — from the first uneasy walk through rooms that have been waiting, through demolition and the raw exposure of the frame, through the slow middle season of rough work and suspended patience, to the quiet precision of finish work and, finally, to the moment when a house becomes livable again. Season by season, room by room, the poems follow a carpenter who discovers what most people who have ever taken something apart and put it back together already know: the structure being restored is never only the structure. The self is always somewhere in the walls. Haiku is the right form for this work. It is a form that trusts the particular — a brown ring on plaster, a chalk line's blue dust settling, a thermos gone cold on a sawhorse — to carry what cannot be said directly. It is a form built for attention, and attention is what renovation demands above everything else. Read these poems the way you would move through a house you are learning to know: slowly, with your hands, noticing what holds and what gives.

brown ring on plaster—

the leak confessed everything

the ceiling kept quiet

neighbors slow their cars,

reading the dumpster's contents—

someone else's walls

where the roof is gone

pigeons have learned the new edge

they land, look, leave fast

a two-by-four marked

with an X where the stud goes—

simple, sufficient

plastic sheeting tears

one inch at the staple line—

we patch it with tape

the switch clicks quietly—

after months of extension cords,

this is enough
