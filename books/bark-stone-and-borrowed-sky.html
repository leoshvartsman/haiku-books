Bark, Stone, and Borrowed Sky

Alex Nguyen
25 sonnets
Download PDF Download EPUB
Alex Nguyen has spent nearly a century in the business of listening — to clients, to cities, to the small negotiations between living things and the ground beneath them. These sonnets are not a memoir, though they move through time. They are not a nature collection, though they are full of birds, trees, rain, and the patient indifference of stone. They are, perhaps, the record of a long life's slowest realisation: that the world has always been speaking in a language that requires no translation, and that the work is simply learning to stand still long enough to hear it. The book moves through four cities — Brisbane, Tokyo, Beijing, Montreal — not as a travelogue but as a kind of grammar lesson, each place teaching a different conjugation of the same verb: to pay attention. The form is the sonnet, chosen not for its elegance but for its discipline, the way it insists that feeling submit to shape, that the sprawling thing be held. At ninety-eight, Nguyen knows something about the uses of constraint. The fourteen-line room turns out to be large enough for a century, if you arrange it carefully.

Sample Sonnets

The footpath purple, yes—but also brown

at its edges where the blossoms bruised and dried,

and underneath, the bitumen's dark ground

still holding heat the way the living hide

their losses underneath whatever blooms.

Forty-four years coaching lives: I know the trick

of presence covering grief. The city grooms

itself in jacaranda, season-quick,

and we walk through it grateful, barely noticing

the weight of purple underfoot, the slow

compression of what's beautiful. The thing

is this: the tree doesn't perform its show

for us. It does what trees do in their time.

We're just the ones who needed it to rhyme.

The library opened at seven, and the cold

came in before the librarian arrived —

November cold, specific, dry, and old

with the smell of ink that somehow had survived

the centuries the texts pretended to.

I wore my coat inside. My breath was brief

white punctuation in the aisle. I drew

my fingertip across a spine, the leaf-

thin paper of the index cards, the wood

of the catalogue drawer worn smooth as bone.

A discipline was forming. Solitude

was not the Brisbane kind — that heat-drenched, known

and sprawling lonitude. This was a choice

the cold made for you. Ink. A single voice.

A hutong cat crossed my path every week

for seven years and never once looked back.

I'd come at nine, she'd cross, I'd hear the creak

of my client's gate, descend the track

of the alley's broken tiles, and think: that cat

and I are both professionals. We keep

our circuit without sentiment. The flat

indifference of the regular is deep

as any bond. By year eleven I

would miss that cat like missing a fixed star,

not for its warmth but for the way the sky

arranges itself around what we are:

the gate, the alley, the expected small

grey body crossing, making sense of all.
