The Long Way Round

Robin Hughes
25 sonnets
Download PDF Download EPUB
Robin Hughes was not always Robin Hughes. Before that name there was another, and before that city there were others, and the life traced in these sonnets moves not forward through time so much as outward through space — Prague to London to Boston to Austin to Stockholm, each place leaving its particular light on the glass. The Long Way Round is the record of that movement, but it is not a travel book and it is not quite a memoir. It is something more like an excavation: each poem a layer turned up, examined, set down again with care. The sonnets are arranged not by date but by self — by the names and cities in which different versions of the speaker lived and worked and slowly became who he is now. What holds them together is a voice: dry, precise, capable of tenderness without sentimentality, and possessed of the kind of patience that can only be earned. Hughes has always written as someone who has already survived the thing he is describing, and that retrospective clarity is not distance — it is, in fact, the poem's subject. How do we understand a life while we are still inside it? How do we know which losses were formative and which were merely losses? At eighty-nine, in a Stockholm winter, the speaker arrives at something that is not quite peace and not quite resolution but is perhaps better than either: a way of being present without needing to explain the journey. Readers who have ever left a place, or a name, or an earlier version of themselves will find their own sediment here.

Sample Sonnets

The fog came in before the milkman came,

before the kettle, before my mother's voice,

and settled on the brick like a grey claim

on everything that might have been a choice.

I pressed my face against the cold front glass

and watched it eat the houses one by one,

the lamp post last, a ghost of hammered brass,

and then the street entire, then the sun.

What's swallowed whole is not the same as gone—

I've learned that much in eighty years of breath.

The fog would lift by ten, by noon, by one,

and still the houses stood, the same as death

stands patient in the hallway, known, unnamed,

and everything re-emerges, slightly changed.

By March the Charles was moving, dark and free,

the ice dispersed, the surface rough with wind,

and I had seven sonnets, possibly

a self, and one new name that I had pinned

provisionally to the work, to see

if it would hold: not Novak, something other,

a name with room inside it, that could be

a writer's name, not just a mother's mother's.

Hughes. Plain English. Two hard sounds, one syllable.

I wrote it on a draft and left it there.

The river ran below, illegible,

fast-moving, cold, indifferent to my care.

A name is just a container for the work.

I kept it anyway. I liked the dark.

What the forms never asked was how it felt

to carry two names like two different coats —

one heavy wool, inherited, which smelled

of someone else's house, of camphor, boats

pulled up from the Thames in winter, fog

on Southwark Bridge, the whole damp catalogue

of being born into a name, a log

of someone else's crossings, someone's Prague.

The other coat I cut and sewed myself,

imperfect at the seams but mine to wear,

not folded out of sight upon a shelf

but on my back in Stockholm's cold clear air.

I am not reckless with what I left behind.

I simply learned which coat fits my own mind.
