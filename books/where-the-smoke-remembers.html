Where the Smoke Remembers

Xin Wang
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
There is a kind of memory that bypasses language entirely — the memory held in the body, in the nose, in the particular way cold feels when it comes from a specific direction. Xin Wang's haiku in Where the Smoke Remembers do not chart a life through its milestones or migrations but through these smaller, more stubborn registrations: coal smoke rising in a Beijing courtyard, the delayed sound of harbor ice splitting in a Finnish winter, fog moving through San Francisco with the patience of something that has been moving for a long time. The book asks what it means to belong to more than one place, and whether belonging is even the right word for what accumulates in a person who has lived across four latitudes, four weathers, four ways of being quiet. These 250 haiku are arranged not as a travelogue but as a phenomenology — a record of what the senses catch when the mind is not looking. The traditional haiku form, with its insistence on the present moment and the concrete image, becomes here a kind of ethical discipline: no explaining, no translating, no converting one culture's textures into another's vocabulary. A coal brick splits. Ink dries at the corner of a page. A woman's hands move to her collar under a documentary lamp. Wang trusts the image to carry what argument cannot, and the cumulative effect of that trust is something close to a self-portrait — not of a face but of an attention. Readers will find no neat resolution across these seven sections. The smoke does not clear; the fog does not lift into revelation. What the book offers instead is something more honest and more durable: the recognition that displacement, practiced long enough, becomes its own form of intimacy — that to carry a place imperfectly is still to carry it, and that the grain of sand in a suitcase seam is as much a home as anything with an address.
