The Light Keeps Changing

Casey Smith

There is a theory, held quietly by people who have lived in many places, that geography is not merely where you are but what you become. That the angle of light in a particular city lodges itself somewhere behind the sternum. That fog, peat smoke, the sound of a ferry horn at dawn — these do not simply pass through us but accumulate, the way sediment accumulates, until we are partly made of them. Casey Smith's debut collection, 'The Light Keeps Changing,' is built on this theory, and on the long, careful work of testing it against a life. The book moves through seven stations — Lisbon, New York, the Scottish moors, the transit spaces between, Oslo, the quiet rooms of voluntary service, and the borderless country of language itself — tracing not a journey so much as a series of transformations. The haiku form, with its insistence on the present moment and its tolerance for what cannot be said, turns out to be the perfect instrument for this kind of reckoning. Each poem is a latitude fix: here I was, here is what the light was doing, here is what I could not yet name. Taken together, they compose something rarer than a memoir — a cartography of attention, drawn by someone who has learned that paying close attention to a place is one of the most serious ways of loving it. Readers who have lived across borders will find their own sediment disturbed here. Those who have not may find something equally useful: a reminder that even a single life, measured carefully in mornings and ferry horns and sign-in sheets and frost still in the wood at noon, contains more latitudes than we tend to notice.
