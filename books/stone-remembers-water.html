Stone Remembers Water

Andrea Muñoz

A life is not a line. It is a sediment — layer pressed upon layer, each city leaving its particular light behind the eyes, each language carving a slightly different shape into the mouth. Andrea Muñoz was born in Barcelona, crossed the Atlantic, negotiated contracts in three currencies, wrote poems in a Tuesday-night workshop at seventy-eight, and eventually settled in Reykjavik, where the geothermal pavement breathes and the moss holds no opinions. Stone Remembers Water is the record of that accumulation — not a memoir, not a timeline, but a body of small illuminations gathered across nine decades and several continents. Haiku, a form that has always understood that the largest things arrive in the smallest openings, is the natural vessel for this kind of knowing. What Muñoz has discovered — and what these 250 poems enact — is that at a certain depth of living, the borders between inside and outside, between memory and weather, between the self and the stone it leans against, grow thin enough to see through. The orange rind curls. The linden blossom falls on the quarterly report. The candle does not hurry. These are not metaphors so much as accurate reports from a consciousness that has learned to pay attention the way water pays attention to stone: patiently, persistently, and with permanent effect. The sections here are not strictly chronological, though they move, roughly, through the arc of a long life. They are better understood as climates — each one carrying its own quality of light, its own emotional weather, its own particular way of being alive. A reader may enter anywhere. But those who begin at the beginning will find themselves, by the final poem, somewhere they could not have predicted: in a room lit by one candle, looking out at a street that blurs into everything it ever was, held by a silence that is not empty.

orange rind curling—

the sun takes what it can get

before the clouds come

Hudson fog thickens—

I press my palm to the glass

find my own handprint

bicycle lane, six

in the morning, February—

one red light, no one

radiator off

by the time I notice warmth—

Reykjavik trained me

the water tower

does not explain its stillness—

I study the seams

last night's aurora—

this morning only the thought

of what green can do
