Dirt Knows My Name

Quinn Jones
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
There is a particular education that only displacement can give. It begins in the body — in the way unfamiliar heat sits differently on the skin, in the way a new city's noise arrives before its meaning does, in the way your hands reach for tools and words and rituals that belong to a place you no longer live. Quinn Jones has lived this education four times over, moving between Brisbane's subtropical sprawl, Manhattan's vertiginous density, Austin's pale and stubborn earth, and the wide red quiet of rural India — and in each place, the same slow reckoning: the ground does not know you yet, and you must begin again. "Dirt Knows My Name" is the record of that beginning, repeated. Written in haiku — a form that trusts compression, that believes a single image can hold a climate — these poems do not narrate a life so much as they press its moments into something small enough to carry. A jacaranda flower in a storm drain. A fire escape railing sheened with new ice. Caliche splitting white under a boot. A finger writing a Hindi word on a fogged window before the mind has caught up with the hand. Each poem arrives as a fragment of attention, and together they accumulate into something larger: a portrait of a self that has learned, again and again, to tend the unfamiliar — a garden, a language, a way of belonging — until tending itself becomes the home. The collection moves loosely in time but not strictly in sequence, the way memory actually works: place bleeding into place, season rhyming with distant season, a crow in Austin answering a crow in India. Readers who have known the particular loneliness of arrival, or the stranger loneliness of return, will find their experience named here — not explained, but held. That is what haiku does best. It does not tell you what to feel. It sets something in the light and waits.
