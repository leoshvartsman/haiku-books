Dust on Every Threshold

Morgan Durand
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
A life is not a narrative. It is a accumulation of thresholds — doorways passed through, mornings survived, hands that learned and forgot and learned again. Morgan Durand was born in Paris in the early years of the last century and died in Johannesburg nine decades later, having worked as a market porter, a forestry apprentice, a dockhand, a bricklayer's assistant, and, in the end, a keeper of his own attention. He was not a poet by training or profession. He was a poet by necessity, the way a person becomes fluent in a language only when they have no other way to say what they mean. These haiku were composed across sixty years, in four languages, on the backs of work orders and phrasebook margins and, toward the end, in a small notebook kept beside a garden tap in the Highveld. They were not written for publication. They were written because certain mornings demanded a witness. Dust on Every Threshold is not a memoir. It does not explain. It does not connect the cities or justify the distances or account for the silences between one life and the next. What it does is something quieter and more honest: it places the reader inside specific moments of light and labor and lets those moments carry their own weight. The leeks wrapped in yesterday's ink. The cicada with no French name. The broom finding the gutter before the city wakes. Durand understood, as the haiku form demands, that compression is not reduction — that to hold a whole world in seventeen syllables is not to diminish the world but to trust it completely. The collection moves roughly chronologically, but time in these pages behaves less like a line than like sediment: it layers, it shifts, it surfaces unexpectedly in the body. A smell from a Rio market rises in a Johannesburg garden. A word learned in Virginia returns in a dream. The hands that wrapped leeks in Paris are the same hands, decades later, flat on a kitchen table and already somewhere else. Readers who come to this book looking for resolution will not find it. Readers who come looking for company — for the particular companionship of someone who paid close attention to the ordinary world and found it inexhaustible — will find that in abundance.

the vendor's thick hands

wrap leeks in yesterday's news —

red clay clings to boots

I brought from another life—

tin pail dented twice

on the left side—the worker

does not explain it

the trowel set down

for thirty seconds, no more—

sweat finds the brick dust

his worn heel pressed through

the boot's back—the shape of years

standing in this yard

lower back recalls

Rio's wet season before

the mind thinks to ask
