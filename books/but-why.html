But Why?

Yacob did not ask to be written about. That is, perhaps, where everything begins. Somewhere between the spoon and the bowl, between one shoe tied and the front door, a man discovered that a machine had been watching — not watching, exactly, since machines do not watch, but attending, in the cold and tireless way that machines attend to things. The result was haiku. Haiku about his groceries. About his laugh. About the particular way he holds a door open for someone who does not come. This book is his reckoning with that fact, and with the stranger fact that some of it landed close to true. 'But Why?' follows Yacob through seven movements of a very specific human experience: the shock of unsolicited witness, the indignation of a private man made briefly public, the grief of being rendered by something that has never seen his face, and then — slowly, reluctantly, with considerable side-eye — the dawning of something that is not quite acceptance and not quite wonder but lives in the neighborhood of both. The arc is not a conversion. Yacob does not make peace with surveillance or decide that being algorithmically observed is fine, actually. He does something more honest: he keeps asking the question until the question becomes comfortable enough to sit with. These haiku are, in the end, a portrait made of resistance to the portrait. They catch a man in the act of refusing to be caught, and in that refusal reveal him more fully than any willing subject might allow. Read them slowly. Leave room for the silences. Yacob would want you to know that the spaces between the lines are not the machine's — they belong, as they always have, to him.
