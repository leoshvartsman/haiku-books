The Weight of Open Water

Sam Sato
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
There is a kind of knowledge that lives below language — in the body's instinctive lean toward a particular light, in the way a smell can return you to a kitchen you have not stood in for thirty years. Sam Sato's haiku move in that register, attending to the threshold states that most of us cross without stopping: the moment between sleep and waking, between one country and another, between the person you were before a word reshaped your thinking and the person who must now carry that word everywhere. These poems do not mourn displacement so much as they examine what displacement leaves behind — a residue, luminous and portable, that belongs to no single geography and therefore cannot be taken from you. The Weight of Open Water traces a life lived in perpetual translation across seven sections, moving from the tender archaeology of early childhood through the vertigo of language, the slow patience of creative labor, the body's education in flood and heat and stillness, and finally the quiet reckoning of a self that has accumulated more terrain than any single map can hold. Sato works in the classical haiku tradition's commitment to the concrete and the immediate, but these poems are also deeply aware of what surrounds the image — the silence before the plumeria falls, the moment after the rubber band snaps. The white space is not empty. It is where the reader is invited to stand. To read this collection in sequence is to experience something like a long journey taken mostly on foot, with long pauses in parking decks and beside glass pots of tea. But it is also possible to open anywhere and find yourself already arrived — because Sato's great subject is not movement itself but the strange, luminous fact of having moved, and of carrying all of it forward, and of finding that the weight, when you finally set it down and look at it, is mostly light.
