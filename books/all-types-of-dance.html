All Types of Dance

Frank Smith
380 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
Dance is not always beautiful. It is also nervous, sweaty, private, exhausted, and half-remembered. It happens in ballrooms and basements, on cardboard and parquet, in kitchens at midnight and down the center aisle of a church on a Tuesday. It happens in the body before the mind has agreed to it, and it lingers in the body long after the mind has moved on. This collection is not about dance as spectacle. It is about dance as the thing the body does when language runs out — or hasn't started yet. The haiku gathered here move through a single arc: the held breath before the music, the release into motion, the strange wisdom of muscle and bone, and finally the particular silence that follows the last song. Along the way they pass through ballrooms and block parties, through sacred processions and solo spins beside a stove, through the charged geography of two bodies learning to share a tempo. High and low, trained and instinctive, witnessed and entirely alone — all of it is here, and all of it counts equally. Haiku has always been a form alert to the fleeting and the physical — the image that arrives and is already leaving. It seemed the right vessel for this subject. A dancer and a haiku poet are after the same thing: the moment of pure presence, the thing that cannot be held, only inhabited. Frank Smith's poems do not try to freeze that moment. They try to stand inside it, just long enough.

the woman beside me sways

we start the phrase again, slow—
