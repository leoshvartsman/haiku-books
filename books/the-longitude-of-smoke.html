The Longitude of Smoke

Ryan Walsh
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
Ryan Walsh has lived in four cities, worked in four languages, and is, at ninety-six, still paying attention. This collection is the record of that attention — not a memoir in sequence, but something closer to what memory actually does: it burns everything at once. Dublin and Casablanca, Beijing and the Cave Canem workshops in upstate New York, the Liffey and the hutong laneway, his mother's grey coat and a winter plum branch bending under snow — none of these are past. They are simultaneous, held together in the body that has carried them across eight decades and four meridians. The haiku form, with its insistence on the present tense and the concrete particular, turns out to be the only form equal to this kind of simultaneity. Walsh came to haiku late, as he came to several things that mattered most to him — late and with the particular alertness of someone who knows lateness is its own form of luck. He wrote his first haiku at seventy-eight, in a Beijing winter, in a notebook he had intended to use for something else entirely. What he discovered was not a small form but a precise one: a form that could hold a shopping trolley and the January sky in the same breath, that could let a muezzin and an HTML tag open toward the same silence, that could find in the arc of an elder poet's wrist the whole of what it means to be seen. The two hundred and fifty haiku gathered here are arranged not chronologically but geographically and emotionally, tracing the meridians of a life that has refused, stubbornly and joyfully, to resolve into a single place or tongue. The title comes from a question Walsh says he has been trying to answer since childhood: if you could locate smoke on a map — turf smoke settling over Dorset Street, incense drifting from a Casablanca medina, coal smoke rising from a Beijing courtyard — would all those columns of smoke share a longitude? He suspects they do. This book is his working out of the coordinates.

low tide on the Liffey—

a shopping trolley holds

the January sky

server log at dawn:

404, 404—

not found, not found, not

the Atlantic light

is different here than at home—

flatter, less forgiving

I drove through three states

with one unfinished stanza

riding the back seat

someone on the radio

laughs in French — I laugh too, late,

half a beat behind

the bicycle bell—

a sound that exists only

between two silences
