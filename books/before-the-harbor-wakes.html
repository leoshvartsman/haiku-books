Before the Harbor Wakes

Quinn Clark
25 sonnets
Download PDF Download EPUB
These sonnets are organized around four places, but they are not travel poems. They are poems about what the body remembers when it arrives somewhere — the specific weight of a window, the sound a school bus door makes in cold air, the way a crow on a Mumbai ledge can look like a small accountant going over the numbers. Quinn Clark has lived in Maine, Beirut, and Mumbai, and has spent years in the American workshop rooms where language becomes a kind of shelter. What this book tracks is not the movement between those places but the self that persists through them, carrying its names like documents in a coat pocket. The form is the sonnet — fourteen lines, a tradition of argument and turn — and Clark uses it the way an accountant uses a ledger: to find out what balances and what doesn't, to locate the rounding error where the real information hides. The book moves from childhood to a chosen name to a hard-won morning practice, but it does not move in a straight line. Memory doesn't, and neither does grief. What it moves toward is something quieter than resolution — the peace of a name that fits because it has been used, the window faced anyway, the notebook opened again.

Sample Sonnets

My mother's handwriting was very small,

as if she'd learned to write in hiding, pressed

against the page to keep the letters tall

but narrow, conserving space. Each word expressed

with that economy. Kim, she'd write,

and mean: I see you still. The Mumbai crows

begin their argument before the light

has fully come, and in that noise, the close

of childhood opens briefly—the bus door

folding back, the cold entering like a fact,

the driver's face, the road, the frozen shore

of the inlet glazed and grey and exact

as anything my mother ever wrote:

a name, a date, a number, and a coat.

The marked-up drafts accumulate like debt,

each workshop leaving interest on the page,

a stet here, cut there, nothing to forget,

the marginalia of another age.

I came to this in French and came to it

in English, and in Arabic tried once

to write a sonnet where the lines would fit

a script that moves against the usual dunce-

direction of my training, right to left,

and found the volta came too soon, too fast,

the turn arrived before I'd built the theft

of tension that a sonnet needs to last.

The room is always rented. Dawn is free.

The draft is what remains between the me.

At dawn the city grants a moment's truce —

the crows not yet, the vendors not yet, just

the light arriving grey above Colaba, loose

and uncommitted, the way it always must

above a tidal inlet going still

in an October Maine I no longer own,

above a Beirut office on a hill

where the columns balanced and the day was known

by what it cost. I've learned that light is light —

it finds the same face at the same old table,

the same hands around the cup, the same slight

ritual of beginning, the unstable

peace of a name that fits because it's used,

because the morning chose it and I've not refused.
