What the Body Keeps

Parker Sato

There is a version of the travel memoir that ends with arrival — with the self finally located, the coordinates confirmed. This is not that book. What the Body Keeps is concerned with what happens after the moving stops, or slows, or becomes a different kind of motion: the interior kind, involuntary and ongoing. These poems do not describe landscapes so much as they describe the residue landscapes leave — in the wrist's memory of a brushstroke, in the nose's refusal to forget a January kitchen, in the ear's stubborn loyalty to a first language even as the mouth learns another. The body, it turns out, is a poor editor. It keeps everything. The haiku here move through seven stations — a birth month half-remembered, the comedy and ache of translation, the alienating brightness of a northern winter, the slow work of naming a self, the archive of appetite, the particular aliveness of an Osaka summer, and finally the quiet of someone who has stopped waiting to arrive. But they do not move in a straight line, and they do not resolve into a single answer about what it means to be assembled from so many elsewheres. What they offer instead is something more honest: the texture of that assembly, moment by moment, sense by sense. The question this book keeps asking — whether a person made from multiple climates can be said to have a single interior season — is not answered so much as it is lived, in thirty-odd syllables at a time.
