Grease and Monsoon

Krishna Patel

A mechanic learns the world through his hands. He learns it in red laterite dust and tubewell rust, in hydraulic fluid and fluorescent hum, in the particular silence of a creek that has no word for him. Krishna Patel's debut collection follows one body across four countries and four decades, tracing the education that happens below the neck — in the palm that memorizes a torque setting, the thumb that ghosts a chai glass, the knuckle that knows a bolt has given before the mind does. These are not poems about immigration. They are poems about attention, and what attention costs, and what it quietly accumulates. Haiku has always been a form that trusts the body over the argument. It does not explain the frog or the crow; it places you inside the moment the frog or the crow makes undeniable. Patel works in this tradition but brings to it a sensibility tuned to displacement — to the particular alertness of someone who has learned, more than once, that the ground beneath him is not permanent. The seventeen syllables become a unit of survival: small enough to carry across a border, precise enough to hold a whole country inside a smell. Neem pods. Woodsmoke. Engine oil finding every crack it knows. The collection moves through seven sections that are less chapters than weather systems — each with its own pressure, its own quality of light. But the hand that moves through them is always the same hand, and the grease under the nail does not wash out. What Patel is listening for, in the space between all these worlds, is what the book's epigraph calls the blue note: the sound that belongs to no single tradition and is therefore available to all of them. Whether he finds it, or whether finding it is the wrong word entirely, is something the reader must decide — probably more than once, probably in different languages.
