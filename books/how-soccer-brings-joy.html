How soccer brings joy

Number 10
364 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
Soccer is not a metaphor. The ball is real, the mud is real, the net swinging inward after a goal is as real as anything the body has ever witnessed. This collection begins from that insistence — that joy in the beautiful game is not an idea hovering above the pitch but a physical fact embedded in its smallest details: the dew that prints a toe, the hollow clang of ball against woodwork, the tape peeled slowly from an ankle at the end of a long season. Number 10 writes from inside the game, from the place where sensation arrives before thought does, and these haiku are the record of that arrival. The poems move the way a match moves — from the hush of anticipation through the full-bodied roar of eruption and back again to quiet. They follow soccer across seven stations: the charged stillness before kickoff, the sensory life of the pitch itself, the hunger of movement without the ball, the electric compression of the goal mouth, the tenderness of injury and loss, the warm exhaustion of aftermath, and finally the open field where the game continues beyond any single result. Taken together, they trace not a story but a rhythm — the same rhythm the game has always kept. What this book asks of you is simple: slow down to the speed of a haiku. Let a corner flag snapping in wind carry its full weight. Let a deflated ball slumped against a doorframe mean everything it means. Soccer delivers us to ourselves through accumulation — ten thousand small moments that add up, without warning, to something luminous. These poems are an attempt to catch those moments before they disappear into the final whistle, and to show that joy was there all along, living in the grass, the tape, the net, the mud, the step where someone sits and cannot yet undo the knot.

bare foot meets cold grass—

the dew prints each small toe

before the first step

both boots white at kick-off—

by halftime only the right—

he tells you his story

the winger's left shoulder

drops before the rest of him—

the full back drops too

ball clips the outside

of the right post, rolls away—

the striker doesn't watch

tape comes off in strips—

underneath, the same pale skin

that started the game

the sponsor's logo —

a bank, or a car dealership —
