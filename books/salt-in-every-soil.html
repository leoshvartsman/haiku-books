Salt in Every Soil

Quinn Novak
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
These poems were written in the margins of a life that refused to stay in one place. Quinn Novak has lived in Hanoi, Baltimore, Casablanca, and Kathmandu, among other cities that do not appear here by name but whose light is everywhere in these lines — the particular angle of an afternoon, the specific weight of a season's humidity, the way a body learns to move through heat or altitude or grief and then cannot unlearn it. Salt in Every Soil is not a travel book. It is not, strictly speaking, a memoir. It is an account of what the body insists on keeping after the mind has done its reasonable work of letting go. Haiku has always been a form interested in the instant — the hinge between perception and meaning, the moment before interpretation closes over experience like water over a stone. Novak uses that hinge with precision and without sentimentality. These poems do not explain their wounds. They do not resolve their displacements into lesson or consolation. What they do is remain present to the texture of a life lived across latitudes and languages, where the word for home exists in four tongues and fits comfortably in none of them. The collection moves — roughly, not rigidly — from inherited grief toward something that might be called stillness, though Novak is careful never to let that stillness be mistaken for peace. Peace would be easier. This is harder and more honest. The seven sections trace an arc that is less chronological than geological: layers of place and wound and labor and language, compressed until they yield something durable. Readers who have lived between cultures, between languages, between the person they were given and the person they are becoming, will find this terrain familiar. But the poems do not require that biography. They require only attention — the same quality they themselves practice, line by line, in the difficult and ordinary work of staying present to a world that is always, in every soil, a little salt.

lotus root in mud—

I pull and pull and the lake

will not let it go

sweat prints on the sheet

the shape of a body that

could not stay asleep

the fog takes the cranes

apart piece by piece by dawn—

assembles nothing

alkaline dust cloud

behind another man's plow —

I watch from my row

prayer flags unravel

one thread caught on a crow's foot —

the city below

four windows, one room—

all the rain I've ever known

threading the same light
