What the Season Remembers

Ren Yamamoto
25 sonnets
Download PDF Download EPUB
The Japanese haiku tradition uses kigo — seasonal words, small anchors of time and place — to locate a poem in the world. A frog leaping into water means spring. The first frost means something is ending. Ren Yamamoto grew up inside this system, where the calendar was also a kind of grammar, and the natural world spoke in a language everyone around her already knew. Then she left. And the question this book asks, quietly and persistently across twenty-five sonnets, is what happens to that grammar when the seasons stop cooperating — when the plum tree that announced winter in Kyoto has no equivalent in Colorado, and Colorado has no equivalent in Jakarta, and the body keeps reaching for markers that the landscape refuses to provide. What the Season Remembers moves through four cities and roughly six decades of a life: Kyoto in childhood, Boulder in early adulthood, Athens in middle age, Jakarta now. The sonnet — a form with its own internal seasons, its own logic of turn and resolution — becomes the vessel for this inquiry. Yamamoto works the form with both reverence and freedom, trusting its architecture to hold material that resists easy settlement. These are poems about belonging, but they are not anxious poems. By the end, something has been arrived at: not a conclusion exactly, but a way of standing still inside multiplicity. The reader is invited to follow that arrival, step by careful step.

Sample Sonnets

She never called it practice. Never called

it discipline, or art, or meditation.

My grandmother just swept. The courtyard, walled

on three sides, took her broom without ovation.

Dry leaves in autumn, petals in the spring,

the dust of Kyoto in its oldest quarters—

she moved through all of it the same, offering

the ground her full attention, as a daughter

might offer presence to a parent's need:

not gladly always, not with joy each time,

but faithfully. I watched her and agreed

to something wordlessly, the way a rhyme

agrees with what precedes it, not from force

but from the logic of the thing's own course.

The Flatirons at dusk in early fall

go purple first, then something past a name —

a color Murasaki might recall,

though Boulder's palette is a different game.

She wrote in Heian court, in layered silk,

of seasons turning through an inner room.

I write at a formica desk, with milk

gone sour in a cup, in student gloom.

And yet the angle of the failing light

does not collapse time — it reverses it,

makes Heian now, makes now the Heian night,

until the eras lock and perfectly fit.

The mountain holds its purple like a proof:

all comparison is true beneath one roof.

I've watched the Aegean go from blue to steel

to something with no name by four o'clock,

when the tourist boats return and the tourists feel

the particular sorrow of the dock —

the mainland waiting, bags already packed,

the taverna receipt still in the hand,

and something they were reaching for, and lacked

the word for, left behind upon the sand.

I know that feeling from the other side:

the one who stayed, who made the city work,

who learned the language not for joy or pride

but because the contracts needed a clerk

who understood the clause about delays —

I stayed so long I lost the tourist's gaze.
