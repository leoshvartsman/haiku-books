Soccer Fields at Night

Lenny Black
359 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
These poems live in the hours between lunch and the streetlights coming on — in cul-de-sacs and culverts, on bikes with loose chains, at the edges of lots where something was almost built. The suburbs they move through are not exotic or dramatic. They are ordinary in the way that only the places of childhood can be ordinary: completely known and somehow inexhaustible, mapped and remapped by the same bodies over the same summers until the geography becomes a kind of inner weather. The collection follows a year, more or less, though it keeps no strict calendar. It begins in the long unstructured light of June and moves through the slow compression of fall, the bare quiet of November, and back again toward a March that isn't warm yet but is trying. The kids in these poems are never named. They appear in threes and twos and ones, on bikes or standing still, bored or daring or just waiting for something to happen. They are not symbols. They are just kids, which is its own kind of symbol. Haiku has always understood that the smallest moment can hold the largest feeling without announcing it. A sprinkler sweeping a driveway. A shin guard in the wet grass. A bike left outside the first cold night. Lenny Black trusts that understanding completely. Nothing in these poems reaches for more than it finds. And yet by the end of the year — by the time the bike comes out of the garage and the March light falls differently on the fence — something has been said about time and freedom and the particular ache of growing up in a place that will look exactly the same long after you have changed.
