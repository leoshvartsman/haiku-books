Meridians of Forgetting

Aditya Khan
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
A pilot learns to read the sky before he reads the city below it. He learns to trust instruments over instinct, to navigate by coordinates that care nothing for longing. Aditya Khan spent three decades doing both — logging flight hours and accumulating latitudes, moving between the red soil of his childhood and the grey winters of adopted cities, between languages that named the world differently and silences that named it not at all. This collection is the logbook he could not file with any authority: a record of crossings that left no official trace, only a body changed by altitude and broth and the particular quality of light on water that is not the water you first knew. The haiku form is not incidental here. Its compression mirrors the pilot's instrument scan — a quick, disciplined sweep that trusts the reader to feel the altitude. Each poem is a coordinate fix: a moment precise enough to stand in, brief enough to leave. Across two hundred and fifty poems and seven movements, Khan traces what he calls "accumulated belonging" — not the clean narrative of immigration, arrival, assimilation, but something more honest and more weather-worn: the discovery that a self can be plural without being fractured, that carrying four languages in your mouth is not confusion but a kind of chorus. Readers who have lived between homelands will find their own unlabeled feelings named here with startling accuracy. Readers who have not will find something rarer: genuine passage into an interior geography that most literature leaves unmapped. Begin anywhere. The meridians will find you.

one says *neem*, one says *field* —

no word for what's here

my passport photo:

a man who has not yet slept

in this many beds

the tango couple
