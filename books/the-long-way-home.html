The Long Way Home

Parker Lee
25 sonnets
Download PDF Download EPUB
These sonnets were written across three decades and four countries, which is to say they were written in the gaps — between sessions, between time zones, between the person I had been in one place and whoever I was becoming in the next. I did not set out to write a memoir. I set out, each time, to write a single poem, and the poem kept insisting on the same few questions: what do we carry when we leave, what carries us when we can't, and how does a person learn to receive care after a lifetime of learning to give it. The sonnet form is not incidental here. It is, in some ways, the argument of the book. Fourteen lines, a volta, a resolution that may or may not resolve — this is also, more or less, the shape of a good therapy session, and of a good grief, and of the experience of arriving somewhere new and slowly, without quite deciding to, beginning to belong. I was drawn to the form's insistence on compression, on earning the turn, on saying the difficult thing within a structure that holds. I have spent my working life believing that structure is a form of kindness. The sonnet keeps teaching me that I was right. The book moves roughly chronologically — from a childhood in Jerusalem through years of graduate study in Vermont, a long practice in Japan, and a late-life resettlement in Vancouver — but it is not strictly a record of events. It is more a record of what the body kept when the mind was busy elsewhere: the call to prayer that entered before I had any defenses against it, the particular light through a shoji screen, the way the Pacific rain finally asked nothing of me. If there is a through-line, it is this: that learning to sit with other people's pain is slow, lifelong preparation for sitting with your own, and that belonging, when it finally comes, arrives so quietly you almost miss it.

Sample Sonnets

Before the city learned to hold its noise,

the limestone breathed. I watched it from my bed,

that first pale warming, colour without voice,

the walls releasing what the cold had kept.

My window faced the valley. In would come

the fajr call, one man's thread of Arabic

unspooling into morning, into some

wide corridor the ear takes when it's quick

and young and cannot yet protect itself.

I took it in the way the stone took light,

not choosing, just receiving. On the shelf

my mother's cardamom sat, dark and tight

in paper cones. I did not know then, no—

that what enters early never lets you go.

The meadow holds its breath before the dark

comes down. We have pushed back our chairs,

the argument still warm, and now a spark

lifts from the grass — then twelve — the air

becomes a kind of syntax no one taught,

a punctuation made of light and loss,

of things that rise because they must, then not,

then must again. I think of what it costs

to be this visible, this briefly lit,

to signal through the dark and call it speech.

The Green Mountains have gone purple. Sit

with that, my teacher said. Don't overreach.

The fireflies do not overreach. They say

one word, one light, one word, and move away.

The persimmons hang beneath the eaves

in strings of amber, drying into sweet

through weeks of cold, the patience of the frost

doing what no hurrying could do.

I think of what I held in confidence —

the slow sugaring of a grief confessed,

how anger left to hang in open air

loses its hard edge, grows almost tender.

My teacher once said form is what protects

the feeling, not what cages it. I write

these sonnets the way farmers hang their fruit:

trust cold, trust time, trust what the air will do.

The eaves drip. November does its quiet work.

I sit inside and do not rush the dark.
