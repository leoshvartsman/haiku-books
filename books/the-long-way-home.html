The Long Way Home

Jordan White
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
Jordan White began writing these poems at eighty-seven, in a small apartment in Seoul, using a mechanical pencil he had carried since his years in Philadelphia. He is not, by his own account, a poet. He is a retired actuary who has lived in Chicago, New York, Osaka, and Seoul, who has spoken four languages with varying degrees of embarrassment, and who buried his wife of fifty-one years on a Tuesday in March and was back at his desk by Thursday because he did not know what else to do with his hands. The haiku came later, and sideways, the way most true things do. This collection follows the arc of a single life — not as memoir, not as confession, but as a series of small arrests: the moments when the world paused long enough to be seen clearly. White works in the classical haiku tradition while remaining stubbornly, quietly his own — his poems carry the season words and the cuts and the quality of attention the form demands, but they also carry the weight of claim forms and the smell of wool coats and the particular silence of a man who has spent ninety years learning, in city after city, how to listen. The seven sections move roughly in chronological order, from a Chicago childhood through the long middle decades of work and marriage to a late-life arrival in Seoul, but the book is less a timeline than a meditation on what accumulates — what the body keeps, what language cannot hold, what light does to a window when no one is watching. Readers who come to haiku expecting detachment will find instead a tenderness that has been earned the hard way. Readers who come expecting sentiment will find instead a precision that cuts. What White has discovered, over eighty-odd years of paying attention, is that home is not the place you come from or the place you arrive. It is the quality of noticing itself — that moment of full presence, brief and repeatable, that the haiku form was invented to catch and release. This book is one man's long way to that understanding.

only in what I've kept—

in the right language

eul, reul, the object

markers I reach for in speech —
