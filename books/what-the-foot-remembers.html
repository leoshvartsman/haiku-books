What the Foot Remembers

Morgan Ali
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
A body is not a neutral thing. It carries longitude in its joints, altitude in its breath, the memory of particular soils in the soles of its feet. What the Foot Remembers is a life rendered in moments of sensation — not the grand narrative of migration and arrival, but the quieter record kept by skin and muscle and bone: the way red laterite dust settles into a heel's crease, the way mist at a high-altitude window feels like a question you almost understand, the way cold tiles at midnight speak in a language older than any you were taught. Morgan Ali has spent a life crossing thresholds — between countries, between languages, between the selves that different places called into being. These 250 haiku do not attempt to resolve that crossing into a single coherent story. Instead, they trust the form to do what prose cannot: to hold a moment complete in itself, without explanation or apology, and to let the accumulation of such moments become, quietly, a portrait. The haiku here are not decorative. They are acts of attention paid to a world that rewards attention with revelation. Read slowly, and in order if you can. The sections move as a life moves — from formation through searching, from wonder through vocation, from transit through stillness — but they do not march. They circle back. They contradict themselves. They forget and then remember. By the final section, the foot knows things the mind has not yet admitted, and that knowledge, the book argues, is enough.

red dust on my heel—

the laterite road holds heat

long after I've gone

I cross out Sato

with a single horizontal

line — still visible

the mist this morning

carried the smell of something

burning far upslope

she copies my error

faithfully into her book—

I do not correct

someone's jasmine scent

on the seat I have just left —

I leave mine there too

migratory birds:

they know what I took years to learn—

land is not a promise
