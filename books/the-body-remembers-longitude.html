The Body Remembers Longitude

Simone Colombo
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
There is a kind of knowledge the body holds that the mind cannot quite reach — accumulated in the press of bark against a child's cheek, in the groan of river ice heard through a motel wall, in the particular silence that follows a client's careful lie. Simone Colombo has spent decades as a life coach, sitting with others at the edges of their transformations, asking the questions that loosen things. This collection is what happened when she turned that attention on herself. The result is not a memoir and not a confession. It is something more patient than either: a reckoning conducted in the compressed, exacting form of haiku, where every syllable must earn its place and nothing can be hidden behind explanation. The title's meridians are both geographical and bodily — the lines of longitude that mark displacement across continents, and the invisible channels of traditional medicine along which energy, and grief, and habit are said to travel. Colombo has lived across several worlds: a Tuscan childhood, years in the thin air of the American Rockies, a professional life conducted in the glass towers of Singapore, and a present rooted, provisionally, in Osaka. Each move deposited something. Each move required her to leave something behind that she did not always know she was carrying. These poems ask what survives the crossing, and what the body — wiser, slower, and more honest than the self we perform — quietly decides to keep. Readers familiar with haiku's classical tradition will find Colombo in faithful conversation with its core demands: the concrete image, the present moment, the turn that opens rather than closes. But this is also unmistakably a contemporary and autobiographical collection, one that allows the form to hold professional exhaustion, midlife vertigo, immigrant longing, and the strange tenderness of witnessing other people's crises for a living. The seven sections move roughly chronologically through a life, though time here is not linear — it folds, doubles back, and occasionally arrives before it is expected. That, Colombo might say, is how it has always worked.
