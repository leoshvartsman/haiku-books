Salt in Every Mouth

Sky Smith
25 sonnets
Download PDF Download EPUB
These sonnets began as a question about salt — why it appears in every kitchen I have worked in, every coast I have lived beside, every language I have tried to make my own. Salt preserves and salt corrodes. It is what the body loses when it labors and what the cook adds back by hand. It is the taste of the sea and the taste of grief, and in my experience those two things have rarely been far apart. I did not set out to write a spiritual autobiography. I set out to write about food, and the food kept opening into everything else. This book moves through four cities — Manila, Montreal, Irvine, Rio de Janeiro — and four languages that live in my mouth with varying degrees of comfort and claim. It moves, too, through a name change that was less a rupture than a long negotiation, a seam rather than a cut. I have chosen the sonnet because it is a form that knows about containment and pressure — fourteen lines that hold their shape even as something inside them strains. The body is like that. So is a life. So, I think, is the self that survives its own becoming. I have tried to write poems that trust the reader to feel what they are about without being told. The salt is in every mouth. You will know the taste.

Sample Sonnets

Lola's hands were never still. They pressed

the steaming rice against the banana leaf,

each motion practiced past the need to rest,

a grammar older than my disbelief.

The fingers moved the way a tide withdraws—

deliberate, unhurried, pulling back—

and left behind a shape that held its laws,

the puso tight and clean along the crease.

I've worked ten years in kitchens, learned the art

of stock reduced to something dark and true,

but nothing I have made could match that start,

that first instruction, wordless, passing through.

She never named what she was teaching me.

The hands just moved. The rice held its geometry.

In April the St. Lawrence breaks apart—

not all at once, not gently, but in faults

that split like something urgent through the heart

of all that stillness, opening its vaults.

I watched it once from the Pont Jacques-Cartier,

the ice below me seaming, going pale,

the river finding, after months of gray

suspension, what it needed to exhale.

The floes moved south with a deliberate weight,

each one a room the winter had abandoned,

and I was late for service, running late,

the kitchen waiting, everything commanded.

But I stayed. Let the river have its say.

Some breaks remake the ones who choose to stay.

Before the form, a name — I typed it twice,

then pressed delete, then typed it once again,

as if the law could hold what doesn't splice

together cleanly: Sato into rain,

into the river city, into smoke

above a wok, into the hands that learned

a grandmother's language before they spoke

the names of sauces, everything that turned

and kept on turning — what the self becomes

when it refuses burial or bloom

but lives inside the seam. The cursor hums.

The form awaits. I fill the empty room

with both names written once before I choose,

and what I keep is also what I lose.
