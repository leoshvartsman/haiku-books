What the Ice Remembers

Cameron Singh
25 sonnets
Download PDF Download EPUB
Cameron Singh has lived under five names for God and across four continents, and this book is the record of what that kind of living does to a person — not the biography of it, but the weather of it. These sonnets move the way memory moves: not in order, not with clean borders, but with the sudden adjacency of things that happened decades apart and yet belong together. A frozen street in Helsinki sits beside a hillside in Rio. A document signed in a taqueria echoes a krar string ringing past its last note. The form holds what the life keeps trying to exceed. At ninety-five, Singh is writing from a vantage point that is not quite the past and not quite the present but something that contains both — a place where the body has become its own landscape, as layered and as legible as any country he has carried. What the Ice Remembers is not an elegy, though it knows what elegy knows. It is closer to an act of attention: the long, patient work of a man learning, at the end, to hold everything he has been without gripping it too tightly.

Sample Sonnets

Polar dark at four o'clock, the street

a white erasure under lamplight's cone,

and in that silence something made complete

what daylight broke apart—the self, alone,

not frightened yet, just watching from the step

while breath rose up in small, convinced white plumes,

each exhalation proof the body kept

its engine burning in those shuttered rooms.

The night was not a punishment. It asked

a reckoning with what the self contains

when stripped of color, stripped of sun, unmasked

by cold that clarifies the way a plain

of ice clarifies a sky—I breathed and knew:

the dark is not an absence. It goes through.

The bluebonnets grow where no one asks permission,

through caliche cracked like old and sunburned lips,

through limestone rubble, through the long attrition

of August, and they take no scholarship tips.

I'd walk the highway shoulder at the slips

of dawn before the heat locked everything,

and watch them hold their blue in small eclipse

of all that hardness—delicate, offering

nothing but their own insistence there.

A name can be like that, can push through ground

that wasn't made for it, can find the air

not given but arrived at, crack by crack,

till what was sealed begins to give it back.

In Amharic the word for heart is libb,

and when Girma sang, he held the word

the way the tej-bét holds the evening's rib

of warmth — a vessel for what can't be heard

in any other register. I knew

by then three languages of cold reserve,

Finnish, English, Japanese — the blue

and measured distances they each preserve.

But libb in Girma's mouth was architecture,

a room you could stand inside and breathe,

and I who had spent years as a strict student

of restraint learned then what songs bequeath.

I write now in Portuguese, in Rio's heat.

But libb is still the word my meters meet.
