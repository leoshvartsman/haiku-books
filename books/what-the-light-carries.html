What the Light Carries

Quinn Kim

A life is not lived in one place, even when it appears to be. Quinn Kim's haiku move across four countries and several decades, but the real terrain they map is interior: the slow, accumulative work of learning where you are, and who you become in the learning. These poems do not announce their migrations. They arrive quietly, through the angle of winter light on a fondue pot, through the smell of a paddy field before sunrise, through the particular silence of a tatami room. They ask you to be still enough to notice what they notice, and in that stillness, something accumulates. 'What the Light Carries' is organized not as a memoir but as a series of thresholds — moments when the self is caught between what it knew and what it is becoming. Switzerland, Japan, rural fieldwork, a notebook kept across years, the disorientation of languages that do not translate cleanly, and finally the late surprise of Tel Aviv: each section holds a different quality of attention, a different temperature of belonging. Kim does not resolve the tension between rootedness and movement. Instead, the poems honor both the leaving and the staying, the things carried forward and the things that cannot be. Haiku, as a form, has always understood that the largest things arrive in small containers. A thread of melting cheese. A rubber band gone grey. A cloud recognized from childhood, now passing a different window. These poems trust that precision is not smallness — that to see one thing exactly is to touch everything it connects to. Read them slowly. Let the white space do its work.

fondue fork lifted,

the long thread of cheese catching

winter light just so

blossom on stone—

not arranged, not fallen wrong—

simply where it stopped

the farmhouse window

fogs from inside at dawn—

proof someone is there

midday fence post, bare—

the dragonfly gone somewhere

the page still open

he asks where I'm from

and I list four cities slowly

like a train schedule

the crumbling Bauhaus

cornice holds a pigeon pair —

architecture adapts
