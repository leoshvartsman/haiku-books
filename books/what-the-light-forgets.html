What the Light Forgets

Saanvi Singh

There is a kind of forgetting that is not failure. It happens slowly, in the body before the mind notices — a word that was once automatic now requiring a moment's reach, a smell that arrives before its name does, a gesture the hands still know long after the reason for it has dissolved. What the Light Forgets is a book about these quiet losses and quieter survivals: not the dramatic uprootings but the gradual ones, the kind that only become visible in retrospect, when you realize you can no longer quite reconstruct the thing you were so certain you would always carry. Saanvi Singh moves through these poems across continents and languages — a village in India, a winter coast, the fish markets of Busan, the unhurried mornings of Rome — but the true geography of this collection is interior. It is the space between what a place gives you and what it takes without asking, between what you chose to leave and what left without your consent. The haiku form, with its demand for the precise and the present, becomes in Singh's hands a tool for catching what is almost gone: the detail that persists after the narrative has faded, the sensation that outlasts its context, the word whose meaning has slipped but whose shape in the mouth remains. This is not a book of arrivals or departures. It is a book of thresholds — the held breath, the half-remembered, the thing that survives translation not intact but alive. Read it as you would follow a figure moving through rooms where the lights are being turned off one by one, not in grief, but in the ordinary way of evenings, noticing what the darkness keeps.
