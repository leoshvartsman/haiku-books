Between Ice and Thaw

Cameron Brown
250 haiku

A haiku is a small door. You do not walk through it so much as you press your eye to it and see, for a moment, what is on the other side. In Between Ice and Thaw, Cameron Brown opens door after door — in Toronto alleyways and Vienna arcades, in fluorescent workshop rooms and on the open deck of a Norwegian ferry — and what you find on the other side is not scenery but sensation: the particular weight of a winter that will not name itself, the fork held between two languages, the sun that refuses to go down. These poems are short, but they are not small. They carry the freight of diaspora, of inheritance, of a young Black Canadian moving through the world's rooms and learning to read each one. The collection is organized as a journey, though not a triumphant one. Brown does not arrive anywhere in the conventional sense. Instead, the poems trace a weather system — shifting pressure, changing light, the body's slow calibration to each new climate. From the lake-grey winters of home through the humid intensity of an American fellowship, the formal grandeur of Vienna, and the raw coastal silence of Norway's coast, the self in these pages is always in transit, always slightly off-center, and always, stubbornly, present. Memory and language are the twin instruments Brown uses to navigate: French and English, Norse and silence, the cursive of a father's letter and the low syllables of a mother's lullaby. Haiku has long been a form preoccupied with the moment of perception — the instant when the world and the mind meet and something true passes between them. Brown brings that tradition into contact with a contemporary Black diasporic consciousness, asking what it means to be fully present in a place that was not made with you in mind, and what it means to find beauty there anyway. The stillness these poems arrive at is not peace exactly. It is something harder and more honest: the stillness of a person who has learned to stand in the weather without flinching, and to notice everything.
