Concrete Roots, Open Sky

Diya Kapoor
250 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
These poems began, as most honest things do, in the body — in the smell of rain hitting hot asphalt, in the particular silence of a therapy room after someone has said something true, in the shock of a fig tree splitting a marble curb in a city I had no right to love as much as I did. I have lived in enough places to know that nature does not save itself for forests. It comes for the grout lines, the drainage ditches, the gaps behind the signage. It comes whether or not you are watching. Writing haiku in cities taught me that attention is itself a form of belonging — that you do not have to be from somewhere to be changed by it, and you do not have to stay somewhere for it to stay in you. This collection moves through seven landscapes, though landscape may be too grand a word for what I mean. I mean the gutter outside my mother's building during monsoon. I mean the spider plant in my consulting room that has been reaching toward the window for three years without complaint. I mean the bougainvillea on a glass facade in Bangalore that has not once asked permission. Each section is a different city, a different quality of light, a different way of being lost or found — but the same question runs underneath all of them: what does it mean to grow in conditions that were not designed for you? I have asked this of my clients. I have asked it of myself. The weeds, it turns out, have the most useful answers. Haiku has always seemed to me the form most honest about what we actually experience — not the whole story, but the moment the whole story turned on. A sandal lifted above floodwater. A name half-written in ink. A sapling bending in a storm drain, righting itself, bending again. I offer these not as conclusions but as evidence: that something in us, like something in the peepal and the fig and the moss between the slabs, keeps finding the crack and keeps going through it.
