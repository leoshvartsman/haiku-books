The Names I Carried

Aarav Reddy
25 sonnets
Download PDF Download EPUB
These sonnets follow a man through four cities and two names — Kapoor, then Reddy, Delhi to New York to Accra to Copenhagen — but they are not, finally, a story about leaving. They are a story about what travels with you: the specific sound of a gate, the colour red in all its migrations, the way ambition can harden around a person before he notices. Aarav Reddy has written a sequence that trusts the form's old architecture — fourteen lines, the pressure of the turn — to hold the weight of a life that kept refusing to stay still. A word about the title. To carry a name is not the same as owning one, and not the same as losing one either. What this book proposes, quietly and with considerable patience, is that identity is less a thing possessed than a thing translated — carried across borders the way a word is carried between languages, arriving on the other side changed in register, in feeling, in the company it keeps, but not emptied of its original meaning. The man at the end of this book is not the boy at its beginning. He is also, unmistakably, the same person.

Sample Sonnets

What is the word for knowing late that you

were happy in a place you couldn't wait

to leave—the yellow smog, the avenue

of neem trees on the road, the rusted gate

that needed lifting as you pushed it through,

the sound it made, specific as a name,

the way the monsoon hit the tin roof too,

percussive, loud, and every year the same?

I left at twenty-something, full of plans,

and took the name Reddy like a coat,

and put my Delhi childhood in my hands

and did not look at it. Now from remote

and fog-grey Copenhagen I look back:

a courtyard full of red. A gate. A crack.

The wire transfer: two hundred, maybe less,

from the account I kept at Chase on Broadway,

sent back to Delhi, south by southwest,

reducing both my balance and my birthday.

I worked on Thursdays at the bank, a clerk

in everything but title, entering sums,

learning that the world's most serious work

is moving numbers into other columns.

The MFA cost what a life in Delhi

costs across ten years of middle comfort.

I paid it without drama, paid it fully,

and felt the zero balance as a verdict.

To be useful in the currency of others

is how I learned to pay for what I'd smothered.

Twelve years I moved through corridors of glass

and polished floors that held my own reflection

looking competent, and let it pass

for confidence, for purpose, for direction.

The poems lived inside a notebook, small,

a blue cloth cover bought in Osu market,

and I was careful, wrote against the wall

of silence, kept the notebook in my jacket.

The bank required a version of my mind

that worked in columns, cleared the foreign funds,

translated value into the assigned

denominators, spoke the numeral tongues.

The other mind was slower, wrote by hand.

Both needed Accra, neither one had planned.
