Every Border a River

Riley Ali

A life is not a single place. It is the fog that rolls off the Pacific before the city has decided to exist, the red dirt of Texas baking under a sky that has never heard your first language, the blue lights spinning across Stockholm ice at three in the morning, the pohutukawa bleeding red into an Auckland harbour you have only just learned to call yours. Riley Ali's 'Every Border a River' is a collection built from thresholds — the doorways between countries, languages, seasons, and selves that a single body must cross, again and again, in order to survive. Written across seven sections and a lifetime of migrations, these haiku do not mourn displacement so much as examine it with the precise, unhurried attention of someone who has learned that attention is the only home you can carry. From childhood in Lima's garúa — that soft, grey, oceanic fog — through the sensory rupture of the American Southwest, the compressed urgency of a paramedic's years in Stockholm, and the long, uncanny Swedish light, to a final harbor in Tāmaki Makaurau, Auckland, the poems trace what accumulates in a body that has never stopped moving: grief, fluency, muscle memory, wonder, the particular tenderness of a person who has had to learn the word for rain more than once. Haiku, with its devotion to the present moment and its trust in what is left unsaid, turns out to be the ideal form for a life lived at borders. Each poem here is a threshold in miniature — seventeen syllables wide, a whole world on either side. Readers are invited not to follow a narrative so much as to stand still, briefly, in each of the places this life has passed through, and to feel, as Ali does, that the passing itself is the point.
