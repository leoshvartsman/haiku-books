Wind Off the Strait

Sky Novak
250 haiku

There is a particular kind of attention that only becomes possible after you have left. Sky Novak's Wind Off the Strait is a book built from that attention — patient, precise, and quietly relentless. Moving across decades and hemispheres, from the wind-scoured harbour of Wellington to the peat-dark stillness of rural Scotland, with long stops in the American South and the civic machinery of Chicago, these haiku ask what a person carries when they keep moving, and what, if anything, can be set down. The answer, this collection suggests, is not very much — but the carrying itself changes you. Novak came to haiku through politics, which is perhaps the most honest way to arrive at a form devoted to what cannot be argued. These poems bear the marks of that history: they notice power, they notice who is cold and who is not, they notice the flag hanging limp and the placard dissolving in sleet. But they also do what haiku has always done — they stop. They hold a single moment until it opens into something larger than itself, or admits that it will not open, and that this too is information. The political animal does not disappear in these pages; it learns, slowly and with some resistance, to go quiet. The haiku accumulate here the way weather accumulates: no single poem announces the change, but by the final section something has shifted in the air. Readers may find themselves, as Novak clearly did, surprised by what the small form can hold — a grandmother pausing at a ferry horn, red clay tracked through a seminar room, a corridor of airport light that belongs to no country. This is a book about distance as a form of knowledge, and about the stubborn, necessary work of staying present to what you have loved and lost and cannot stop naming.

Sample Haiku

the ferry horn low—

my grandmother stops speaking,

listens, then forgets

workshop fluorescence

flattens every face the same—

still, someone weeps once

corner store owner

sweeps the step before the cold

can make it harder

the sign stake remains

long after the face is gone—

wire rusts in clay

one stamp from Chicago,

the date smudged—even the ink

unsure I was there

frost on the window—

the pattern has no meaning

and I am relieved
