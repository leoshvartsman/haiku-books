What the Rain Remembers

Sam Lewis
25 sonnets
Download PDF Download EPUB
These sonnets began as an attempt to answer a question that kept arriving in different weather: what does a person carry when they have spent their life in motion? Not luggage — the other things. The residues. The way a particular quality of morning light can make you briefly eight years old again, or the way grief finds you not in the expected moments but in a river going the colour of something you grew up beside. Sam Lewis has lived across four landscapes — Scottish moorland, Oxford, rural India, Taiwan — and this book is neither memoir nor travelogue but something stranger and more honest: a reckoning with what moves through a person when the person keeps moving. The sonnet form is itself a kind of argument here. Its fourteen lines, its volta, its old habit of turning back on itself — these feel like the right container for a mind that is always negotiating between what it knew and what it knows now. The poems appear in English, with Scots Gaelic and Mandarin arriving at the edges, not as decoration but as evidence: some things can only be said in the language that shaped them, and the gaps between languages are where much of this book lives. Readers who share none of these languages will still find the gaps legible. That is the wager the book makes, and it earns it.

Sample Sonnets

There was a language the glen had at dawn,

before the quad bikes started, before school,

a dialect of curlew-call and drawn-

out mist across the field, a kind of rule

of quiet that preceded everything.

I'd wake to it and lie there, eight years old,

the window running wet, the slow beginning

of light like something cautious, something cold

deciding whether to commit. I have

tried to write this in three languages now

and each one fails a different way, a half-

translation of a sound I don't know how

to hold in any alphabet I own.

Some mornings I wake here and hear the stone.

The Thames at Iffley in February ice:

I cycled out there once to clear the room

of other voices, paid a reckless price

in cold — my hands gone white inside their gloom

of fingerless gloves, the reeds along the bank

locked rigid in their gestures, the grey flow

beneath the grey sky carrying the rank

and patient smell of silt, of let it go.

I'd failed a supervision. Not the work —

the self inside the work, too visible,

some rawness showing at the edge, a quirk

of feeling that a tutor found divisible

from argument. He marked it: too intense.

The river didn't care. That made some sense.

The betel nut girl is gone from the kiosk,

the plastic booth still lit but shuttered through

with yellow light. The heat brings out the musk

of temple incense from the lane. I knew

a temple smell in Rajasthan: marigold

and ghee and something underneath, the ground

itself participating, something old

released by heat. Here it's a different sound

the incense makes — I mean the way a smell

can sound, can have a register, a key.

Sandalwood and rain. The temple bell

is counted out in pairs. I let it be

instruction: stand here, pay attention, wait —

the unfamiliar opens like a gate.
