Acting Up

William Kaitel
360 haiku
Download PDF Download EPUB
We are always, in some sense, in rehearsal. We learn our lines from the people who raised us, from the rooms we waited in, from the faces we studied and slowly became. We develop a repertoire of gestures — the way we pass the salt, the way we hold a steering wheel, the way we arrange our expressions before walking through a door. Most of the time, we do not notice we are performing. That is how good we have gotten at it. Acting Up follows the arc of that performance: from the anticipatory dread of the audition to the quiet aftermath when the set is struck and the costumes are folded away. Along the way, it moves through mimicry and restlessness, through the vertiginous moment when the script runs out, through the solidarity of others waiting in the wings, and through the raw, unguarded instants when the mask slips and something unscripted shows through. These haiku do not argue that performance is false and authenticity is true. They suggest, instead, that the distance between the two is where most of living happens — and that it is worth paying attention to. Haiku, with its insistence on the concrete and its tolerance for the unresolved, turns out to be a natural form for this subject. A waiting room chair angled slightly wrong. A shopping cart rolling through an empty parking lot. A painted sky rolled up and rubber-banded with the rest. The poems here do not explain what these images mean. They trust that you have been in the room, that you know the chair, that you have felt the particular loneliness of a laugh moving through a closed door and not being meant for you. They trust, in other words, that you have been human — which is to say, that you have been performing all your life, and that you are very tired, and that occasionally, in the right light, you have been something close to real.

waiting room chairs, cold—

each one angled slightly wrong

for the body brought

at the long oak table

everyone cuts meat the same

particular way

applause from the left

side only, then it catches—

then it stops catching

empty parking lot,

my car alone at the far end —

I chose that on purpose

two in the kitchen,

her light on, mine off—we share

the same sleepless hour

I turned the mirror

face-down before the review —

I read it anyway
