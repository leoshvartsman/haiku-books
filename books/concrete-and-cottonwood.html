Concrete and Cottonwood

Jamie White has always lived in two registers at once: the world as it is sold to us, and the world as it insists on being. Concrete and Cottonwood moves between Denver and New Orleans, between a sales floor and a workshop porch, between the person who had a quota and the person who learned to stand still long enough to watch an anole's throat flash red. These haiku are the record of that education — not a dramatic conversion, but a slow, persistent noticing, the kind that happens when the living world refuses to wait for your attention and simply takes it. A cottonwood seed drifts through a July alley. A weed finds the crack where the handicap sign sank. The river decides something. You were already watching.
