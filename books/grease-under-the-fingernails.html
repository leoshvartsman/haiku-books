Grease Under the Fingernails

Krishna Patel
25 sonnets
Download PDF Download EPUB
These sonnets began as a question about hands — what they carry across borders that the rest of the self is still trying to name. Krishna Patel has worked as a mechanic in four countries, and this book moves through those four landscapes the way an engine moves through its cycles: pressure, release, the long idle, the thing that finally turns over. The red clay of a Gujarat monsoon, the limestone fog of the Tennessee Plateau, the acid haze of Pudong at three in the morning, the river gums peeling along Melbourne's Yarra — each place left its residue, and the sonnets are where that residue gets examined. The form matters here. The sonnet has always been a machine for holding two things in tension — the octave and the sestet, the problem and the turn — and Patel finds in that structure something like the mechanic's own discipline: the torque spec, the clearance tolerance, the moment when you feel the thread seat rather than see it. Jazz enters too, its blue notes bending the pitch between major and minor, belonging to neither scale entirely. That in-between place — not quite here, not quite there, fluent in the grammar of several worlds and native to none — is where this book lives. It doesn't resolve that condition so much as learn, slowly, to work within it.

Sample Sonnets

The clay splits clean as a fault line, then drinks—

I watched it once, that first rain hitting dust,

the courtyard gasping through its dried-out links,

the earth accepting water like it must.

My grandmother bent low to touch the seam

where wet met dry, her fingers reading mud

the way a mechanic reads a cooling stream

of oil, the way a body reads its blood.

I've touched cold steel in Shanghai, read its grain

for heat-warp, stress, the lie a bearing tells—

but nothing taught me fracture like that rain,

the way need opens, swallows, and compels.

The clay remembered what the drought forgot:

that breaking wide is not the same as rot.

The dogwood doesn't know my name, which seems

correct — I haven't earned these Tennessee hills,

their limestone patience or the way fog streams

through cedar gaps and then, without a fuss, stills.

I came from red earth, from the smell of dung

and diesel, from a courtyard where the heat

pressed down like something old and iron-lunged.

Here, white blossoms open in the sleet.

I don't know what to do with so much pale,

with petals notched like something was once torn,

with beauty that arrives without travail,

without a season first of being worn.

The workshop starts at nine. I'll bring my doubt.

The dogwood will continue without me, no doubt.

Chalk on the repair order, three characters

I sound out slow, my Mandarin still raw

around the edges, catching on the barriers

of tone I flatten with my Rajasthani jaw.

The customer has left his number, left

his car, his trust, a kind of small surrender.

A man who hands his engine over, bereft

of the know-how, is briefly, quietly tender.

I think of how my uncle left his tractor

idling in the field and walked inside,

how someone had to be the fixing factor,

the one who stayed and worked while others cried.

I write the estimate. I make the call.

The Mandarin comes out. It works. That's all.
