Where the Light Changes Language

Taylor Andersson

There is a kind of knowledge that cannot be carried in a suitcase — the way equatorial rain smells before it arrives, the particular silence of a desert that has been burning for ten thousand years, the color of an Arctic sky that has no name in any language you were born into. Taylor Andersson has spent a life accumulating this knowledge in the only way it can be accumulated: by standing still long enough in each place to let it enter the body, and then by moving on. Where the Light Changes Language is the record of that accumulation — not a travel memoir, not a confession, but a series of small arrests, moments caught at the exact instant when landscape and interior life became indistinguishable from each other. The haiku gathered here span continents and decades, moving from the luminous, rain-soaked altitude of Bogotá through the red solitude of the Australian Outback to the long volcanic winters of Reykjavik. But geography is not finally the subject. The subject is what geography does to a person — how each climate installs a different set of instincts, a different grammar for noticing, a different threshold for what counts as beauty or loss. A life measured in latitudes is also a life measured in the successive selves those latitudes made necessary, and this collection is, among other things, an honest accounting of that making. Haiku has always been a form suited to the threshold — the moment between seasons, between sleeping and waking, between what is seen and what is understood. Andersson uses that threshold quality with particular precision, trusting the white space around each poem to hold what the words cannot. Readers are invited not to move through this book quickly but to pause at each poem the way one pauses at a window when the light outside has done something unexpected — not quite able to name it, but unwilling to look away.
