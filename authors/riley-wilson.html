Selected Sonnets

What the snow did was make the city legible,

each track a sentence in a script of pressure,

each print a word, the whole made eligible

for reading if you slowed to take the measure.

The dog went here. The child ran here and stopped.

Someone stood under this window, looking up.

A bottle rolled and here is where it dropped.

The city writes itself and then the cup

of sky tips over and erases all,

begins the page again by morning, clean,

and I am in Miami in the fall

when nothing falls but rain, and I have been

trying to read a city that won't write.

The snow gave me a way to read. The light.

The fluorescent hum above the seminar table

makes everything confessional and pale—

our faces lit like something in a fable

where truth arrives in sections, without fail.

Outside, the sleet was building on the brick,

the rowhouses in February bent

toward each other like the grammar-sick

who lean to hear what revision meant.

Someone read a draft aloud. The room

absorbed it like a river takes a stone,

without applause, without the word for bloom,

just the dull fact of weight and depth and bone.

I wrote it down. The margin held my hand.

I wasn't ready yet to understand.

The mangrove roots reach down like question marks

into the brackish water, half in air,

half gripped in silt where something nameless parks

itself and waits — I recognize that stare.

I've stood at other edges, other tides,

the Tagus silver-grey at shift's last fare,

the Charles in October when it hides

its cold beneath a surface almost fair.

But here the water's warm and doesn't freeze,

the roots breathe up through mud they also hold,

and what I took for drowning was just these —

small architectures making good on old

arrangements: neither leaving nor the same.

The GPS says: recalculating. I came.
