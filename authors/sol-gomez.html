Sol Gómez

3 collections

Sol Gómez's work moves between specific coordinates: the sunbeams of Madrid's streets, California's golden skies, a river bend remembered from some unnamed elsewhere. Jasmine. Twilight. The small, exact moment before it dissolves. Three collections of haiku built from the space where self and surroundings lose their edges.

The work asks what remains when a moment passes — whether catching it in language preserves anything at all, or only marks the loss. No resolution offered. Only the next moment, and the next, held briefly in place.

Selected Sonnets

The Guadarrama range turns white in January,

a cold fact above the roofline, above the aerials

rusting their slow rust. I was eight. The ordinary

morning milk, the radiator's cough, the serials

my mother watched at noon—all of it arranged

below that ridge like furniture beneath a shelf.

The mountains did not care. That much I learned, estranged

already from the city's noise, already half

in love with what refused me: stone, altitude, the thin

air of a place I couldn't reach by metro or by bus.

Now sixty, I still look for ridgelines. Lisbon's skin

is light and salt, its hills are low, they make no fuss.

But some cold mornings, squinting west toward the Tagus mouth,

I find Guadarrama, north, where north is not south.

The carrel window held a single pine

against a white November sky—Torrey,

ancient, wind-torqued, as if some design

had tried and failed to make the tree obey

a straighter form. I knew that kind of failing.

My notebook held three drafts of the same thought,

each version slightly worse, the logic trailing

at the turn. Outside, the pine stood caught

mid-lean, its roots gripping the sandstone shelf

the way a hand grips something it won't name.

I watched it long enough to find myself

less interested in straightness than the claim

a body makes against the force that bends—

that angle held, held, until the holding ends.

The camera doesn't save the thing it sees.

I learned this early, learned it with the creek

the summer that we shot the dying trees

above the dam—three days, the whole technique

correct, exposure right, the focus true,

the sound crew catching even the dry crack

of branches giving up their last of dew.

The film was beautiful. The creek stayed cracked.

We screened it twice, a festival, a grant.

I stood for questions in a darkened room.

Outside, the Texas summer was adamant.

Someone asked about the film's costume.

I want to be wrong about what film can do.

I want the river back more than I do.
