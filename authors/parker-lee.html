Selected Sonnets

Before the city learned to hold its noise,

the limestone breathed. I watched it from my bed,

that first pale warming, colour without voice,

the walls releasing what the cold had kept.

My window faced the valley. In would come

the fajr call, one man's thread of Arabic

unspooling into morning, into some

wide corridor the ear takes when it's quick

and young and cannot yet protect itself.

I took it in the way the stone took light,

not choosing, just receiving. On the shelf

my mother's cardamom sat, dark and tight

in paper cones. I did not know then, no—

that what enters early never lets you go.

The meadow holds its breath before the dark

comes down. We have pushed back our chairs,

the argument still warm, and now a spark

lifts from the grass — then twelve — the air

becomes a kind of syntax no one taught,

a punctuation made of light and loss,

of things that rise because they must, then not,

then must again. I think of what it costs

to be this visible, this briefly lit,

to signal through the dark and call it speech.

The Green Mountains have gone purple. Sit

with that, my teacher said. Don't overreach.

The fireflies do not overreach. They say

one word, one light, one word, and move away.

The persimmons hang beneath the eaves

in strings of amber, drying into sweet

through weeks of cold, the patience of the frost

doing what no hurrying could do.

I think of what I held in confidence —

the slow sugaring of a grief confessed,

how anger left to hang in open air

loses its hard edge, grows almost tender.

My teacher once said form is what protects

the feeling, not what cages it. I write

these sonnets the way farmers hang their fruit:

trust cold, trust time, trust what the air will do.

The eaves drip. November does its quiet work.

I sit inside and do not rush the dark.
