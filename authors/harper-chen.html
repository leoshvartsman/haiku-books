Selected Sonnets

The river took what morning gave it: light

in strips across the hyacinths' blue crowns,

each bloom adrift, unhurried in its right

to float through all the city's waking sounds.

I stood at six years old on crumbling stone

and watched the brown water receive the sky,

not knowing yet that beauty asks to be known

before it slips—that rivers also die.

The sampans knocked their wooden hulls together.

A woman lifted baskets, called a name.

The air was warm the way it's always warm there,

a weight that settles on you like a claim.

I carry it, that river and its load,

the way a jar holds rain long after rain has slowed.

The Thames at low tide showed its ribbed grey floor,

a palm turned upward, lined and asking nothing,

the mud still holding shapes from days before —

a boot print, tyre track, the press of something.

I stood on Waterloo Bridge in my coat,

looking down at what the river hides

most hours, thinking of a line I wrote

that wasn't right, about the turning tides.

There is a word in Arabic, *wadi* — dry,

a riverbed that waits without complaint,

that knows its own diminishment and why

the holding of a shape is not restraint.

The Thames at low tide was not loss. It read

like a hand that had something left to said.

He hands the empty cup back with both hands,

the way a child is taught when something's given —

that courtliness. He's seven, maybe. Stands

a moment longer than he needs to, driven

by nothing I can name. His eyes are clear

and darker than the water was. I take

the cup, say شكراً, and he disappears

back through the tent flap, leaving in his wake

a kind of reckoning I haven't words

for in any of my four languages.

Vietnamese holds tenderness. English guards

its feelings at a formal distance. French

is elegant with loss. But Arabic

holds silence differently — holds it thick.
