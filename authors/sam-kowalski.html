Selected Sonnets

Before the city woke, the limestone caught

the first pale fire along its pitted face,

and I would watch from our roof how light was brought

like something owed the stone, returning to its place.

The walls held centuries the way my hands

held nothing yet — too young to name the weight

of all that gold-tinged silence, how it lands

inside a child who learns too soon that late

is how the light arrives, and briefly stays.

I didn't know then what I carried home

each morning back to breakfast, how the blaze

of limestone at first light becomes the bone

of everything you build when you are grown —

the ache of beauty seen before it's known.

The smog comes in from Tláhuac, settles, stays.

By five o'clock the mountain has been swallowed whole.

My clients move through the city's amber haze

with papers in their bags and papers in their soul.

I learned in childhood that a city holds its dead

inside its walls, inside its dust, its morning call.

Jerusalem taught me that the stones are also bread,

that something feeds you even as it makes you small.

Here, in Colonia Doctores, different stones:

the broken sidewalk, the taped-up office glass,

the chair whose leg is shimmed with folded phones—

everything repaired to last a little past.

I come from cities built on older cities' grief.

I work in one that offers law in place of relief.

What is abundance if it also aches?

Mercado Jamaica has its answer: build

the marigold so high the scaffold breaks

your sense of scale, until your eye is filled

with orange that outranks the afternoon.

And still somewhere below the vendor's hands

are cracked, are calculating, done too soon

with sleep, with ease, with all that living demands.

I learned this from the people I called clients—

that need and beauty are not opposites,

that in the most exhausted, there is science

in how they fold a life until it fits.

The market is cathedral, market, tomb.

The marigolds make every kind of room.
