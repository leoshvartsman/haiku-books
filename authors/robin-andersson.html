Robin Andersson

3 collections

Robin Andersson's work moves through Istanbul's worn stones, seven coastlines, the light that refuses to stay in one place. Haiku caught between languages. A body measured in water rather than years. The Bosphorus in childhood; the sea again at the end.

The work asks what persists when light travels across decades and continents — whether the same brightness that fell on crumbling streets can mean the same thing from a different window. No resolution offered. Only the moment held still long enough to notice it.

Selected Sonnets

The Atomium stands in January fog,

its steel spheres hung like breath above the plain,

each molecule enlarged, each catalogue

of atoms sealed in silver, cold, and vain.

My father switched to French mid-argument,

my mother answered back in slow, firm Dutch,

and I sat parsing what the other meant

by tone alone, by table-edge, by touch.

The endives in the cellar lost their green

to darkness, blanched to something soft and pale,

a vegetable logic: what has been

deprived of light becomes more fit for sale.

I learned to read the room in both tongues then,

to thrive on what the dark withheld from men.

The Hudson holds the dark like a held note,

three in the morning and the water black,

a saxophone phrase unraveling its coat

of sound across the bridge and not coming back.

I stood at Morningside and felt the cold

come off New Jersey like a verdict read

aloud — the kind of cold that makes you bold

or makes you nothing, strips you down to need.

Brussels gave me fog, but fog with purpose,

fog that knew the tram lines, kept the route —

this dark had no such loyalty, no surface

to press your palm against and feel it out.

The river kept the beat the city lost,

and I stood counting what the crossing cost.

At 96th the busker holds his ground

between the uptown and the downtown track

and plays the changes slow, rebuilding sound

from what the last express tore off its back.

I learned in London how a room can turn

on one voice finding the right time to speak —

not louder, but more certain, let it burn

clean through the noise by being sharp andeak

with truth. The saxophonist knows this too:

he plays against the rumble, not with it,

the way you write against what's told to you,

the way a volta doesn't just submit.

The downtown train arrives. He doesn't stop.

The note keeps going over all the drop.
