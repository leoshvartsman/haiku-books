Selected Sonnets

The murmurating starlings never land

on anything for long—they are the motion,

not the resting. I have tried to understand

this as permission, as a kind of ocean-

crossing logic: that the shape you make

in moving is itself a form of home,

that restlessness and reverence can take

the same road without one devouring one.

I am fifty-four. I live in Philadelphia.

I write in English and I dream in both.

The Tiber runs in January still—

green, slow, indifferent, and beautiful—

and I am here, and I am also there,

the ochre falling softly through the air.

The suitcase lay unlatched on the thin mattress,

its hinged mouth breathing Rome into the room—

a smell of cedar, lavender, andress

shirts folded by my mother in the gloom

of our apartment on a Tuesday morning.

I pressed my palms against the fabric lining

as if the warmth still lived there, still adorning

the cloth with something prior to resigning

myself to this: the pale Californian light

that fell through dormitory blinds like chalk,

the quad already bleached by August's white

insistence, and the oak trees' amber stalk

of shade that seemed to ask what I had brought.

I named each item. Counted what I'd thought.

I've learned to love the wry side of the work,

the way a slogan fails in its ambition,

the earnest *synergy* that starts to smirk

at its own face somewhere past the third revision.

There is a comedy in making meaning

for products that will live six months and fade,

a kind of lightness in the overweening

seriousness with which the pitch is made.

In Rome we call it *teatro*—not dismissal,

more like acknowledgment that performance holds

a truth adjacent to the thing, the gristle

of real life wrapped in something that unfolds.

I play my part. The whiteboard gets erased.

I've built a life on what cannot be placed.
