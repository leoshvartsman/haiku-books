Selected Sonnets

What I want to say about that town is this:

it didn't know it was a place I'd leave.

It offered what it had — the hard dark bliss

of winter mornings, the particular weave

of French and English on the radio,

the creek, the spruce, the rink's fluorescent hum,

the bus, the silence — without needing to know

it was a country I'd be exiled from

before I'd learned to name it. Now I write

from warm rooms in a city built for sun,

in languages my childhood had no right

to, reaching back for what cannot be undone:

that frozen place, those nine o'clock dark streets,

the first country the body never leaves.

The formstone rowhouses on Calvert Street

held rain the way a person holds a grief—

not visibly, but in the sheen, the sleet-

grey surface that refuses all relief.

I'd lived in hutong alleys, slatted walls,

in rooms that smelled of coal smoke and machine oil,

in a bachelor flat where winter falls

through single panes and frosts the bathroom tile.

But Baltimore had something past the cold,

a beauty pressed so tight against the sad

it bruised—the white marble steps, the mould

of ornament on brick, the neighbourhood's bad

luck worn like ceremony, like a creed.

I took the long way home. I had no need.

I learned the character for crisis late:

危机, danger threaded through with chance—

the teacher drew it slowly on the slate

and said, you see, it holds two things at once.

I thought of Baltimore, the seminar's

bright argument about the lyric I,

whether the self survives its own rupture—

we were so sure that theory was the sky

and everything beneath it merely ground.

But here the ground is actual, and poured

by hands I supervise, and the sound

of concrete settling is its own accord

of meaning. I know now what the character means:

you stand inside the danger. Then you begin.
