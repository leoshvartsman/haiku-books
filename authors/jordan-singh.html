Before the city learned to name its God

I learned it stone by stone, the way a child

will press his palm to limestone, feel the broad

warm pulse of something older, undefiled

by doctrine. Dawn came pink above the Wall,

the muezzin threading through the cypress air,

and I stood small beneath the ritual call,

not knowing whose voice owned the morning there.

The olive trees along the Kidron kept

their ancient, arthritic lean toward light—

they had outlasted every prayer wept

into that valley, every sacred fight.

I only knew the stone was warm. I knew

the warmth was real. I didn't know whose.

I held the exposed film up to the glass,

that first reel, barely knowing what I'd shot,

and watched the light come through like broken mass,

the negative of everything I sought.

A yellow cab, a woman mid-step, rain

arrested into silver on her coat—

these were the images my eye could claim,

the city caught inside my throat.

In the Old City once I'd seen light fall

through lattice onto stone in just this way,

but that light carried God, or seemed to call

His name in shadow. This was different: day

stripped to its bones, profane and absolute—

the world itself, without the need to compute.

Inside the wooden church the candles make

a mathematics out of dark and warm,

each flame a small decision not to break

against the nothing pressing at the storm.

The pews are plain as sentences that work,

no gilded column, no mosaic saint,

just spruce and pine and one small painted clerk

above the altar, modest in restraint.

I have prayed in three languages before

and found that God is somewhere in the gap

between the words, the hinge inside the door

of meaning — Arabic, the click and wrap

of Hebrew, English flat as winter ice —

and now Norwegian, tender and precise.
