Selected Sonnets

Buenos Aires gives me morning wide

and blue above the Rio de la Plata,

a sky without a ceiling, and inside

the cafés, men read Borges and Errata

del paraíso both. The medialunas

come warm. The coffee comes in small

white cups. At five a.m. the laguna

of streetlight holds the empty avenue,

and I sit writing in that borrowed dark

with Rome beneath my pen and Buenos Aires

outside the glass, and Tel Aviv, the mark

of all those markets, and the estuary's

salt breath, and Stanford's fog — all of it

converging here, where I begin to sit.

Before the tarpaulins lift, the pyramids hold still,

turmeric banked like sand against the morning chill,

cumin in its cone, the saffron threads laid out

like something sworn—I learned to price by doubt,

to press a pinch between my fingers, close my eyes,

and read the weight of it the way you read a lie:

with patience, with a nose for what's been cut with dust.

The vendor watches. This is ritual, not trust.

At last the canvas falls. The light comes in like noise.

A hundred colors shout what words cannot. The boys

who slept beneath the stalls are folding up their mats.

I write the number down. He crosses out. And that's

the volta no one teaches you in school—

the price is never final. Neither is the fool.

The river doesn't end so much as blur—

that brown expanse at dawn where sky won't part

from water, where the egrets don't confer

but stand like unpunctuated thought, apart

from any shore I'd name. I came here last

of all the cities, carrying the weight

of markets, libraries, a childhood's cast

of light on stone. The river will not wait

for me to find its edge. Each morning breaks

the same: I rise before the city does,

pour coffee, watch the Plata as it makes

its slow erasure of the things that was.

I've lived in enough dawns to know the cost—

the found place is the one you know you've lost.
