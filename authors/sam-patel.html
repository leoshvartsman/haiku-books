Selected Sonnets

The brown water carries nothing you can see

and everything you know is somewhere in it —

a plastic sandal, someone's history,

the sediment of the Paraná's limit

pushed south and wider into something vast

enough to be mistaken for the sea.

My father called it patient. Said the past

was how the river moved: eventually.

Now I live above the Witwatersrand,

no river near, the rain when it arrives

comes fast and leaves fast, nothing like the land

of gradual accumulation. What survives

of patience here is underground, is gold,

is what they took. The river taught me: hold.

There is a signature I think about—

an elder's name written so slowly down

the page it seemed like she was working out

the terms of something larger than the form.

She'd told me first in French, then switched to Cree,

and I caught none of it but held the thread

of what she meant, let it translate through me

into the boxes, into what was said.

I've sat in four languages and known

that none of them contained the thing required,

that every form is also a kind of stone

you're asked to carry, tired, already tired.

She signed. She thanked me. Drove back on the ice.

I filed it. That was never going to suffice.

The rain comes and it comes without apology,

sheets of it off the Witwatersrand,

and I am standing in it, my anthology

of selves getting wet—the child, the man

with the clipboard, the student with the draft

marked up in red, the one who crossed the river

and kept crossing. Someone up the shaft

of the street is laughing, sheltering under

a shop awning, watching the water run.

I raise my hand. She raises hers. The rain

does what rain does: makes everyone

briefly the same. I have lived this equation

before, in other cities, other weather.

But this is the first time I've stood still to gather.
